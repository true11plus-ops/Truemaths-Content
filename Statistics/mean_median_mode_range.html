1. The Mean (The Average)

🔵 What is the Mean?

The mean is what people usually mean by "the average." You share the total equally across all items.

Mean = Sum of all values ÷ Number of values

Memory jogger: "Mean = Add and Divide"

📝 Worked Example 1 — Basic

Find the mean of: {3, 5, 8, 2, 2}

1Add all values: 3 + 5 + 8 + 2 + 2 = 20
2Count: there are 5 numbers
3Divide: 20 ÷ 5 = 4
✅ Mean = 4
📝 Worked Example 2 — With Decimals

Find the mean of: {4.5, 6, 7.5, 8, 4}

1Sum: 4.5 + 6 + 7.5 + 8 + 4 = 30
2Count: 5 numbers
330 ÷ 5 = 6
✅ Mean = 6
⚠️ Common Mistake If a 0 appears in the set, it must be counted both in the sum (adds nothing) and in the count (still a value!). E.g. {4, 0, 6} has mean = 10 ÷ 3 = 3.33, NOT 10 ÷ 2 = 5.

2. The Median (The Middle)

🟢 What is the Median?

The median is the middle value when numbers are arranged in order from smallest to largest.

  • Odd count: there is one exact middle value
  • Even count: find the mean of the two middle values

Memory jogger: "Median = Middle of the road"

📝 Worked Example 1 — Odd Count

Find the median of: {3, 5, 8, 2, 2}

1Order: 2, 2, 3, 5, 8
25 numbers → middle is position 3
3Position 3 = 3
✅ Median = 3
📝 Worked Example 2 — Even Count

Find the median of: {4, 7, 2, 9, 1, 6}

1Order: 1, 2, 4, 6, 7, 9
26 numbers → two middle values at positions 3 and 4 → 4 and 6
3Mean of 4 and 6: (4 + 6) ÷ 2 = 5
✅ Median = 5
📝 Worked Example 3 — Even Count with Decimals

Find the median of: {3, 7, 1, 8, 5, 4}

1Order: 1, 3, 4, 5, 7, 8
2Two middle values: 4 and 5
3(4 + 5) ÷ 2 = 4.5
✅ Median = 4.5
📝 Worked Example 4 — Larger Set (8 numbers)

Find the median of: {15, 3, 22, 7, 18, 11, 9, 14}

1Order: 3, 7, 9, 11, 14, 15, 18, 22
28 numbers → positions 4 and 5 → 11 and 14
3(11 + 14) ÷ 2 = 12.5
✅ Median = 12.5
🚨 #1 Most Common 11+ Mistake! ALWAYS rewrite the list in order first. Forgetting to order is the single most common median error on GL papers.

3. The Mode (The Most Common)

🟡 What is the Mode?

The mode is the value that appears most often.

  • A set can have no mode (all values appear once)
  • A set can have two or more modes (bimodal/multimodal)
  • The mode is the only average that can be used for non-numerical data (e.g. favourite colour)

Memory jogger: "Mode = Most common"

📝 Worked Example 1 — One Mode

Find the mode of: {2, 2, 3, 5, 8}

1Count each: 2 appears twice, others appear once
✅ Mode = 2
📝 Worked Example 2 — Bimodal (Two Modes)

Find the mode of: {3, 5, 3, 7, 5, 9}

1Count: 3 appears twice, 5 appears twice
✅ Mode = 3 and 5 (bimodal)
📝 Worked Example 3 — No Mode

Find the mode of: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}

1All values appear once — no value is more frequent
✅ No mode

4. The Range (The Spread)

🟣 What is the Range?

The range measures how spread out the data is. It is not an average — it is a measure of spread.

Range = Largest Value − Smallest Value

Memory jogger: "Range = the distance from top to bottom"

📝 Worked Example

Find the range of: {3, 5, 8, 2, 2}

1Largest = 8, Smallest = 2
2Range = 8 − 2 = 6
✅ Range = 6

5. Averages from Frequency Tables

📋 How to Use a Frequency Table

Frequency tables group data. To find averages from them:

  • Mode: the value with the highest frequency
  • Mean: add a "Score × Frequency" column, sum it, then divide by total frequency
  • Median: use cumulative frequency to find the middle position
📝 Worked Example — Full Frequency Table

6. Tally Charts & Pictograms

📊 Tally Charts

A tally chart uses marks to count. Every 5th mark crosses the previous four (five-bar gate). Convert tallies to frequencies first, then find averages normally.

📝 Worked Example — Tally Chart

Students recorded how many books they read last month:

BooksTallyFrequency
1III3
2IIII I6
3IIII5
4II2
1Total students = 3+6+5+2 = 16
2Mode = 2 (highest frequency = 6)
3Mean = (1×3 + 2×6 + 3×5 + 4×2) ÷ 16 = (3+12+15+8) ÷ 16 = 38 ÷ 16 = 2.375
✅ Mode = 2, Mean = 2.375

🎴 Pictograms

Each symbol represents a fixed number (the key). Convert symbols → numbers first, then find averages.

Important: Half symbols = half the key value (e.g. if ⭐ = 4, then ½⭐ = 2)

📝 Worked Example — Pictogram

Books sold each day. Key: 📚 = 10 books

DayPictogramBooks
Mon📚📚📚30
Tue📚📚📚📚📚50
Wed📚📚20
Thu📚📚📚📚40
Fri📚📚📚📚📚📚60

Each 📚 = 10 books

1Values: 30, 50, 20, 40, 60
2Mean = (30+50+20+40+60) ÷ 5 = 200 ÷ 5 = 40

✅ Mean = 40 books, Range = 40 books

7. Averages from Bar & Line Charts

📈 Bar Charts & Line Graphs

Read off the values from the chart, then apply the standard mean, median, mode, or range methods.

  • Read each bar/point value carefully from the scale
  • Write out the values as a list, then proceed normally
  • Watch out for scales that go up in 2s, 5s, or 10s
📝 Worked Example — Bar Chart

A bar chart shows the number of goals scored per match: Mon=2, Tue=5, Wed=3, Thu=5, Fri=1, Sat=4

1Read values: {2, 5, 3, 5, 1, 4}
2Mean = (2+5+3+5+1+4) ÷ 6 = 20 ÷ 6 = 3.33
3Range = 5 − 1 = 4
4Mode = 5 (appears twice)
✅ Mean ≈ 3.3, Range = 4, Mode = 5

8. Pie Charts — Reading & Interpreting

🥧 Reading Pie Charts

A pie chart shows parts of a whole. The full circle = 360° = 100% of the total.

  • If you know the total and a fraction/percentage, you can find actual values
  • If you know one actual value and its fraction, you can find the total
Value = (Angle ÷ 360) × Total
Value = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Total
📝 Worked Example 1 — Finding a Value

60 children were asked their favourite sport. The pie chart shows: Football = 120°, Cricket = 90°, Tennis = 60°, Other = 90°

1Football: (120 ÷ 360) × 60 = ⅓ × 60 = 20 children
2Cricket: (90 ÷ 360) × 60 = ¼ × 60 = 15 children
✅ Football = 20, Cricket = 15, Tennis = 10, Other = 15
📝 Worked Example 2 — Finding the Total

A pie chart shows that 25% of students chose Science. If 15 students chose Science, how many students were surveyed in total?

125% = 15 students
21% = 15 ÷ 25 = 0.6 students
3100% = 0.6 × 100 = 60 students
✅ Total = 60 students

📋 Two-Way Tables

A two-way table sorts data by two different categories simultaneously. Row totals and column totals should always add up to the grand total.

To find averages, first extract the relevant data from the table, then apply your methods.

10. Outliers & The Best Average

🔍 What is an Outlier?

An outlier is a value that is far away from all the others in a data set. It can dramatically pull the mean up or down, making the mean a misleading average.

📝 Worked Example — Outlier Effect

Weekly wages (£): {300, 310, 295, 320, 305, 1500}

1Mean = (300+310+295+320+305+1500) ÷ 6 = 3030 ÷ 6 = £505
2Median (ordered: 295, 300, 305, 310, 320, 1500) = (305+310)÷2 = £307.50
3The mean of £505 is misleading! Most workers earn around £305. The median better represents the group.
✅ The median is the best average here because the outlier (£1500) distorts the mean.
SituationBest AverageWhy
Data has an outlier (very high or low value)MedianNot affected by outliers
Most typical/popular value neededModeShows most common value
Data is evenly spread (no outliers)MeanUses all data equally
Non-numerical data (colours, names)ModeOnly average that works
Comparing two groups fairlyMeanBalances all values

11. Reverse Mean & Missing Values

🔄 The Reverse Mean Method

You are given the mean and count, and must find a missing value.

Required Total = Mean × Count   →   Missing Value = Required Total − Sum of Known Values
📝 Worked Example 1 — Basic Reverse Mean

The mean of five numbers is 8. Four of the numbers are 5, 9, 11, and 7. Find the fifth number.

1Required total = 8 × 5 = 40
2Sum of known = 5+9+11+7 = 32
3Missing = 40 − 32 = 8
✅ Fifth number = 8
📝 Worked Example 2 — Combined Groups

Class A (20 students) has a mean mark of 65. Class B (30 students) has a mean mark of 75. What is the combined mean for both classes?

1Class A total = 65 × 20 = 1300
2Class B total = 75 × 30 = 2250
3Combined total = 1300 + 2250 = 3550
4Combined count = 20 + 30 = 50
5Combined mean = 3550 ÷ 50 = 71
✅ Combined mean = 71 (NOT 70 — you cannot simply average two means!)
⚠️ Critical Trap! You cannot find a combined mean by averaging two means unless the group sizes are equal. Always multiply mean × count first, then sum totals, then divide by total count.

📋 Quick Reference Cheat Sheet

🔵 Mean

Add all values ÷ count

Include zeroes in both sum and count

💡 "Add and Divide"

🟢 Median

Order first! Middle value. Even count → mean of two middle values

💡 "Middle of the Road"

🟡 Mode

Most frequent. Can be 0, 1, or 2+ modes. Only average for non-numbers.

💡 "Most Common"

🟣 Range

Largest − Smallest. Measures spread, NOT an average.

💡 "The Distance"

📋 Freq. Table

Mean = Σ(score×freq) ÷ Σfreq

Mode = highest frequency

🔄 Reverse Mean

Total = Mean × Count

Missing = Total − Known Sum

🥧 Pie Chart

Value = (Angle÷360) × Total

Value = (% ÷ 100) × Total

🔍 Outliers

Outlier present → use Median

Most common → use Mode

No outliers → use Mean

👥 Combined Mean

Get each group's total, sum them, divide by combined count

⚠️ Never just average two means!
