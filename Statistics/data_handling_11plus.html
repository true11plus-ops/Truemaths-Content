1Scale Reading — The Foundation Skill

Before reading any value from any chart, always identify the scale. The most common mark-losing mistake in data handling is misreading the scale and being off by one or two gridlines.

📐 The Golden Formula
Value per small division = (top value − bottom value) ÷ number of divisions
Then: bar value = nearest labelled line + (divisions above it × value per division)
✏️ Scenario a — Bar lands exactly on a gridline
Scale: 0 to 50, with 5 gridlines above 0. Each division = 50 ÷ 5 = 10 units.
Bar top lines up exactly with the 30 mark.
✅ Value = 30. Straightforward read — no estimation needed.
✏️ Scenario b — Bar lands halfway between 30 and 40
Scale goes 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50. Each main division = 10 units.
Bar top is exactly halfway between the 30 and 40 lines.
Halfway between 30 and 40: (30 + 40) ÷ 2 = 35
✅ Value = 35
✏️ Scenario c — Each small division = 4 units
Scale labels: 0, 20, 40. There are 5 small divisions between 0 and 20.
Value per small division = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 units.
Bar is 3 small divisions above the 20 line.
Value = 20 + (3 × 4) = 20 + 12 = 32
✅ Value = 32
Scale reading illustration — each small division = 4 units
⚠️ Never assume each division = 1 or 10

Always count the number of divisions between two labelled values. If labels show 0 and 50 with 5 gaps between them, each division = 10. If labels show 0 and 50 with 10 gaps, each division = 5. Count first, every time.

2Bar Charts — Single and Grouped

Bar charts represent quantities using the height of rectangular bars. Single bar charts show one data set; grouped (dual) bar charts show two sets side by side.

Favourite Subjects Survey — 30 students (scale: each division = 5 students)
✏️ GL Question Type A — Difference
Q: How many more students chose Maths than Science?
Step 1: Read Maths bar → top aligns with 25 line → value = 25
Step 2: Read Science bar → top aligns with 15 line → value = 15
Step 3: Difference = 25 − 15 = 10
✅ 10 more students chose Maths than Science.
✏️ GL Question Type B — Total
Q: How many students were surveyed in total?
Step 1: Read all bars: Maths = 25, Science = 15, English = 20, History = 10, Art = 12
Step 2: Add: 25 + 15 + 20 + 10 + 12 = 82
✅ 82 students were surveyed in total.
✏️ GL Question Type C — Mean from a bar chart
Q: What is the mean number of students per subject?
Step 1: List values: 25, 15, 20, 10, 12
Step 2: Sum = 25 + 15 + 20 + 10 + 12 = 82
Step 3: Number of bars = 5
Step 4: Mean = 82 ÷ 5 = 16.4
✅ Mean = 16.4 students per subject

Grouped (Dual) Bar Charts — Two bars per group, one for each data set. Always read the key to identify which colour/shade represents which group.

Sports Participation — Boys (blue) vs Girls (pink) — scale: each division = 4 students
⚠️ Grouped Chart Trap

On narrow grouped bars, the scale can be hard to read. Always check: which bar is which colour? If you swap Boys and Girls, every answer is wrong. Read the key first, mark which bar is which before answering.

3Pictograms

Pictograms use repeated symbols to represent data. The key tells you how many items one complete symbol represents. Always establish the key value before counting.

📐 Partial Symbol Values — Key = 8 people per symbol
Full symbol = 8
Half symbol (½) = 8 ÷ 2 = 4
Quarter symbol (¼) = 8 ÷ 4 = 2
Three-quarter symbol (¾) = 8 × ¾ = 6
Books Read Last Month — Key: 🔵 = 4 books
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Reading the total
Q: How many books did all five students read in total?
Key: 1 symbol = 4 books. ½ symbol = 2 books. ¾ symbol = 3 books.
Alice: 4 full = 4×4 = 16
Ben: 2 full + ½ = 8 + 2 = 10
Clara: 3 full + ½ = 12 + 2 = 14
Dan: 1 full + ¾ = 4 + 3 = 7
Eve: 1 full + ½ = 4 + 2 = 6
Total: 16 + 10 + 14 + 7 + 6 = 53
✅ 53 books in total
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Difference with partial symbols
Q: How many more books did Alice read than Dan?
Alice = 16, Dan = 7
Difference = 16 − 7 = 9
✅ Alice read 9 more books than Dan.
📋 Completing a Pictogram — How Many Symbols to Draw?

Q: Key = 8 people per symbol. Add a row for a group with 20 people. How many symbols are needed?

20 ÷ 8 = 2.5 → draw 2 whole symbols and 1 half symbol.

If asked for 6 people: 6 ÷ 8 = ¾ → draw ¾ of a symbol.

⚠️ Never count symbols without applying the key

If there are 3 symbols and you write "3" as the answer, you have forgotten the key. Three symbols with a key of 8 = 24 people. The key multiplier applies every time.

4Line Graphs

Line graphs show how a value changes over time (continuous data). Points are plotted and joined with straight lines. Use a ruler to trace horizontally from a point to the y-axis to read its value accurately.

Monthly Rainfall (mm) — Jan to Jun
📐 Key Skills for Line Graphs
Reading a value: trace from the point horizontally to the y-axis
Interpolation: estimate a value between two plotted points
Trend: rising line = increasing, falling line = decreasing, flat = no change
Steepest section = greatest rate of change
✏️ Worked Example — Interpolation
Q: Estimate the rainfall halfway between March and April.
March = 55 mm, April = 35 mm
Halfway = (55 + 35) ÷ 2 = 45 mm
✅ Approximately 45 mm halfway between March and April

5Pie Charts

A pie chart shows parts of a whole. The full circle = 360°. Each sector's angle is proportional to its fraction of the total.

📐 Key Formulas
Sector angle = (value ÷ total) × 360°
Value from angle = (angle ÷ 360) × total
Fraction = angle ÷ 360°
How 60 students travel to school — total = 60 students
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Find the number who walk
Q: Walk sector = 120°. Total = 60 students. How many walk?
Fraction = 120 ÷ 360 = ⅓
Number = ⅓ × 60 = 20
✅ 20 students walk to school.
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Calculate a sector angle
Q: 15 students travel by bus. Total = 60. What angle should the Bus sector be?
Fraction = 15 ÷ 60 = ¼
Angle = ¼ × 360 = 90°
✅ Bus sector = 90°
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Find a value from a fraction
Q: The Cycle sector is 60°. Total = 60 students. How many cycle?
Fraction = 60 ÷ 360 = 1/6
Number = 1/6 × 60 = 10
✅ 10 students cycle.

6Two-Way Tables & Frequency Tables

Two-way tables organise data across two variables simultaneously. They are common in GL papers and require careful reading of rows, columns, and totals.

Favourite Season — Survey of 80 students
SpringSummerAutumnWinterRow Total
Boys81861244
Girls10148436
Column Total1832141680
📐 Reading Rules
Row total = sum across that row. Column total = sum down that column.
Grand total (bottom-right) = sum of all row totals = sum of all column totals.
To find a missing value: total − all other known values in same row/column.
✏️ Worked Example — Finding a missing value
Q: If "Boys who prefer Autumn" was missing, how would you find it?
Method 1 (use row): Boys total = 44. 44 − 8 − 18 − 12 = 6
Method 2 (use column): Autumn total = 14. 14 − 8 (Girls) = 6
✅ Both methods give 6 — always cross-check using both row and column!

7Mean, Median, Mode & Range

These four statistics describe a data set. All four appear in GL papers — often calculated from chart data.

📐 Definitions and Formulas
Mean = sum of all values ÷ number of values
Median = middle value when data is sorted smallest to largest
Mode = most frequently occurring value (can be more than one)
Range = largest value − smallest value
Missing value given mean: missing = (mean × n) − sum of known values
✏️ Worked Example — All four statistics
Data set: 8, 3, 5, 8, 6, 4, 8, 2
Mean: Sum = 8+3+5+8+6+4+8+2 = 44. Count = 8. Mean = 44 ÷ 8 = 5.5
Median: Sort: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 8, 8. Middle two = 5 and 6. Median = (5+6) ÷ 2 = 5.5
Mode: 8 appears 3 times — most frequent. Mode = 8
Range: 8 − 2 = 6
✅ Mean=5.5, Median=5.5, Mode=8, Range=6
✏️ Worked Example — Finding a missing value from the mean
Q: Five numbers have a mean of 9. Four of them are: 7, 11, 8, 10. Find the fifth.
Step 1: Total needed = mean × count = 9 × 5 = 45
Step 2: Sum of known values = 7 + 11 + 8 + 10 = 36
Step 3: Missing value = 45 − 36 = 9
✅ The fifth number is 9.

8Common Pitfalls — Consolidated
  • ⚠️
    Assuming each scale division = 1 or 10: Always count divisions and calculate the value per division before reading any bar. Never guess the scale.
  • ⚠️
    Pictogram — forgetting to apply the key: Counting symbols and writing that number as the answer gives you the symbol count, not the data value. Always multiply by the key value.
  • ⚠️
    Pie chart — adding 12 instead of calculating the fraction: The angle fraction of 360° gives the data fraction. (angle ÷ 360) × total = value. Don't skip the fraction step.
  • ⚠️
    Mean vs Total: The question asks for the mean — students compute the total and stop. Always divide by the count of values after summing.
  • ⚠️
    Median without sorting: The median is the middle value of a sorted list. Finding the middle value of an unsorted list gives a random number, not the median.
  • ⚠️
    Two-way table — reading the wrong cell: "Boys who prefer Summer" is a specific cell. "Total who prefer Summer" is the column total. Read the correct row and column intersection.
  • ⚠️
    Grouped bar chart — swapping the two bars: Read the chart key first and label which bar is which before answering. Mixing up "Boys" and "Girls" bars makes every answer wrong.
