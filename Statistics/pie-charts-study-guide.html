1 · The Basics — What Is a Pie Chart?

A pie chart shows data as slices of a circle. The whole circle always represents the total.


  • In angles: the total is always 360°
  • In percentages: the total is always 100%
  • In fractions: the total is always 1 (one whole)
⭐

Key rule: Bigger slices = more of the total. Smaller slices = less of the total. The size of a slice tells you what proportion it is — not the actual number — unless you know the total.

2 · The Three Formulas

There is one relationship linking Angle, Value, and Total. It can be rearranged into three forms depending on what you need to find.

Find Value Value = (Angle ÷ 360) × Total
Find Angle Angle = (Value ÷ Total) × 360
Find Total Total = Value × (360 ÷ Angle)
Worked Example A — Find the Value
A pie chart shows the favourite colours of 60 students. The "Blue" sector has an angle of 120°. How many students chose blue?
1
Write down what you know: Angle = 120°, Total = 60
2
Use the formula: Value = (Angle ÷ 360) × Total
3
Calculate: (120 ÷ 360) × 60 = ⅓ × 60
4
Answer = 20 students ✓
Worked Example B — Find the Angle
In a survey of 80 people, 20 chose "tennis". What angle should the Tennis sector have?
1
Write down what you know: Value = 20, Total = 80
2
Use the formula: Angle = (Value ÷ Total) × 360
3
Calculate: (20 ÷ 80) × 360 = 0.25 × 360
4
Answer = 90° ✓
Worked Example C — Find the Total
The "Sport" sector has an angle of 72° and represents 18 students. How many students are there in total?
1
Write down what you know: Angle = 72°, Value = 18
2
Use the formula: Total = Value × (360 ÷ Angle)
3
Calculate: 18 × (360 ÷ 72) = 18 × 5
4
Answer = 90 students ✓

4 · Percentage-Labelled Pie Charts

Some pie charts show percentages on each slice instead of angles. You need to be able to:

  • Convert a percentage → angle: multiply percentage by 3.6
  • Convert a percentage → frequency: multiply percentage by total ÷ 100
💡

Why 3.6? Because 360° ÷ 100% = 3.6° per 1%. So every 1% of the pie = 3.6°.

Worked Example D — Percentage → Angle
A "Science" sector is labelled 25%. What is the angle of this sector?
1
Use: Angle = Percentage × 3.6
2
Calculate: 25 × 3.6 = 90° ✓
Worked Example E — Percentage → Frequency
A pie chart shows exam results for 200 students. The "Pass" sector is labelled 35%. How many students passed?
1
Use: Frequency = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Total
2
Calculate: (35 ÷ 100) × 200 = 0.35 × 200
3
Answer = 70 students ✓

5 · Reading, Ranking & Most/Least Popular

  • Most popular = the largest sector (biggest angle / biggest % / most people)
  • Least popular = the smallest sector
  • To rank: order slices from largest to smallest angle
  • To find how many more: subtract the two values
⚡

Speed tip: You don't always need to calculate — if one angle is clearly larger visually (or as a number), it's the most popular. Save time by spotting this immediately.

6 · Writing Sectors as Fractions in Simplest Form

The question will say: "Write the fraction of students who chose X. Give your answer in its simplest form."


Steps:

  1. Find the sector angle
  2. Write angle / 360
  3. Simplify the fraction by dividing top and bottom by their HCF (highest common factor)
Worked Example F — Simplest Fraction from an Angle
The "Art" sector has an angle of 120°. Write this as a fraction in its simplest form.
1
Write as a fraction: 120/360
2
Find HCF of 120 and 360 → HCF = 120
3
Divide both: 120÷120 = 1, 360÷120 = 3
4
Answer = 1/3 ✓
Worked Example G — Simplest Fraction from a Percentage
The "Music" sector is labelled 40%. Write this as a fraction in its simplest form.
1
Write as fraction: 40/100
2
HCF of 40 and 100 = 20
3
Divide both: 40÷20 = 2, 100÷20 = 5
4
Answer = 2/5 ✓

7 · The Two-Chart Comparison Trap

🚨

The Trap

Two pie charts look similar in size on the page, but they may represent completely different totals. A large slice from a small total can mean fewer people than a small slice from a large total!

Worked Example H — Two-Chart Trap
Chart A (total 40 students) has a "Red" sector of 180°. Chart B (total 200 students) has a "Red" sector of 90°. Which chart has more students choosing Red?
1
Chart A: (180÷360)×40 = ½×40 = 20 students
2
Chart B: (90÷360)×200 = ¼×200 = 50 students
3
Chart B has more Red students (50 vs 20), even though its slice looks smaller!
✅

The fix: ALWAYS check the total for each chart before comparing actual numbers. Never assume the bigger-looking slice means more people.

8 · Exam Habit — Check Your Angles Sum to 360°

After you calculate any angles, always add them all up. They must total exactly 360°. If they don't, you've made an error somewhere.


Also use this to find a missing angle: missing = 360 − (sum of known angles).

Worked Example I — Use the Check to Find a Missing Angle
A frequency table gives: Football 80°, Tennis 120°, Swimming 95°, Hockey ???°. Find the Hockey angle.
1
Add known angles: 80 + 120 + 95 = 295°
2
Subtract from 360: 360 − 295 = 65° ✓
