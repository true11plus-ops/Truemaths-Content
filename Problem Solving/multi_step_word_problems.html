1. The RUCSAC Strategy

Every multi-step problem can be solved with this six-step framework. Never skip straight to calculating — reading and understanding first will save you from expensive errors.

LetterStepWhat to do
R — ReadRead twiceRead the whole question. Underline the final question sentence — this tells you exactly what your answer must be.
U — UnderstandFind knowns & unknownsCircle every number given. Write "?" next to what you need to find. Watch for hidden information (e.g. "he had £20 left" is also a number).
C — ChoosePick your operationsDecide which operations (+, −, ×, ÷) you need. Multi-step problems always need at least two. Identify the correct order.
S — SolveCalculate step by stepLabel each sub-answer ("Step 1: Total cost = ..."). Do not combine steps in your head.
A — AnswerAnswer the right questionCheck your final line answers what was underlined in step R. (Did they ask for change, not cost?)
C — CheckEstimate to verifyRound your numbers, re-calculate roughly, confirm your answer is in the right ballpark.
RUCSAC Fully Demonstrated
Lena buys 4 notebooks at £1.85 each and 3 pens at £0.75 each. She pays with a £10 note. How much change does she receive?
R
Read: Underline "How much change does she receive?" — the answer must be in £, and it's about change, not total cost.
U
Understand: Knowns: 4 notebooks @ £1.85, 3 pens @ £0.75, pays with £10. Unknown: Change returned.
C
Choose: Step 1 needs × (cost of notebooks). Step 2 needs × (cost of pens). Step 3 needs + (total). Step 4 needs − (change).
S
Solve:
Step 1: 4 × £1.85 = £7.40
Step 2: 3 × £0.75 = £2.25
Step 3: £7.40 + £2.25 = £9.65
Step 4: £10.00 − £9.65 = £0.35
A
Answer: The question asked for change. Our final step gives change. ✓ Change = £0.35
C
Check (estimate): Notebooks ≈ 4 × £2 = £8. Pens ≈ 3 × £1 = £3. Total ≈ £11. But she only paid £10 — so our estimate suggests about £0–£1 change. Our answer of £0.35 is in that zone. ✓

Framework A

Total Cost & Change

The scenario: You buy several items at different prices and pay with a note or card. The question asks for the change, the total, or how many of something you can afford.
Step 1: Cost of each item type = Quantity × Price per item
Step 2: Subtotal = add all item costs
Step 3: Change = Amount paid − Subtotal Watch out: the question may ask for change, not total cost.
Worked Example A
Mia buys 3 sandwiches at £2.40 each, 2 juices at £1.15 each, and a bag of crisps for £0.85. She pays with a £20 note. How much change does she get?
1
Sandwiches: 3 × £2.40 = £7.20
2
Juices: 2 × £1.15 = £2.30
3
Crisps: 1 × £0.85 = £0.85
4
Subtotal: £7.20 + £2.30 + £0.85 = £10.35
5
Change: £20.00 − £10.35 = £9.65
Change = £9.65
Easy
Raj buys 5 apples at 30p each and pays with £2. How much change does he receive? (Give answer in pence)
A magazine costs £2.50 and a newspaper costs £1.20. Tom buys one of each and pays with a £5 note. What is his change?
Intermediate
A school trip costs £6.50 per child. There are 32 children. The school pays with a cheque for £220. How much change does the school receive?
Emma buys 4 books at £3.75 each and 2 pens at £1.40 each. She also gets a 50p discount coupon. She pays with £20. How much change does Emma get?
Advanced
Three friends share a restaurant bill equally. They ordered: 2 pizzas at £8.99 each, 3 pasta dishes at £7.50 each, and 4 drinks at £2.25 each. How much does each friend pay? (Round to nearest penny)
Framework B

Reverse Mean

The scenario: You know the mean of a set of numbers, but one value is missing. Or you know a current mean and need to find what score will raise it to a new target.
Key identity: Total = Mean × Count

Step 1: Current Total = Current Mean × Current Count
Step 2: Target Total = Target Mean × New Count
Step 3: Missing value = Target Total − Current Total
Worked Example B
After 4 tests, Priya's mean score is 73. What score must she get on her 5th test to raise her mean to 75?
1
Current total: 73 × 4 = 292
2
Target total (5 tests at mean 75): 75 × 5 = 375
3
Required score: 375 − 292 = 83
Priya needs 83 on her 5th test.
Easy
The mean of four numbers is 9. Three of them are 7, 10, and 11. What is the fourth number?
The mean of 5, 8, 12, and x is 9. Find x.
Intermediate
After 3 matches, a football team's mean goals per game is 2. How many goals must they score in their 4th match so the mean rises to 3?
Six students' mean height is 148 cm. A seventh student joins and the new mean is 149 cm. How tall is the seventh student?
Advanced
The mean of eight numbers is 14. When one number is removed, the mean of the remaining seven is 13. What was the number removed?
Challenging
A class of 20 students has a mean test mark of 64. A second class of 30 students has a mean of 72. What is the overall mean mark for all 50 students? (Give to 1 decimal place)
Framework C

Shared Ratio

The scenario: A total amount is split between people or groups in a given ratio. Then one or both parties do something with their share (spend, save, give away). You need to find a final balance.
Step 1: Total parts = add ratio numbers (e.g. 3:2 → 5 parts)
Step 2: Value of 1 part = Total ÷ Total parts
Step 3: Each share = parts × value of 1 part
Step 4: Apply what they spend/save to get final amount
Worked Example C
£240 is shared between Ali and Beth in the ratio 3:5. Ali spends £30 of his share. How much does Ali have left?
1
Total parts: 3 + 5 = 8 parts
2
Value of 1 part: £240 ÷ 8 = £30
3
Ali's share (3 parts): 3 × £30 = £90
4
After spending: £90 − £30 = £60
Ali has £60 left.
Easy
£120 is shared in the ratio 1:3. What is the larger share?
£350 is shared between Sam and Jo in the ratio 2:3. How much does Jo receive?
Intermediate
£180 is shared between three friends in the ratio 2:3:4. The friend with the largest share spends half of it. How much does that friend have left?
A prize of £500 is shared between winner and runner-up in the ratio 7:3. The runner-up donates £25 to charity. How much does the runner-up keep?
Advanced
Red and blue paint are mixed in ratio 3:7 to make 5 litres of purple paint. Red paint costs £4 per litre and blue costs £2.50 per litre. What is the total cost of paint used?
Framework D

Time & Elapsed Time

The scenario: A question involves a start time and end time, a journey with multiple legs, a timetable, or converting between 12-hour (am/pm) and 24-hour clock. You must find duration, arrival time, or departure time.
12-hour → 24-hour: pm times: add 12 (except 12pm = 12:00). am times: keep as-is (except 12am = 00:00)
Elapsed time: End time − Start time (use number line for times crossing the hour)
Journey total: add each leg's duration, then add to start time
Worked Example D
A train leaves at 09:47 and arrives at 12:23. It stops for 18 minutes at an intermediate station. How long is the train actually moving?
1
Total journey time: 12:23 − 09:47
From 09:47 → 10:00 = 13 min
From 10:00 → 12:00 = 120 min
From 12:00 → 12:23 = 23 min
13 + 120 + 23 = 156 minutes
2
Subtract stop time: 156 − 18 = 138 minutes
3
Convert: 138 min = 2 hours 18 minutes
The train is moving for 2 hours 18 minutes.
Tip — Number line method: For awkward times like 09:47, count up in chunks: to the next hour, then full hours, then remaining minutes. Never subtract time like ordinary numbers.
Easy
A film starts at 2:15 pm and lasts 1 hour 45 minutes. What time does it end? (Give in 24-hour clock, e.g. 16:00)
Convert 7:30 pm to 24-hour clock.
Intermediate
A bus departs at 08:35. It travels for 42 minutes, waits 8 minutes at a stop, then travels a further 25 minutes. What time does it arrive? (24-hour clock)
A train arrives at 14:12. The journey took 2 hours and 38 minutes. What time did it depart? (24-hour clock)
Advanced
A runner starts at 10:52 am. She completes 3 laps. Lap 1: 7 min 45 sec. Lap 2: 8 min 10 sec. Lap 3: 7 min 30 sec. What time does she finish? (Give as HH:MM:SS in 24-hour clock)
Framework E

Unit Conversion Chain

The scenario: A problem gives measurements in mixed units (e.g. a fence length in metres but planks measured in centimetres). You must convert first, then calculate.
Step 1: Identify ALL units used in the problem
Step 2: Convert everything to ONE unit before doing anything else
Step 3: Calculate as normal
Step 4: Convert final answer to the unit requested
Worked Example E
A fence is 7.5 m long. Each plank is 25 cm wide. How many planks are needed to build the fence?
1
Convert fence length to cm: 7.5 m = 7.5 × 100 = 750 cm
2
Number of planks: 750 ÷ 25 = 30
30 planks are needed.
Worked Example E2 — Mass conversion
A recipe uses 1.2 kg of flour. A bag holds 750 g. How many bags are needed? Will any flour be left over?
1
Convert 1.2 kg to grams: 1.2 × 1000 = 1200 g
2
Bags: 1200 ÷ 750 = 1.6 → need 2 bags (must round up — can't buy 0.6 of a bag)
3
Flour left over: 2 × 750 g = 1500 g used. 1500 − 1200 = 300 g remaining
2 bags needed; 300 g left over.
Easy
A ribbon is 3.5 m long. It is cut into pieces each 50 cm long. How many pieces can be cut?
A jug holds 2.5 litres of juice. How many 250 ml glasses can be filled?
Intermediate
A path is 4.8 m long and 1.2 m wide. Square paving stones measure 40 cm × 40 cm. How many stones are needed to cover the path exactly?
Tom runs 2.4 km on Monday and 1850 m on Tuesday. What is the total distance in km?
Advanced
A swimming pool is 25 m long and holds 450,000 litres. If the pool is 2 m deep and 10 m wide, and 1 m³ = 1000 litres, what is the volume in m³? Does this match the capacity given?
Framework F

Percentage & Fraction Chains

The scenario: A price changes by a percentage more than once (e.g. a sale price is then reduced further). Or a fraction of a remaining amount must be found. Each step uses the result of the previous step, not the original value.
Percentage of an amount: Amount × (percentage ÷ 100)
After a reduction: New value = Original × (1 − rate/100)
After an increase: New value = Original × (1 + rate/100)
Successive changes: ALWAYS apply to the running total, not the starting value.
Worked Example F — Successive Percentage
A jacket costs £80. It is reduced by 20% in a sale. The next week, the sale price is reduced by a further 10%. What is the final price?
1
First reduction (20%): £80 × 0.80 = £64
2
Second reduction (10% of £64, not £80): £64 × 0.90 = £57.60
Final price = £57.60
Common error: Adding both percentages (20% + 10% = 30%) and taking 30% off £80 gives £56 — this is WRONG because the second 10% is off the reduced price, not the original.
Worked Example F2 — Fraction of a Remainder
Zara has 60 sweets. She gives ¼ to her sister. Then she gives ⅓ of what remains to her brother. How many sweets does Zara have left?
1
Gives to sister: ¼ × 60 = 15 sweets. Remaining: 60 − 15 = 45
2
Gives to brother (⅓ of 45): 45 ÷ 3 = 15 sweets. Remaining: 45 − 15 = 30
Zara has 30 sweets left.
Easy
A shirt costs £40. It is reduced by 15% in a sale. What is the sale price?
There are 120 pupils. ⅓ go on a trip. How many stay behind?
Intermediate
A laptop costs £600. VAT of 20% is added, then a loyalty discount of 5% is removed. What is the final price?
Leo has 200 cards. He gives ¼ to a friend. Of the rest, he sells ⅖. How many does he have left?
Advanced
A TV costs £900. It is reduced by 30%, then the reduced price is increased by 10% due to an error. What is the final price? Is the final price higher or lower than the original? Type the final price only (£)
Challenging
A shop increases all prices by 25%, then offers a 20% discount on all items. A customer claims "the item is back to its original price." Are they correct? What is the final price of an item originally costing £200? Type the final price
Framework G

Perimeter, Area & Cost

The scenario: A geometry problem (shape dimensions) leads to a real-world cost. Key step: when calculating "how many bags/tiles/rolls", always round UP because you cannot buy a fraction of a bag.
Rectangle: Area = Length × Width  |  Perimeter = 2(L + W)
Triangle: Area = ½ × base × height
After area: Bags needed = Area ÷ Coverage per bag → ROUND UP
Cost = Bags (rounded up) × Price per bag
Worked Example G
A rectangular lawn is 14 m long and 9 m wide. A bag of fertiliser covers 15 m² and costs £3.50. How much will it cost to fertilise the entire lawn?
1
Area: 14 × 9 = 126 m²
2
Bags needed: 126 ÷ 15 = 8.4 → round UP to 9 bags
3
Cost: 9 × £3.50 = £31.50
Cost = £31.50
Do NOT use 8.4 bags — you must buy whole bags. Round up regardless of how small the decimal is.
Easy
A room is 5 m long and 4 m wide. Carpet costs £12 per m². What is the total cost?
A square garden has side length 8 m. What is its perimeter?
Intermediate
A path is 12 m long and 1.5 m wide. Square tiles are 50 cm × 50 cm and cost £2.80 each. What is the total cost of tiles? (Convert units first)
A triangular flowerbed has base 8 m and height 5 m. Mulch costs £4.50 per m². What does it cost to cover the flowerbed?
Advanced
A rectangular garden is 11 m × 7 m. A path 1 m wide runs all the way round the inside edge. Grass seed covers 8 m² per bag and costs £4.20 per bag. What is the cost to seed the inner lawn only?
Framework H

Profit, Loss & Discount

The scenario: An item has an original cost (paid by the seller) and a selling price (paid by the buyer). Profit = Selling Price − Cost Price. A discount reduces the selling price. You may need to find the profit after discount.
Profit = Selling Price − Cost Price (if positive = profit; if negative = loss)
Discount amount = Original price × (discount % ÷ 100)
Sale price = Original price − Discount amount
Profit % = (Profit ÷ Cost Price) × 100
Worked Example H
A trader buys a vase for £24 and sells it for £45. He later reduces his selling price by 20%. What is his profit after the reduction?
1
Discounted selling price: £45 × 0.80 = £36
2
Profit: £36 − £24 = £12
Profit after discount = £12
Easy
A book costs £5 to produce and sells for £8. What is the profit?
A toy costs £12 in a shop. It is reduced by 25% in a sale. What is the sale price?
Intermediate
A market trader buys 50 mugs at £2 each. He sells 40 of them at £4.50 each and throws away the rest. What is his total profit?
A phone's original price is £360. A shop offers 15% discount. The phone cost the shop £280. What is the shop's profit after the discount?
Advanced
A farmer buys 200 kg of apples at 60p per kg. He sells ¾ of them at £1.20 per kg and the rest at 40p per kg (reduced for bruised ones). Calculate his overall profit or loss in £.

The "Final Answer Trap"

GL Assessment problems are deliberately designed so that an intermediate result looks like the final answer. Always check: have I answered what was actually asked?

Trap Example 1 — Area vs Cost
A rectangular garden is 10 m by 6 m. Grass seed covers 12 m² per bag and costs £4.99 per bag. How much does it cost to seed the whole garden?
Student calculates: 10 × 6 = 60 m²… and writes 60 m² as their answer.
Wrong — the question asked for the cost, not the area.
Correct continuation:
Step 2: 60 ÷ 12 = 5 bags
Step 3: 5 × £4.99 = £24.95 ← this is the actual answer
Trap Example 2 — Total vs Change
Ben buys 3 books at £4.50 each and pays with £20. How much change does he receive?
Student calculates: 3 × £4.50 = £13.50… and writes £13.50 as their answer.
Wrong — the question asked for change, not the total cost.
Correct continuation:
Step 2: £20.00 − £13.50 = £6.50 ← the actual answer
🚨 Prevention rule: After every calculation, look back at the underlined question. Ask: "Does my current number directly answer this sentence?" If not — keep going.

Estimation & Checking

After solving, round the numbers and re-calculate quickly. If your estimate and your exact answer are far apart, you have likely made an error.

Estimation Example 1

Problem: 4 notebooks at £1.85 + 3 pens at £0.75 = ? Change from £10

E
Round: 4 × £2 = £8. 3 × £1 = £3. Total ≈ £11. Change from £10 ≈ −£1 (not possible — so total must be under £10). Exact answer £0.35 — plausible. ✓
Estimation Example 2

Problem: A lawn 14 m × 9 m. Bags cover 15 m². Bags needed?

E
Round: 15 × 10 = 150 m². ÷ 15 ≈ 10 bags. Exact answer was 9 bags. In same ballpark. ✓
Tip: In MCQ, use estimation to eliminate 2–3 options immediately. You rarely need to calculate all options precisely.

MCQ Strategy for GL Assessment

GL papers are multiple choice. This changes your approach in three key ways.

1. Eliminate First

Before calculating, look at the answer options. If the question asks for a cost in pounds, eliminate any answer that is negative or clearly too large/small. Often you can go from 5 options to 2 before doing any arithmetic.

2. Back-Substitute

If you are unsure, try plugging each remaining answer back into the question. E.g. "If the answer is £6.50, does £20 − £6.50 = £13.50, and is 3 × £4.50 = £13.50? Yes — so £6.50 is correct." This avoids solving the whole problem from scratch.

3. Check Units

Confirm the answer option is in the same unit as the question. If the question asks "How many metres?", an option like 350 cm is a distractor — it may be the right number but the wrong unit.

4. Time Management

50 questions in 50 minutes = 1 minute per question. Multi-step problems may need 90 seconds. Skip and return — but always make a guess before moving on, since there is no penalty for wrong answers in GL papers.
