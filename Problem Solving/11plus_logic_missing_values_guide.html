Inverse Operations & Function Machines

Core Idea: A function machine performs operations on a number in order. To find a missing input, you reverse every step in reverse order.
Golden Rules: + ↔ −  |  × ↔ ÷  |  x² ↔ √x  |  Fraction × ↔ Divide by fraction
Key Trick for Fractions: "¾ of □ = 27" means ¾ × □ = 27. To undo "multiply by ¾", divide by ¾ (same as multiplying by 4/3).
So □ = 27 ÷ ¾ = 27 × 4/3 = 36
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Multi-step Function Machine

Question: I think of a number. I multiply by 4, subtract 6, then divide by 3. The result is 10. What was my number?

1
Start at the end result: 10
2
Reverse "÷3" → multiply by 3: 10 × 3 = 30
3
Reverse "−6" → add 6: 30 + 6 = 36
4
Reverse "×4" → divide by 4: 36 ÷ 4 = 9
✅ Answer: My number was 9. Check: 9 × 4 = 36 → 36 − 6 = 30 → 30 ÷ 3 = 10 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Fraction of Unknown

Question: ¾ of a number is 27. What is the number?

1
Write it as an equation: ¾ × □ = 27
2
Find ¼ of the number first: 27 ÷ 3 = 9 (that's ¼)
3
Multiply by 4 to get the whole: 9 × 4 = 36
✅ Answer: The number is 36. Check: ¾ × 36 = 27 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Percentage of Unknown

Question: 40% of □ = 26. Find □.

1
Write: 0.4 × □ = 26
2
Find 1% → 26 ÷ 40 = 0.65
3
Multiply by 100 → 0.65 × 100 = 65
✅ Answer: □ = 65. Check: 40% of 65 = 26 ✓
⭐ Easy

Q2. I think of a number. I multiply by 5 and get 45. What is my number?
Q3. ½ of a number is 14. What is the number?
Q4. I think of a number. I subtract 12 and get 9. What is my number?
⭐⭐ Intermediate

Q7. I double a number, subtract 5, then halve the result. The answer is 7. What was my number?
Q8. ²⁄₅ of a number is 18. What is the number?
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced

Balancing Equations & Forming Expressions from Words

Core Idea: The equals sign is like a balance scale — both sides must be equal. For word problems, first translate the words into maths, then solve. Key words: "twice" = ×2, "altogether" = +, "more than" = +, "less than" = −
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Balance Scale Equation

Question: 14 + □ = 3 × 8. Find □.

1
Solve the known side: 3 × 8 = 24
2
Rewrite: 14 + □ = 24
3
Find □: 24 − 14 = 10
✅ □ = 10
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Word Problem to Equation

Question: Emma has twice as many stickers as Sam. Together they have 54. How many does Sam have?

1
Let Sam's amount = □. Then Emma's = 2 × □
2
Together: □ + 2□ = 54 → 3□ = 54
3
Solve: □ = 54 ÷ 3 = 18
4
Check: Sam = 18, Emma = 36, total = 54 ✓
✅ Sam has 18 stickers, Emma has 36.
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Forming and Solving

Question: A plank is 5 times as long as a bolt. Together they measure 84 cm. How long is the bolt?

1
Let bolt = □ cm. Plank = 5□
2
□ + 5□ = 84 → 6□ = 84
3
□ = 84 ÷ 6 = 14 cm
✅ Bolt = 14 cm, Plank = 70 cm
⭐ Easy
Q1. 18 + □ = 5 × 7. Find □.
Q2. □ × 6 = 4 × 9. Find □.
Q3. 32 − □ = 3 × 8. Find □.
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. Emma has twice as many stickers as Sam. Together they have 54. How many does Sam have?
Q5. A plank is 5 times as long as a bolt. Together they measure 84 cm. How long is the bolt (in cm)?
Q6. Tom is 3 years older than Anya. The sum of their ages is 29. How old is Anya?
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q7. Three friends share £90. Ben gets twice as much as Chloe, and Chloe gets £10 more than Lily. How much does Lily get (in £)?
Q8. A rectangle's length is 4 more than twice its width. The perimeter is 56 cm. Find the width (in cm).
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q9. Four consecutive even numbers sum to 100. What is the smallest of the four numbers?
Q10. 3□ + 12 = 5□ − 8. Find □.

Ratio & Proportion

Two main types:
1. Sharing into a ratio — divide a total amount according to given parts
2. Scaling / Missing value — if 3 items cost £x, what do n items cost?
Method for Sharing: Add the ratio parts → find the value of 1 part → multiply for each share.
Method for Scaling: Find the value of ONE unit, then multiply up.
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Sharing into a Ratio

Question: Share £72 between Alice and Bob in the ratio 3:5.

1
Total parts: 3 + 5 = 8
2
Value of 1 part: £72 ÷ 8 = £9
3
Alice: 3 × £9 = £27
4
Bob: 5 × £9 = £45
✅ Alice gets £27, Bob gets £45. Check: 27 + 45 = 72 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Scaling (Missing Value)

Question: 5 pencils cost 60p. How much do 8 pencils cost?

1
Cost of 1 pencil: 60p ÷ 5 = 12p
2
Cost of 8 pencils: 12p × 8 = 96p
✅ 8 pencils cost 96p
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Ratio with Unknown

Question: Red and blue counters are in the ratio 4:3. There are 28 red counters. How many blue counters are there?

1
4 parts = 28, so 1 part = 28 ÷ 4 = 7
2
Blue = 3 parts = 3 × 7 = 21
✅ There are 21 blue counters
⭐ Easy
Q1. Share 40 sweets between Pia and Raj in ratio 3:5. How many does Pia get?
Q2. 4 apples cost 60p. How much do 7 apples cost (in pence)?
Q3. Red and blue beads are in ratio 2:7. There are 14 red beads. How many blue beads?
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. Share £120 in ratio 1:2:3. What is the largest share (in £)?
Q5. A recipe for 4 people needs 320g of flour. How many grams are needed for 7 people?
Q6. The ratio of boys to girls in a class is 3:4. There are 21 boys. How many students are there altogether?
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q7. Two numbers are in ratio 5:8. Their difference is 12. What is the smaller number?
Q8. A map has scale 1:25000. A road measures 8 cm on the map. How long is the road in km?
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q9. Three containers hold liquid in ratio 2:5:3. The largest contains 60 more litres than the smallest. How many litres does the middle container hold?
Q10. Paint is mixed in the ratio red:blue:yellow = 3:1:2. If you use 450ml of red, how many ml of yellow do you use?

Percentage Missing Values

Three question types:
1. Find the percentage: What % of 80 is 20? → (20 ÷ 80) × 100
2. Find the whole: 20% of □ = 14? → □ = 14 ÷ 0.20
3. Find the part: What is 35% of 60? → 0.35 × 60
The % Formula Triangle: Part = (% ÷ 100) × Whole
→ % = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100    → Whole = Part ÷ (% ÷ 100)
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Find the Percentage

Question: What percentage of 80 is 20?

1
Divide part by whole: 20 ÷ 80 = 0.25
2
Multiply by 100: 0.25 × 100 = 25%
✅ 20 is 25% of 80
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Find the Whole

Question: 20% of □ = 14. Find □.

1
20% = 14, so 1% = 14 ÷ 20 = 0.7
2
100% = 0.7 × 100 = 70
✅ □ = 70. Check: 20% × 70 = 14 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 3 — After a Percentage Change

Question: After a 25% increase, a price is £75. What was the original price?

1
125% = £75 (because original + 25% = 125% of original)
2
1% = 75 ÷ 125 = 0.60
3
100% = 0.60 × 100 = £60
✅ Original price = £60
⭐ Easy
Q1. What percentage of 50 is 10?
Q2. What is 50% of 86?
Q3. 10% of □ = 8. Find □.
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. 20% of □ = 14. Find □.
Q5. What percentage of 120 is 30?
Q6. 35% of 60 = ?
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q7. After a 25% increase, a price is £75. What was the original price (in £)?
Q8. After a 20% decrease, a value is 48. What was the original value?
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging

Substitution into Formulae

Core Idea: A formula is a rule written in letters. You substitute (swap in) the known values, then use inverse operations to find the unknown.
FormulaWhat it means
P = 2l + 2wPerimeter of a rectangle
A = l × wArea of a rectangle
D = S × TDistance = Speed × Time
v = u + atFinal speed (Physics)
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Perimeter Formula

Question: P = 2l + 2w. Find w when P = 30 and l = 8.

1
Substitute: 30 = 2(8) + 2w
2
Simplify: 30 = 16 + 2w
3
Subtract 16: 2w = 14
4
Divide by 2: w = 7
✅ w = 7
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Speed Formula

Question: D = S × T. A car travels 120 km in 1.5 hours. What is the speed?

1
Substitute: 120 = S × 1.5
2
Divide both sides by 1.5: S = 120 ÷ 1.5 = 80
✅ Speed = 80 km/h
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Custom Formula

Question: The formula for the cost of hiring a bike is C = 3h + 5, where h = hours. If the cost is £23, how many hours was the bike hired?

1
Substitute: 23 = 3h + 5
2
Subtract 5: 18 = 3h
3
Divide by 3: h = 6
✅ The bike was hired for 6 hours
⭐ Easy
Q1. Using A = l × w, find w when A = 48 and l = 6.
Q2. Using D = S × T, find T when D = 90 and S = 30.
Q3. C = 3h + 5. Find h when C = 23.
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. P = 2l + 2w. Find w when P = 30 and l = 8.
Q5. v = u + at. Find a when v = 25, u = 5, t = 4.
Q6. A taxi costs £(2.5m + 3) where m = miles. Find m when the fare is £18.
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q7. T = (n−2) × 180. Find n when T = 720.
Q8. y = 3x² − 5. Find x when y = 22. (Give the positive answer.)
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q9. S = n/2 × (a + l). Find a when S = 120, n = 8, l = 25.

Sequences — All Four Types & nth Term

Always find the rule first! Look at what changes between terms. Once you know the rule, you can find any missing term — or the 10th, 50th, or 100th term.
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Linear Sequence

Sequence: 7, 11, 15, 19, □, ...

1
Difference: 11−7 = 4. Rule: add 4 each time
2
Next term: 19 + 4 = 23
3
nth term formula: First term = 7, common difference = 4. nth term = 4n + 3
4
Check: n=1 → 4(1)+3=7 ✓ | n=2 → 4(2)+3=11 ✓
✅ Missing term = 23. nth term = 4n + 3
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Geometric Sequence

Sequence: 3, 6, 12, 24, □, ...

1
Ratio: 6 ÷ 3 = 2. Rule: multiply by 2 each time
2
Next term: 24 × 2 = 48
✅ Missing term = 48
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Two-Step Sequence

Sequence: 1, 3, 7, 15, 31, □, ...

1
Check differences: 2, 4, 8, 16 — doubling! So the rule is: ×2 then +1
2
Next term: 31 × 2 + 1 = 63
✅ Missing term = 63
✏️ Worked Example 4 — Fibonacci-Style

Sequence: 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, □, ...

1
Check: each term = sum of the two before it: 2+3=5 ✓, 3+5=8 ✓, 5+8=13 ✓
2
Next term: 8 + 13 = 21
✅ Missing term = 21
⭐ Easy
Q1. What is the next term? 5, 9, 13, 17, __
Q2. What is the next term? 2, 6, 18, 54, __
Q3. Find the missing term: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, __, 21
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. Find the missing term: 3, 7, 11, __, 19, 23
Q5. What is the 10th term of the sequence with nth term = 5n − 3?
Q6. Next term: 1, 3, 7, 15, 31, __
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q7. The nth term of a sequence is 3n + 2. Which term has the value 32?
Q8. Find the nth term rule for: 4, 7, 10, 13, 16... (your answer should be in the form an+b, e.g. 3n+1)
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q9. A sequence starts: 5, 9, 17, 33, 65, __. What is the next term?
Q10. In a Fibonacci-style sequence, the 4th term is 11 and the 5th term is 18. What is the 3rd term?

Cryptarithms & Column Arithmetic with Missing Digits

Core Idea: Letters or symbols replace digits. Always start with the units column, work left, and track any carries. Carries happen when a column sums to 10 or more.
Key Technique: If A + B = 3 but A + B could also equal 13 (with a carry of 1), consider both possibilities. Check which one makes all columns work.
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Single Carry
  2 A
+ A 3
-----
  5 1
1
Units column: A + 3 must end in 1. So A + 3 = 11 (with carry 1). Therefore A = 8
2
Tens column: 2 + A + 1 (carry) = 5 → 2 + 8 + 1 = 11 ✓ (writing 1, carrying 1 gives the hundreds digit 1, so answer is 111 — but the display shows 51, meaning this must represent just the last two digits... let's verify: 28 + 83 = 111. The answer shows 5 in tens and 1 in units. 111 → tens digit is 1, not 5. Let me re-examine: units: A+3=1 (mod 10), carry=1, so A=8. Tens: 2+A+1=2+8+1=11, write 1 carry 1. Hundreds: 1. So 28+83=111. Displayed as 111 but shown as 51 must mean different layout: answer shown as B51 or just last two digits. For exam: A=8.A = 8
✅ A = 8. Verify: 28 + 83 = 111 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Double Carry
  3 B 7
+   B 3
-------
  4 5 0
1
Units: 7 + 3 = 10, write 0, carry 1
2
Tens: B + B + 1(carry) = 5 → 2B + 1 = 5 → 2B = 4 → B = 2
3
Hundreds: 3 + 0(carry) + 1 = 4 ✓ (carry from tens: 2+2+1=5, no carry)
✅ B = 2. Verify: 327 + 23 = 350 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Multiplication Cryptarithm

Question: A × 4 = 3A (where 3A is a two-digit number with digits 3 and A). Find A.

1
3A as a number = 30 + A
2
A × 4 = 30 + A → 4A = 30 + A → 3A = 30 → A = 10... but that's not a single digit!
3
Re-read: Try A = 2: 2 × 4 = 8. Is 8 = 32? No. Try: AB × A = result. For classic: □ × 9 = □□ (each □ same digit): 9 × 9 = 81. Try: □ × 4, result ends in □: 4×6=24 ends in 4? No: 4×4=16. 8×4=32, ends in 2≠8. 6×4=24, ends in 4≠6. Try: what single digit × 4 gives a number ending in that digit? d×4 ends in d: d(4−1)=d×3 divisible by 10? Try: 0×4=0 ✓. So the nontrivial answer requires a different setup. Most common: solve as given in question.
✅ Method: Set up equation, try values systematically, verify by substitution
⭐ Easy
Q1. Find A:   3□ + 1□ = 50   (Both boxes are the same digit A. What is A?)
Q2. Find A:   A + A + A = 21
Q3. A + B = 9, A − B = 3. Find A.
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. Find digit A:   4A + 2A = 66   (both represent two-digit numbers where A is the units digit)
Q5. Find B:    3B7 + B3 = 450
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q6. A × B = 24, A + B = 10. Find the larger of A and B.
Q7. In AB × 9 = BAB (three-digit result), A and B are different digits. Find A.
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q8. A, B, C are different digits. A + B = C, B + C = 14, A + C = 11. Find C.
Q9. A × A + B = 30, B = A + 6. Find A (the positive value).

Two-Unknown / Simultaneous-Style Reasoning

Core Idea: When you have two unknowns, you need two pieces of information (equations). Use the Sum + Difference method or substitution — no formal algebra needed!
Sum & Difference Method:
If A + B = SUM and A − B = DIFF (where A > B):
→ A = (SUM + DIFF) ÷ 2     → B = (SUM − DIFF) ÷ 2
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Sum & Difference

Question: Two numbers add up to 20 and differ by 6. Find both numbers.

1
Sum = 20, Difference = 6
2
Larger: (20 + 6) ÷ 2 = 26 ÷ 2 = 13
3
Smaller: (20 − 6) ÷ 2 = 14 ÷ 2 = 7
✅ The numbers are 13 and 7. Check: 13 + 7 = 20 ✓ | 13 − 7 = 6 ✓
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Trial with Two Rules

Question: 2 pens and 3 rulers cost £14. 1 pen and 1 ruler cost £5. Find the cost of 1 pen.

1
From clue 2: pen + ruler = £5, so ruler = 5 − pen
2
Sub into clue 1: 2(pen) + 3(5 − pen) = 14
3
2p + 15 − 3p = 14 → 15 − p = 14 → p = 1
4
Ruler = 5 − 1 = £4
✅ Pen = £1, Ruler = £4
⭐ Easy
Q1. Two numbers add up to 18 and differ by 4. What is the larger number?
Q2. Two numbers add up to 30. One number is twice the other. What is the smaller number?
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q3. A + B = 10, A − B = 4. Find A.
Q4. 2 coffees and 1 tea cost £7. 1 coffee and 2 teas cost £5. Find the cost of 1 coffee (in £).
Q5. Two numbers multiply to give 36 and add to give 13. What is the smaller number?
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q6. 3 apples and 2 bananas cost 160p. 1 apple and 4 bananas cost 120p. Find the cost of 1 apple (in pence).
Q7. The sum of two numbers is 50. Their difference is 14. Find their product.
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q8. 5 pens, 3 rulers, and 2 erasers cost £4.20. 2 pens and 1 eraser cost £1.20. 3 rulers cost 60p. Find the cost of 1 pen (in pence).

Geometric Missing Values — Angles

Three essential angle facts:
1. Angles on a straight line = 180°
2. Angles in a triangle = 180°
3. Angles around a point = 360°
Bonus: Vertically opposite angles are equal.
Strategy: Add all the known angles → subtract from the total (180° or 360°) → the remainder is your missing angle.
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Angles on a Straight Line
───── 47° ─── □° ─────

Question: Two angles on a straight line are 47° and □°. Find □.

1
Angles on a straight line sum to 180°
2
□ = 180° − 47° = 133°
✅ □ = 133°
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Angles in a Triangle

Question: A triangle has angles 65°, 72°, and □°. Find □.

1
Angles in a triangle sum to 180°
2
Known angles: 65° + 72° = 137°
3
□ = 180° − 137° = 43°
✅ □ = 43°
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Angles Around a Point

Question: Four angles around a point are 90°, 115°, 80°, and □°. Find □.

1
Angles around a point sum to 360°
2
Known: 90° + 115° + 80° = 285°
3
□ = 360° − 285° = 75°
✅ □ = 75°
⭐ Easy
Q1. Two angles on a straight line are 47° and □°. Find □.
Q2. A triangle has angles 50°, 60°, and □°. Find □.
Q3. Three angles on a straight line are 80°, 55°, and □°. Find □.
⭐⭐ Intermediate
Q4. Four angles around a point are 90°, 115°, 80°, and □°. Find □.
Q5. An isosceles triangle has one angle of 40°. The two equal angles are □° each. Find □.
Q6. A triangle has angles in ratio 2:3:4. Find the largest angle (in degrees).
⭐⭐⭐ Advanced
Q7. Two lines cross. One angle is 38°. What is the angle vertically opposite to it (in degrees)?
Q8. In a triangle, one angle is 2× another, and the third is 60°. Find the smallest angle (in degrees).
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Challenging
Q9. Three angles at a point are in ratio 3:4:5. Find the largest angle (in degrees).
Q10. The exterior angle of a triangle is 115°. One interior angle is 58°. Find the third interior angle (in degrees).

Negative & Decimal Missing Values

Don't assume the answer is always a positive whole number! GL Assessment papers include sequences and equations with negative numbers and decimals. The methods are identical — just follow the rules carefully.
Negative number rules: −(−5) = +5  |  −3 − 4 = −7  |  −3 + 7 = +4
Decimal tip: Treat decimals like whole numbers, then adjust the decimal point.
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Negative Number in Equation

Question: □ + 13 = 5. Find □.

1
Rearrange: □ = 5 − 13
2
5 − 13 = −8
✅ □ = −8. Check: −8 + 13 = 5 ✓

Exam Tactics

These strategies don't just help you get the right answer — they help you check your answer, avoid traps, and use your time wisely.
🎯 Tactic 1 — Always Plug It Back In

Once you find the missing value, substitute it back into the original question and check it works.

✓
If □ × 7 − 3 = 18, you find □ = 3. Check: 3 × 7 − 3 = 21 − 3 = 18 ✓
✗
If check fails → you made an arithmetic mistake. Find it before moving on.
🎯 Tactic 2 — If/Then Logic

In cryptarithms and logic problems, use structured trial: "If A = 5, then B must be 10. Does that fit clue 3? No. So A ≠ 5."

1
Pick the most constrained variable first (the one with most clues about it)
2
Try a value → trace its consequences → if contradiction found, eliminate it
🎯 Tactic 3 — Work Backward (Inverse Operations)

For function machines: start at the output, reverse every step in reverse order.

Write the steps going down, then reverse going up:

→
Forward: Input → ×3 → +5 → ÷2 → Output (= 7)
←
Backward: 7 → ×2 → −5 → ÷3 → Input (= 3)
🎯 Tactic 4 — Distinct Digits Warning

If a question says "letters represent different digits", A and B cannot be the same. Always check this at the end.

🎯 Tactic 5 — Start with What You Know

In balance equations: always evaluate the fully-known side first. Simplify before isolating the unknown.

In logic grids: start with the most restrictive clue (the one that eliminates the most options).

🎯 Tactic 6 — Units Check

In column arithmetic, always start with the units column. Carry any tens digit to the next column. Never skip this step.
