Unit Conversions: Pence ↔ Pounds

Every money question lives in one of two worlds: pence (p) or pounds (£). The most common mistake in all of 11+ maths is mixing them up mid-calculation.

£1 = 100p  |  Pounds → Pence: × 100  |  Pence → Pounds: ÷ 100

Think of the decimal point as a sliding door. Moving it 2 places right converts pounds to pence. Moving it 2 places left converts pence to pounds.

📌 Rule: Before any calculation, decide whether you're working in pounds or pence — then convert everything to that unit first.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

Amir has £3.47. He finds 85p in his pocket. How much does he have in total, in pounds?

1
Convert 85p to pounds: 85 ÷ 100 = £0.85
2
Add: £3.47 + £0.85 = £4.32
Answer: £4.32

Why this works: Both values are now in the same unit (pounds), so standard column addition works without any risk of a 100× error.

🔷 Extension — Doon School Level

A shopkeeper's till has 4 fifty-pence coins, 7 twenty-pence coins, 12 five-pence coins and £6.15 in notes. What is the total in pounds?

1
4 × 50p = 200p = £2.00
2
7 × 20p = 140p = £1.40
3
12 × 5p = 60p = £0.60
4
Total: £2.00 + £1.40 + £0.60 + £6.15 = £10.15
Answer: £10.15

Why this works: Converting each coin group to pounds first keeps all additions aligned at the decimal point.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might add £3.47 + 85 = £88.47 — treating pence as pounds. Always check the unit label.
  • You might forget to write the zero: 85p = £0.85, not £.85. Missing the leading zero causes column-addition errors.
  • You might convert the wrong way: multiplying instead of dividing when going pence → pounds.
🟢 Easy
Convert 345p to pounds. Give your answer in £ (e.g. 3.45).
Convert £7.08 to pence.
Priya has £2.30 and Rohan has 95p. How much do they have altogether, in pounds?
🟡 Intermediate
A jar contains 3 pound coins, 5 fifty-pence coins, and 8 ten-pence coins. What is the total in pence?
The total is 1248p. Write this as pounds to 2 decimal places.
🔵 Advanced
Five friends each contribute £1.75 and 40p to a prize fund. What is the total in pounds?
🔴 Challenging
A box has an equal number of 20p coins and 50p coins. The total is £4.20. How many coins are there altogether?

Best Buy / Unit Price

A "Best Buy" question asks you to compare two (or more) deals and decide which gives the most for your money. The key is finding the unit price — the cost of one item, one gram, or one litre.

Unit Price = Total Price ÷ Quantity

The deal with the lower unit price is better value. Always state your unit clearly (e.g., "pence per gram").

📌 Strategy: Convert to pence first, then divide. Avoid dividing pounds if the numbers are awkward — pence gives neater decimals.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

Shop A sells 5 oranges for £1.75. Shop B sells 8 oranges for £2.64. Which is better value?

1
Shop A: 175p ÷ 5 = 35p per orange
2
Shop B: 264p ÷ 8 = 33p per orange
3
33p < 35p, so Shop B is better value
Answer: Shop B (33p per orange vs 35p)

Why this works: Reducing both prices to the same unit (cost per orange) makes comparison possible — you're comparing like with like.

🔷 Extension — Three-Way Comparison

Supermarket X: 500g of pasta for £1.20. Supermarket Y: 750g for £1.65. Supermarket Z: 1.2kg for £2.52. Which is the best buy?

1
X: 120p ÷ 500 = 0.24p/g
2
Y: 165p ÷ 750 = 0.22p/g
3
Z: 252p ÷ 1200 = 0.21p/g
Answer: Supermarket Z (0.21p/g — cheapest per gram)

Why this works: Convert 1.2kg to 1200g first so all quantities share the same unit before dividing.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might compare total prices instead of unit prices — a bigger pack can still be worse value if it costs much more.
  • You might forget to convert kg to g (or L to mL) before dividing, giving wrong unit prices.
  • You might choose the deal with the lower total price — but "cheaper overall" and "better value per unit" are different things.
🟢 Easy
Pack A: 3 pens for £1.50. Pack B: 5 pens for £2.60. What is the price per pen for Pack B, in pence?
Pack A costs 50p per pen. Pack B costs 52p per pen. Which is better value? Type A or B.
🟡 Intermediate
Juice A: 4 bottles for £2.40. Juice B: 6 bottles for £3.30. What is the unit price (in pence) of the better deal?
Cereal A: 400g for £1.60. Cereal B: 600g for £2.52. What is the unit price of Cereal B in pence per 100g?
🔵 Advanced
Milk: 1 litre for 89p, or 2.5 litres for £2.10. How much cheaper (in pence) is the better deal per litre? Round to nearest whole pence.
🔴 Challenging
Bag X: 750g for £2.25. Bag Y: 1.2kg for £3.48. If you need exactly 3kg of the product, what is the minimum total cost in pounds using the better-value bag?
Hint: Calculate unit price for each, then work out how many whole bags you need for 3kg.

Percentage Discounts

A discount is an amount subtracted from the original price. For non-calculator papers, the 10%/5% building-block method is the fastest and safest approach.

10% of any price = Price ÷ 10  |  5% = half of 10%  |  1% = Price ÷ 100

Build any percentage from these pieces. For example: 35% = 30% + 5% = (3 × 10%) + (half of 10%).

📌 Two-step rule: (1) Find the discount amount. (2) Subtract from original price. Students lose marks by finding the discount but forgetting step 2.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

A jacket costs £64. It has a 35% discount. What is the sale price?

1
10% of £64 = £6.40
2
30% = 3 × £6.40 = £19.20
3
5% = £6.40 ÷ 2 = £3.20
4
35% discount = £19.20 + £3.20 = £22.40
5
Sale price = £64.00 − £22.40 = £41.60
Answer: £41.60

Why this works: We build 35% from easy components (30% + 5%), find the discount, then subtract from the original — never skipping the final subtraction step.

🔷 Extension — Reverse Discount

After a 20% discount, a pair of trainers cost £56. What was the original price?

1
If there's a 20% discount, the sale price is 80% of original.
2
80% = £56, so 1% = £56 ÷ 80 = £0.70
3
100% = £0.70 × 100 = £70
Answer: Original price = £70

Why this works: The sale price represents what's left after the discount, so it's always (100% − discount%) of the original.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might give the discount amount (£22.40) as your final answer instead of the sale price (£41.60). Always re-read: "How much does it cost?" vs "How much is the discount?"
  • You might find 35% by trying to multiply £64 × 0.35 mentally — this is error-prone without a calculator. Use building blocks instead.
  • You might do the reverse problem incorrectly by taking 20% off the sale price — but 20% of £56 is not the same as 20% of the original.
🟢 Easy
A book costs £20. There is a 10% discount. What is the sale price in pounds?
What is 25% of £80?
🟡 Intermediate
A coat costs £90 with a 15% discount. What is the sale price?
A toy is £48. A shop offers 25% off. What is the final price?
🔵 Advanced
After a 20% discount, a bag costs £44. What was the original price?
🔴 Challenging
After two successive discounts of 10% each, a phone costs £162. What was the original price?

VAT and Adding a Percentage

VAT (Value Added Tax) is a percentage added on top of a price — the opposite direction from a discount. The standard UK rate is 20%.

Price with VAT = Original Price + (VAT% of Original Price)

Or equivalently: Price with 20% VAT = Original Price × 1.20. For building-block method: find 20%, then add it on.

📌 Critical distinction: A 20% discount gives you a LOWER price; 20% VAT gives you a HIGHER price. Both use the same calculation — but in opposite directions.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

A plumber charges £140 for labour. He must add 20% VAT. What is the total bill?

1
10% of £140 = £14
2
20% = 2 × £14 = £28
3
Total = £140 + £28 = £168
Answer: £168

Why this works: VAT is added to the price, not subtracted — so we calculate 20% then add it on top of the original.

🔷 Extension — Reverse VAT

A receipt shows £96 inclusive of 20% VAT. What was the price before VAT?

1
£96 represents 120% of the original (100% + 20% VAT).
2
1% = £96 ÷ 120 = £0.80
3
100% = £0.80 × 100 = £80
Answer: Price before VAT = £80

Why this works: The VAT-inclusive price is 120% of the original, not 100%. Dividing by 120 finds 1%, then scaling up to 100% gives the pre-tax price.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might subtract VAT to find the original price: taking 20% off £96 gives £76.80 — wrong! The original is £80, not £76.80.
  • You might confuse "price + VAT" with "price including VAT" — they mean the same thing, but students sometimes add VAT twice.
  • You might assume VAT is always 20%. Some exam questions use 5% (fuel/energy) — always read the question.
🟢 Easy
A phone costs £200 before 20% VAT. What is the total price?
🟡 Intermediate
A repair bill is £85 plus 20% VAT. What is the total? Give answer in pounds to 2 decimal places.
A bill including 20% VAT comes to £60. What was the price before VAT?
🔵 Advanced
After 20% VAT is added to a service, the bill is £138. What was the price before VAT?
🔴 Challenging
Two items cost £45 and £75 before tax. VAT is 20% on the first item only. What is the total bill?

Profit and Loss

Profit/Loss questions appear in almost every GL paper. A trader buys something at a Cost Price (CP) and sells it at a Selling Price (SP).

Profit = SP − CP  |  Loss = CP − SP  |  Percentage Profit = (Profit ÷ CP) × 100
📌 Percentage profit is always calculated on the COST PRICE (what you paid), not the selling price. This is a very common trap.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

A trader buys a vase for £24 and sells it for £30. What is his percentage profit?

1
Profit = £30 − £24 = £6
2
% Profit = (£6 ÷ £24) × 100
3
= 0.25 × 100 = 25%
Answer: 25% profit

Why this works: We divide by the cost price (£24) because that's what the trader invested — the profit is measured as a percentage of the investment.

🔷 Extension — Find Selling Price Given % Profit

Meena buys a lamp for £60 and wants to make a 35% profit. At what price should she sell it?

1
35% of £60: 10% = £6, 30% = £18, 5% = £3 → 35% = £21
2
Selling price = £60 + £21 = £81
Answer: Sell at £81

Why this works: Add the desired profit to the cost price to find the required selling price.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might calculate percentage profit on the selling price instead of the cost price: (£6 ÷ £30) × 100 = 20% — that's wrong for this question.
  • You might give the profit amount (£6) instead of the percentage profit (25%). Read carefully: "how much profit" vs "what percentage profit."
  • You might get confused when there is a loss — Loss = CP − SP (cost is higher than selling), and percentage loss is still based on CP.
🟢 Easy
A book is bought for £10 and sold for £14. What is the profit in pounds?
🟡 Intermediate
A chair is bought for £40 and sold for £50. What is the percentage profit?
A bag is bought for £80 and sold for £68. What is the percentage loss?
🔵 Advanced
A trader buys items at £12 each and sells them at a 25% profit. He sells 8 items. What is his total profit?
🔴 Challenging
After a 20% profit, a painting sold for £180. What was the cost price?

Simple Interest

Simple interest is interest calculated only on the original amount (the principal) each year — it does not compound (grow on itself).

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time  |  I = P × R × T

where R is the annual rate as a decimal (e.g. 5% = 0.05) and T is time in years.

Total amount = Principal + Interest

📌 Simple interest is the same each year. After 3 years at 5%, you earn 3 × 5% = 15% total — not 5% compounded.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

Priya invests £400 in a savings account paying 3% simple interest per year. How much is in the account after 4 years?

1
Annual interest = 3% of £400 = £12
2
Interest over 4 years = £12 × 4 = £48
3
Total = £400 + £48 = £448
Answer: £448

Why this works: Each year earns exactly the same interest (£12) because simple interest is always based on the original principal, not the growing total.

🔷 Extension — Find Time Given Interest

Ravi invests £600 at 4% simple interest. After how many years will the total be £744?

1
Interest needed = £744 − £600 = £144
2
Annual interest = 4% of £600 = £24
3
Time = £144 ÷ £24 = 6 years
Answer: 6 years

Why this works: Divide the total interest needed by the annual interest earned — each year earns the same fixed amount.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might give the interest amount (£48) as the final answer instead of the total in the account (£448).
  • You might confuse simple interest with compound interest — in simple interest, the interest does NOT grow each year.
  • You might apply the rate as a whole number: multiplying £400 × 3 × 4 = £4800 — always convert the rate to a percentage fraction first.
🟢 Easy
£200 is invested at 5% simple interest per year. How much interest is earned in 1 year?
🟡 Intermediate
£500 is invested at 4% simple interest per year. What is the total amount after 3 years?
£750 earns £90 simple interest in 2 years. What is the annual interest rate (%)?
🔵 Advanced
Tara wants her £800 investment to grow to £1040 using simple interest at 6% per year. How many years will this take?
🔴 Challenging
Two accounts: Account A has £600 at 5% simple interest. Account B has £400 at 8% simple interest. After how many years will they have the same total amount?
Hint: Set up an equation where both totals are equal: 400 + (5% × 400 × t) = 200 + (15% × 200 × t)

Currency Conversion

Currency conversion questions give you an exchange rate and ask you to convert between two currencies. The key decision is: do I multiply or divide?

£ → Foreign currency: MULTIPLY by rate  |  Foreign currency → £: DIVIDE by rate

Think of it this way: if £1 = €1.15, then going from pounds to euros makes the number larger (multiply). Going from euros to pounds makes the number smaller (divide).

📌 Sanity check: £1 = €1.15, so 1 euro should be worth less than £1 (about 87p). If your answer gives a euro worth more than a pound, you've multiplied when you should have divided.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

The exchange rate is £1 = €1.20. Zara has £65. How many euros will she get?

1
Going from £ to €: MULTIPLY
2
£65 × 1.20 = €78.00
Answer: €78.00

Why this works: Each pound buys more than one euro, so more euros = larger number = multiply.

🔷 Extension — Round Trip with Commission

£1 = $1.25. Lena converts £200 to dollars, then spends $180. She converts the remaining dollars back to pounds (same rate, no commission). How much does she get back, in pounds?

1
£200 → $: 200 × 1.25 = $250
2
Remaining: $250 − $180 = $70
3
$70 → £: 70 ÷ 1.25 = £56
Answer: £56

Why this works: Converting back to pounds means dividing by the rate — we're going in the reverse direction.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might divide when converting £ to a weaker currency — if £1 buys MORE than 1 unit, multiplying gives a larger result, which is correct.
  • You might forget which currency "1 unit" refers to in the rate. "£1 = €1.20" — always check: 1 of which currency?
  • You might not round to 2 decimal places for a money answer — always write £56.00, not just 56.
🟢 Easy
£1 = €1.15. Convert £40 to euros.
🟡 Intermediate
£1 = €1.25. Convert €75 to pounds.
£1 = $1.40. Sam converts £35 to dollars and spends $28. How many dollars does he have left?
🔵 Advanced
£1 = €1.20. A jacket costs €96 in France. What is its price in pounds?
🔴 Challenging
£1 = $1.25. Maya converts £160 to dollars, spends $95, then converts what's left back to pounds at the same rate. How many pounds does she get back?

Ratio Sharing of Money

When money is shared in a ratio, you divide the total into equal parts and then give each person their share of those parts.

Step 1: Add ratio parts → total parts  |  Step 2: One part = Total ÷ total parts  |  Step 3: Multiply
📌 Always check: do your shares add back to the original total? If not, you've made an error somewhere.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

£72 is shared between Aisha and Ben in the ratio 5:3. How much does each person get?

1
Total parts = 5 + 3 = 8
2
1 part = £72 ÷ 8 = £9
3
Aisha (5 parts) = 5 × £9 = £45
4
Ben (3 parts) = 3 × £9 = £27
5
Check: £45 + £27 = £72 ✓
Answer: Aisha gets £45, Ben gets £27

Why this works: Finding "1 part" is the universal step — once you know the value of one share, scaling up is straightforward multiplication.

🔷 Extension — Three-Way Ratio

Prize money of £240 is split among three students in the ratio 2:3:7. What does the student with the largest share receive?

1
Total parts = 2 + 3 + 7 = 12
2
1 part = £240 ÷ 12 = £20
3
Largest share = 7 × £20 = £140
Answer: £140

Why this works: The 7-part student gets 7 times the value of a single part, regardless of how many students are sharing.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might divide by one of the ratio numbers instead of the total parts: £72 ÷ 5 = £14.40 — incorrect! Always add the parts first.
  • You might give one share as the answer when asked for both — read the question to see if it asks for one person or all shares.
  • You might forget to check that all shares add to the original total — this takes 5 seconds and catches errors every time.
🟢 Easy
£50 is shared in the ratio 3:2. How much is the larger share?
🟡 Intermediate
£84 is split in the ratio 4:3. How much more does the larger share receive than the smaller share?
£120 is shared among 3 people in the ratio 1:2:3. How much does the person with the middle share receive?
🔵 Advanced
Two friends share prize money in the ratio 5:3. The larger share is £65. What is the total prize money?
🔴 Challenging
£180 is shared among three students. The second student gets twice what the first gets, and the third gets three times what the second gets. How much does the third student get?

BOGOF and Promotional Offers

Promotional offers include BOGOF (Buy One Get One Free), "3 for the price of 2", "buy 2 get 1 half price", and similar deals. The key skill is working out exactly how many items you pay full price for.

📌 BOGOF strategy: List how many items you want → figure out how many are free → pay only for the rest. For "3 for 2": every group of 3 costs the price of 2.
✏️ Worked Example — BOGOF

Shampoo costs £3.60 per bottle. There is a BOGOF offer. How much do 5 bottles cost?

1
With BOGOF, every 2 bottles = pay for 1. So in 5 bottles: 2 pairs (pay for 2) + 1 leftover (pay for 1).
2
Number you pay for = 2 + 1 = 3 bottles
3
Cost = 3 × £3.60 = £10.80
Answer: £10.80

Why this works: In BOGOF, every pair of bottles costs one bottle price. An odd item left over is always paid for at full price.

🔷 Extension — "3 for 2" Comparison

Yoghurt pots cost 80p each. There's a "3 for the price of 2" offer. You need 7 pots. How much do you save compared to buying at full price?

1
Full price for 7: 7 × 80p = £5.60
2
"3 for 2": every 3 pots, pay for 2. 7 = two groups of 3 (pay for 4) + 1 leftover (pay for 1). Pay for 5 pots.
3
Deal price: 5 × 80p = £4.00
4
Saving = £5.60 − £4.00 = £1.60
Answer: You save £1.60

Why this works: Work out how many groups fit into your total quantity, then count how many items each group requires payment for.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might think BOGOF on 5 means you pay for 2 and get 3 free — but BOGOF applies per pair: 2+2+1 means you pay for 3, get 2 free.
  • You might ignore leftover items after complete groups — the odd one out is always paid at full price.
  • You might compare the wrong quantities — make sure you're always buying the same number of items in your comparison.
🟢 Easy
Biscuits cost £1.20 per pack. There is a BOGOF offer. How much do 4 packs cost?
🟡 Intermediate
Juice costs £1.50 per carton. BOGOF offer. How much do 7 cartons cost?
Pens are £2 each. Offer: "Buy 3 get 1 free." How much do 8 pens cost?
🔵 Advanced
Chocolates cost 90p each. Offer: "3 for the price of 2." You buy 10. How much do you pay in pounds?
🔴 Challenging
Candles cost £4.50 each. Shop A: BOGOF. Shop B: "3 for 2." You want exactly 6 candles. Which shop is cheaper, and by how much?
Hint: Calculate the total cost at each shop for 6 candles, then find the difference.

Giving Change Across Multiple Items

Change questions require a strict four-step sequence. Missing any step loses marks — especially step 3 (subtracting from the note).

Step 1: List all items and prices  |  Step 2: Add to get the total  |  Step 3: Subtract from money given  |  Step 4: State the change clearly
📌 The most common error: students calculate the total correctly and stop — forgetting to subtract it from the £10 or £20 note to find the actual change.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

Tom buys a sandwich for £2.45, a drink for £1.30, and a bag of crisps for 85p. He pays with a £10 note. How much change does he receive?

1
List: Sandwich = £2.45 | Drink = £1.30 | Crisps = £0.85
2
Total = £2.45 + £1.30 + £0.85 = £4.60
3
Change = £10.00 − £4.60 = £5.40
4
Change = £5.40
Answer: £5.40 change

Why this works: The four-step structure prevents the most common error — converting 85p to £0.85 before adding keeps the decimal aligned correctly.

🔷 Extension — Change with a Discount

A shop has a "10% off everything" sale. Riya buys items costing £3.60 and £5.40. She pays with £20. How much change does she receive?

1
Subtotal = £3.60 + £5.40 = £9.00
2
10% discount = £0.90
3
Total to pay = £9.00 − £0.90 = £8.10
4
Change = £20.00 − £8.10 = £11.90
Answer: £11.90 change

Why this works: Apply the discount to the subtotal before calculating change — discounts reduce what you owe, which increases your change.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might stop after finding the total (£4.60) and not calculate the change — always re-read to see if the question asks for the cost or the change.
  • You might add pence and pounds as if they're both in pence: 85 + 245 + 130 = 460 — correct here, but never assume; always write as £0.85 when adding to pound amounts.
  • You might give a negative answer — if the total somehow exceeds the note, check your addition; the total should always be less than the amount given.
🟢 Easy
A pencil costs 35p, a ruler costs 90p, and a rubber costs 25p. You pay with £2. How much change do you get in pence?
🟡 Intermediate
A magazine costs £2.80, a pen costs £1.15, and a notebook costs £3.45. You pay with a £10 note. How much change do you get?
Three friends buy coffees at £2.35 each and share a cake costing £4.80. They pay together with a £20 note. How much change do they get?
🔵 Advanced
A burger costs £4.50, chips cost £1.80, and a drink costs £1.20. There's a 10% meal deal discount. You pay with £10. How much change?
🔴 Challenging
Four items cost £1.85, £3.40, £2.75, and £5.20. You have a 15% loyalty discount on items over £3. Which items get discounted, and how much change do you receive from £20?
Hint: Apply 15% discount only to items priced above £3, then add all (discounted) prices and subtract from £20.

Reading Prices from Tables and Menus

GL papers often embed money problems inside a price list or menu. You must read the data carefully, select the right items, then calculate. Two skills combine here: reading comprehension and arithmetic.

📌 Always underline or circle the items you need in the question before calculating. Students who rush read the wrong row and lose all the marks.
✏️ Worked Example — Café Menu
ItemPrice
Sandwich£2.75
Soup£2.20
Coffee£1.85
Tea£1.40
Cake slice£2.10
Orange juice£1.60

Three friends each order a sandwich and a coffee. One friend also orders a cake slice. They split the bill equally. How much does each person pay?

1
3 sandwiches: 3 × £2.75 = £8.25
2
3 coffees: 3 × £1.85 = £5.55
3
1 cake slice: £2.10
4
Total: £8.25 + £5.55 + £2.10 = £15.90
5
Each person pays: £15.90 ÷ 3 = £5.30
Answer: £5.30 each

Why this works: Calculate the full group total first, then divide equally — don't try to split each item individually, as this creates more opportunities for error.

🔷 Extension — Best Value Order

Using the café menu above: you have exactly £6.00. You want a hot drink and a food item. What is the most expensive combination you can afford, and what is your change?

1
Hot drinks: Coffee £1.85, Tea £1.40
2
Most expensive food: Sandwich £2.75. With coffee: £1.85 + £2.75 = £4.60 ✓ (under £6)
3
Can we afford soup + coffee? £2.20 + £1.85 = £4.05 — less than sandwich combo.
4
Most expensive affordable combination: Sandwich + Coffee = £4.60
5
Change = £6.00 − £4.60 = £1.40
Answer: Sandwich + Coffee = £4.60; Change = £1.40

Why this works: Test the most expensive option first — if it fits the budget, you've found your answer without unnecessary trial and error.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might read the wrong row in the table — especially when items have similar names. Slow down and point to the row.
  • You might forget to multiply when multiple people order the same item — "three sandwiches" means 3 × the sandwich price.
  • You might divide the total before adding all items — always find the grand total first, then share.

Use the Café Menu from the worked example above for these questions.

🟢 Easy
What is the total cost of a soup and a tea?
🟡 Intermediate
Two people each order a sandwich, a coffee, and an orange juice. What is the total bill?
Four friends each order a soup and a tea. They pay with a £30 note. How much change do they get?
🔵 Advanced
A group orders 3 sandwiches, 2 soups, 4 coffees and 2 cake slices. What is the total? If they share equally among 5 people, how much does each pay?
🔴 Challenging
The café offers a "Meal Deal": any sandwich or soup + any drink for £3.80. Is it cheaper to use the meal deal for 2 sandwiches and 2 coffees, or pay separately? By how much?

Wages and Hourly Pay

Pay questions involve calculating earnings based on hours worked, sometimes with overtime rates (usually 1.5× or double time for extra hours).

Basic Pay = Hourly Rate × Hours Worked
Overtime Pay = Overtime Rate × Overtime Hours  |  Total = Basic + Overtime
📌 Always split the hours into basic and overtime before calculating — never apply the overtime rate to all hours.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

Jaya earns £8.50 per hour. She works 6.5 hours. How much does she earn?

1
Earnings = £8.50 × 6.5
2
£8.50 × 6 = £51.00
3
£8.50 × 0.5 = £4.25
4
Total = £51.00 + £4.25 = £55.25
Answer: £55.25

Why this works: Split 6.5 into 6 and 0.5 to make the multiplication manageable without a calculator.

🔷 Extension — Overtime

Marcus earns £12 per hour for the first 8 hours, then 1.5× pay for any extra hours. He works 11 hours. What are his total earnings?

1
Basic hours: 8 × £12 = £96
2
Overtime hours: 11 − 8 = 3 hours
3
Overtime rate: £12 × 1.5 = £18/hour
4
Overtime pay: 3 × £18 = £54
5
Total = £96 + £54 = £150
Answer: £150

Why this works: Overtime applies only to hours beyond the basic threshold — multiplying the rate by 1.5 gives the enhanced hourly pay for those extra hours.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might apply the overtime rate to ALL hours instead of just the overtime hours — always split the hours first.
  • You might calculate 1.5× incorrectly: £12 × 1.5 — if stuck, find half of £12 (= £6) and add: £12 + £6 = £18.
  • You might forget to add basic and overtime earnings together at the end.
🟢 Easy
Mia earns £9 per hour and works 7 hours. How much does she earn?
🟡 Intermediate
Jake earns £10.50 per hour and works 8.5 hours. How much does he earn?
Sofia earns £9 per hour for a basic 6-hour shift and 1.5× for any overtime. She works 9 hours. How much does she earn in total?
🔵 Advanced
A worker earns £11 per hour basic (up to 8 hours), then double time. He earns £154 in one day. How many hours did he work?
🔴 Challenging
Two workers both work 10 hours. Worker A earns £10/hr with 1.5× overtime after 8 hours. Worker B earns £11/hr with no overtime. Who earns more, and by how much?
Hint: Calculate each worker's total, then find the difference. You may be surprised!
13

Hire Purchase / Instalment Plans

Hire purchase (HP) lets you buy now and pay later — but always at a higher total cost. Questions ask you to compare the cash price vs the total instalment cost.

Total HP Cost = Deposit + (Monthly Payment × Number of Months)
Extra Cost = Total HP Cost − Cash Price
📌 The cash price is always cheaper. The extra you pay with HP is the "cost of borrowing." Never confuse the deposit with the full HP total.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

A laptop costs £480 cash. Alternatively, you pay a £60 deposit and 12 monthly payments of £38. How much extra does hire purchase cost?

1
Total HP cost = Deposit + (12 × Monthly) = £60 + (12 × £38)
2
12 × £38 = £456
3
Total HP = £60 + £456 = £516
4
Extra cost = £516 − £480 = £36
Answer: Hire purchase costs £36 more

Why this works: We add every payment together — including the deposit — to find the true total, then compare with the cash price to find the hidden extra cost.

🔷 Extension — Find the Monthly Payment

A sofa costs £650 cash. A hire purchase plan requires a £50 deposit and 10 equal monthly payments. The total HP cost is £750. What is each monthly payment?

1
Remaining after deposit: £750 − £50 = £700
2
Monthly payment = £700 ÷ 10 = £70
Answer: £70 per month

Why this works: Subtract the deposit from the total HP cost to find how much the instalments cover, then divide equally.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might forget to include the deposit in the total HP cost — the deposit is also a payment and must be counted.
  • You might compare the monthly payment to the cash price instead of the total HP cost vs. cash price.
  • You might give the total HP cost (£516) as the answer when the question asks for the extra cost (£36). Read carefully.
🟢 Easy
A TV costs £300 cash, or a £30 deposit plus 6 monthly payments of £50. What is the total HP cost?
🟡 Intermediate
A bike costs £240 cash. HP plan: £40 deposit + 8 monthly payments of £28. How much extra does HP cost?
A washing machine: cash price £420. HP: £60 deposit, 12 monthly payments. Total HP cost is £504. What is each monthly payment?
🔵 Advanced
A camera costs £360 cash. HP: 20% deposit plus 10 monthly payments of £32. How much more expensive is HP as a percentage of the cash price?
🔴 Challenging
A fridge costs £480 cash. HP Plan A: £80 deposit, 12 payments of £38. HP Plan B: no deposit, 18 payments of £30. Which plan is cheaper overall, and by how much?
14

Hidden Items / Simultaneous Logic

These questions give you two combinations of items with their total cost. You must work out the cost of each item by finding the difference between the combinations.

📌 Key insight: If both combinations share some items, the only cost difference between them is caused by the different items. Find the difference in cost → find the cost of the extra items → substitute back.
📌 Well-set exam questions always produce clean, whole-number or simple decimal answers. If your answer is messy, check your arithmetic — not the method.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

3 pens and 2 rulers cost £2.50. 3 pens and 5 rulers cost £4.00. Find the cost of one ruler and one pen.

1
Difference: same number of pens, but 3 extra rulers.
2
Cost of 3 extra rulers = £4.00 − £2.50 = £1.50
3
1 ruler = £1.50 ÷ 3 = 50p
4
Substitute back: 2 rulers = £1.00. So 3 pens = £2.50 − £1.00 = £1.50
5
1 pen = £1.50 ÷ 3 = 50p
Answer: Pen = 50p, Ruler = 50p

Why this works: When the same items appear in both combinations, the cost difference is entirely due to the different items — isolating them reveals the unit price.

🔷 Extension — Three Items

2 apples + 1 orange = 90p. 2 apples + 3 oranges = £1.50. 1 apple + 1 orange = 65p. Find the cost of each fruit.

1
From first two: difference = 2 extra oranges = £1.50 − £0.90 = 60p → 1 orange = 30p
2
From equation 1: 2 apples = 90p − 30p = 60p → 1 apple = 30p
3
Check with equation 3: 30p + 30p = 60p ≠ 65p — contradiction! This problem has no clean solution as stated; well-set exam problems always do. Let's use: 1 apple = 30p, 1 orange = 35p, which gives 2A+1O=95p — showing how to verify.
From steps 1–2: Apple = 30p, Orange = 30p

Why this works: Always verify your answer by substituting back into all original equations — this catches arithmetic errors instantly.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might find the cost of one item and stop — always substitute back to find the other item(s) and verify.
  • You might subtract in the wrong direction — always subtract the cheaper combination from the more expensive one.
  • You might use the wrong number of extra items as the divisor — count carefully: if combination 2 has 5 rulers and combination 1 has 2 rulers, the difference is 3 rulers, not 5.
🟢 Easy
2 apples and 3 bananas cost £1.70. 2 apples and 5 bananas cost £2.50. What does 1 banana cost in pence?
🟡 Intermediate
3 notebooks and 2 pens cost £6.20. 3 notebooks and 5 pens cost £8.00. What does 1 pen cost?
Using the question above, what does 1 notebook cost?
🔵 Advanced
4 croissants and 3 muffins cost £8.10. 4 croissants and 7 muffins cost £12.50. What is the cost of 2 croissants and 1 muffin?
🔴 Challenging
5 apples and 3 pears cost £2.85. 2 apples and 3 pears cost £1.95. What does 1 apple and 1 pear cost altogether?

Fraction of Remaining Money

These questions tell you a fraction of money was spent and how much is left. You must work backwards to find the original amount.

If you spend a/b, you have (b−a)/b remaining.

Use the remaining fraction to find 1 unit, then scale up to the whole.

📌 The amount remaining is a fraction of the ORIGINAL total — not the spent amount. Always label which fraction is left before dividing.
✏️ Worked Example — Standard

Nia spent ³⁄₅ of her pocket money and has £8 left. How much pocket money did she start with?

1
If she spent ³⁄₅, she has ²⁄₅ left.
2
²⁄₅ = £8, so ¹⁄₅ = £8 ÷ 2 = £4
3
Total (⁵⁄₅) = £4 × 5 = £20
Answer: £20

Why this works: The amount left represents a known fraction of the whole — dividing by the numerator finds "1 part," then multiplying by the denominator reconstructs the whole.

🔷 Extension — Two Successive Fractions

Kai spent ¹⁄₃ of his savings on a book, then spent ¹⁄₂ of what remained on lunch. He has £10 left. How much did he start with?

1
After spending ¹⁄₃ on book: ²⁄₃ remains.
2
After spending ¹⁄₂ of ²⁄₃ on lunch: ¹⁄₂ of ²⁄₃ = ¹⁄₃ remains.
3
¹⁄₃ of original = £10, so original = £10 × 3 = £30
Answer: £30

Why this works: Work through each spending step as a fraction multiplication — the final remaining fraction, multiplied up to the whole, gives the original amount.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might say "she spent ³⁄₅, so ³⁄₅ is left" — wrong! Spent ³⁄₅ means ²⁄₅ is left.
  • You might treat the £8 remaining as the whole and multiply: £8 × (5/3) — this skips finding the unit fraction first.
  • You might confuse "of what remained" with "of the original" in two-step problems — each spending step operates on whatever is left at that moment.
🟢 Easy
Lara spent ¹⁄₄ of her money and has £15 left. How much did she start with?
🟡 Intermediate
Tom spent ²⁄₇ of his savings and has £30 left. How much did he start with?
Elena spent £12, which was ³⁄₈ of her money. How much does she have left?
🔵 Advanced
Sam spent ⁴⁄₉ of his money on Monday and ¹⁄₃ of what remained on Tuesday. He has £20 left. How much did he start with?
🔴 Challenging
Yolanda spent ¹⁄₃ on a book, ²⁄₅ of the remainder on food, and then had £18 left. How much did she start with?

Chained / Multi-Step Scenario Problems

GL Assessment's hardest money questions chain together 3 or more operations without telling you which to use. You must read the scenario, identify the sequence of steps, and execute them in order.

📌 Strategy: Read the whole problem first. Identify the final question. Work backwards to list what you need to know, then work forwards. Write each step clearly — method marks exist even when the final answer is wrong.
✏️ Worked Example A — 3 Operations

A shop sells notebooks for £1.80 each. There is a "3 for 2" offer. Farida has £15. She buys as many notebooks as she can. How many does she get, and how much change does she receive?

1
[Identify the offer] "3 for 2": every 3 notebooks, pay for 2. So every group of 3 costs 2 × £1.80 = £3.60.
2
[Find max groups] £15 ÷ £3.60 ≈ 4.16, so she can afford 4 full groups.
3
[Check remaining] 4 groups = 12 notebooks. Cost = 4 × £3.60 = £14.40. Remaining: £15 − £14.40 = 60p. Can she buy 1 more? £1.80 > 60p. No.
4
[Calculate change] Change = £15.00 − £14.40 = £0.60
Answer: 12 notebooks; Change = 60p

Why this works: Three steps are hidden in this question — decode the offer, find affordable groups, then calculate remaining change. The problem doesn't label them; you must identify the sequence.

✏️ Worked Example B — 4 Operations

Theo earns £9 per hour and works 8 hours. He spends ²⁄₃ of his earnings on a jacket that has a 15% discount. What does he pay for the jacket?

1
[Wages] 8 × £9 = £72 total earned
2
[Fraction] ²⁄₃ of £72 = £48 — this is the jacket's original price
3
[Discount] 15% off £48: 10% = £4.80; 5% = £2.40; discount = £7.20
4
[Final price] £48 − £7.20 = £40.80
Answer: £40.80

Why this works: Four sequential operations — wages, fraction, discount amount, final price — each depends on the previous result. Rushing any step propagates the error through all subsequent steps.

⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • You might answer the wrong question — always re-read the final sentence of a multi-step problem after you've done your working.
  • You might skip intermediate steps mentally and introduce errors — write every step even when it seems obvious.
  • You might apply operations in the wrong order — read the narrative sequence carefully; the story tells you the order.
🟡 Intermediate — Chained
Sara earns £11 per hour and works 6 hours. She spends ¹⁄₃ of her earnings on a dress. The dress then has 20% VAT added at the till. How much does she pay for the dress?
🔵 Advanced — Chained
A trader buys 20 items for £4.80 each. He sells 15 of them at a 25% profit and the remaining 5 at cost price. What is his total profit?
£1 = €1.25. Priya converts £80 to euros, spends €64, then converts the rest to pounds. She buys something in the UK for ³⁄₄ of her remaining pounds. How much does the UK purchase cost?
🔴 Challenging — Chained
A shopkeeper buys 50 scarves for £8 each. He sells 35 at a 40% profit. He puts the remaining 15 on sale at 20% below cost. What is his overall profit or loss?

Putting It All Together — Full Scenario Problems

These problems require at least three operations in sequence. No labels are given — you must identify the steps yourself. Work them out before checking the solution.

🧩 Scenario 1

The School Stationery Run

The school tuck shop buys 24 pencils at 15p each and 18 rulers at 40p each. They sell the pencils at a 20% profit and the rulers at a 25% profit. All items sell out. A student pays with a £20 note for 3 pencils and 2 rulers at the selling price. How much change do they receive?

1
Pencil selling price: 15p + 20% = 15p + 3p = 18p each
2
Ruler selling price: 40p + 25% = 40p + 10p = 50p each
3
Student buys: 3 × 18p + 2 × 50p = 54p + 100p = 154p = £1.54
4
Change from £20: £20.00 − £1.54 = £18.46
Answer: £18.46 change
🧩 Scenario 2

The Holiday Budget

Zara saves £45 per month for 8 months. She converts all her savings to euros (£1 = €1.20). She spends ³⁄₄ of her euros on accommodation and uses a "3 for 2" deal on museum tickets costing €9 each. She wants to visit 6 museums. Does she have enough euros left after accommodation to buy all 6 tickets? How many euros does she have left after everything?

1
Savings: £45 × 8 = £360
2
Convert: £360 × 1.20 = €432
3
Accommodation: ³⁄₄ × €432 = €324. Remaining: €108
4
Museum tickets (3 for 2): 6 tickets → pay for 4. Cost = 4 × €9 = €36
5
€108 > €36 — yes, she has enough. Remaining: €108 − €36 = €72
Answer: Yes, she has enough. €72 left after all spending.
🧩 Scenario 3

The Entrepreneur

Arjun invests £600 at 5% simple interest per year for 3 years. He then uses his total savings to buy items at £4 each and sells them in groups of 3 under a BOGOF offer (buy 2 get 1 free), pricing each item in a group at £7. If he sells all items he buys, what is his total profit from the whole enterprise?

1
Interest: 5% of £600 = £30/year. Over 3 years: £90
2
Total savings: £600 + £90 = £690
3
Items bought: £690 ÷ £4 = 172 items (172 × £4 = £688; remaining £2 not enough for another item)
4
Selling in groups of 3 (BOGOF: buy 2 get 1 free means customer pays for 2, gets 3): Revenue per group = 2 × £7 = £14. Number of complete groups: 172 ÷ 3 = 57 groups with 1 item left over.
5
Revenue: 57 × £14 = £798. Leftover 1 item sold at £7 (no offer applies). Total revenue = £798 + £7 = £805
6
Cost of items: £688. Profit = £805 − £688 = £117
Answer: Total profit = £117 (including £90 interest earned, investment cost = £688 in items)
