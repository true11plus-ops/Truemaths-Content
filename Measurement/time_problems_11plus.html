1Clock Systems — 12-Hour and 24-Hour

The 12-hour clock uses am (midnight to noon) and pm (noon to midnight). The 24-hour clock uses four digits and no am/pm — it runs from 00:00 (midnight) to 23:59.

📐 Conversion Rules
am times → 24-hr: same digits (just write with leading zero if needed). 8:20 am = 08:20
pm times → 24-hr: add 12 to the hours. 3:45 pm → 3 + 12 = 15 → 15:45
24-hr (≥13:00) → pm: subtract 12. 21:20 → 21 − 12 = 9 → 9:20 pm
24-hr (< 13:00) → am: same digits. 08:20 = 8:20 am
⚠️ The Four Edge Cases — Read These Carefully

12:00 am = 00:00 (midnight). The day starts at 00:00. 12:00 am is NOT 12:00 in 24-hr.

12:00 pm = 12:00 (noon). Midday stays as 12:00 in 24-hr. It does NOT become 24:00.

12:30 pm = 12:30 — it does NOT become 24:30 or 00:30. Times from 12:00 pm to 12:59 pm stay as 12:xx in 24-hr format.

12:30 am = 00:30 — it does NOT stay as 12:30. The am rule: 12 am is midnight = 00:00, so 12:30 am = 00:30.

✏️ Worked Example 1 — Simple pm conversion
Q: Convert 6:45 pm to 24-hour clock.
Rule: pm → add 12 to hours
6 + 12 = 18
Minutes stay the same: 45
✅ 6:45 pm = 18:45
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Edge case: 12:30 pm
Q: Convert 12:30 pm to 24-hour clock.
Trap: students add 12 → 24:30. WRONG!
Rule: 12 pm = noon = 12:00 in 24-hr. Times from 12:00–12:59 pm stay unchanged.
12:30 pm → do NOT add 12 → stays as 12:30
✅ 12:30 pm = 12:30
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Edge case: 12:30 am
Q: Convert 12:30 am to 24-hour clock.
Rule: 12:00 am = midnight = 00:00
So 12:30 am = 00:30 (30 minutes after midnight)
It does NOT stay as 12:30!
✅ 12:30 am = 00:30
✏️ Worked Example 4 — 24-hour back to 12-hour
Q: Convert 22:15 to 12-hour clock.
22 ≥ 13, so subtract 12: 22 − 12 = 10
Since original was ≥ 12:00, it's pm.
Minutes stay: 15
✅ 22:15 = 10:15 pm
⚠️ Common Mistake — The 12 pm trap

12 pm does not mean add 12 to get 24:00. Noon is simply 12:00 in 24-hr. The add-12 rule applies to 1 pm through 11 pm only. Remember: 12 pm → 12:00, 12 am → 00:00.

2Time Unit Conversions
⚠️ Critical: Time is NOT base-10

Every metric conversion uses ×10 or ×1000. Time uses 60 (seconds↔minutes, minutes↔hours). You cannot shift a decimal point. Always divide by 60 — never by 10 or 100.

📐 Conversion Chain
60 seconds = 1 minute
60 minutes = 1 hour
24 hours = 1 day
To convert minutes → hours and minutes: divide by 60. Quotient = hours, remainder = minutes.
✏️ Worked Example a — Minutes to hours and minutes
Q: Convert 145 minutes to hours and minutes.
Divide by 60: 145 ÷ 60 = 2 remainder 25
Quotient 2 = hours. Remainder 25 = minutes.
✅ 145 minutes = 2 hours 25 minutes
✏️ Worked Example b — Hours and minutes to minutes
Q: Convert 2 hours 40 minutes to minutes.
2 hours × 60 = 120 minutes
120 + 40 = 160 minutes
✅ 2 hours 40 minutes = 160 minutes
✏️ Worked Example c — Hours to seconds
Q: Convert 3 hours to seconds.
3 hours × 60 = 180 minutes
180 minutes × 60 = 10,800 seconds
✅ 3 hours = 10,800 seconds
💡 Decimal Time — Essential for Speed Problems

When using the Speed/Distance/Time formula, time must be expressed as a decimal number of hours.

1 hr 30 min = 1 + (30÷60) = 1 + 0.5 = 1.5 hours
1 hr 45 min = 1 + (45÷60) = 1 + 0.75 = 1.75 hours
1 hr 20 min = 1 + (20÷60) = 1 + 0.333... ≈ 1.333 hours
2 hr 15 min = 2 + (15÷60) = 2 + 0.25 = 2.25 hours
⚠️ Most Dangerous Trap — Decimal Time

1 hour 30 minutes ≠ 1.30 hours. It equals 1.5 hours. If you use 1.30 in an SDT formula, you will get a completely wrong answer. Always convert: minutes ÷ 60.

3Finding a Time Interval — The Jump Method

To find the gap between two times, never subtract like decimals. Use the Jump Method: break the journey into neat steps.

📐 The Jump Method — Three Steps
Step 1: Jump to the next whole hour
Step 2: Jump forward in whole hours to the target hour
Step 3: Jump to the exact final minutes
Step 4: Add all jumps together
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Daytime: 08:15 to 11:40
08:15 → 09:00 = 45 minutes
09:00 → 11:00 = 2 hours
11:00 → 11:40 = 40 minutes
Total: 2 hours + 45 min + 40 min = 2 hours + 85 min = 2 hours + 1 hour 25 min
✅ Total journey = 3 hours 25 minutes
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Crossing Noon: 10:45 to 14:20
10:45 → 11:00 = 15 minutes
11:00 → 14:00 = 3 hours
14:00 → 14:20 = 20 minutes
Total: 3 hours + 15 + 20 minutes = 3 hours 35 minutes
✅ Total interval = 3 hours 35 minutes
✏️ Worked Example 3 — CROSSING MIDNIGHT: Flight departs 22:40, lands 01:15
22:40 → 23:00 = 20 minutes
23:00 → 00:00 (midnight) = 1 hour
00:00 → 01:00 = 1 hour
01:00 → 01:15 = 15 minutes
Total: 1 + 1 hours + 20 + 15 minutes = 2 hours 35 minutes
✅ Flight duration = 2 hours 35 minutes
⚠️ Never Subtract Time Like Decimals

11:40 − 08:15 looks like 3:25 if you subtract digit by digit. But 3 hours 25 minutes ≠ correct answer above (which was 3 hours 25 minutes — coincidence!). Try 14:20 − 10:45: decimal subtraction gives 3:75 which is meaningless. Always use the Jump Method.

4Adding a Duration to a Start Time

This is a different question type from finding an interval. Here you are given a start time and a duration, and must find the end time.

📐 Method — Add Hours, Then Add Minutes (with Carrying)
Step 1: Add the hours part of the duration to the start time
Step 2: Add the minutes. If total minutes ≥ 60, carry 1 hour and subtract 60.
Step 3: Check if you cross midnight (00:00) or noon in a 12-hr context.
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Film ends: 7:35 pm + 1 hour 55 minutes
Q: A film starts at 7:35 pm and lasts 1 hour 55 minutes. When does it end?
Step 1: Add hours: 7 pm + 1 hour = 8 pm (so far: 8:35 pm)
Step 2: Add minutes: 35 + 55 = 90 minutes
Step 3: 90 minutes = 1 hour 30 minutes → carry 1 hour
8 pm + 1 hour (carried) = 9 pm. Remaining minutes = 30.
✅ Film ends at 9:30 pm
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Bus arrival: 13:47 + 2 hours 38 minutes
Q: A bus departs at 13:47 and takes 2 hours 38 minutes. Arrival time?
Step 1: Add hours: 13 + 2 = 15 (so far: 15:47)
Step 2: Add minutes: 47 + 38 = 85 minutes
Step 3: 85 minutes = 1 hour 25 minutes → carry 1 hour
15 + 1 = 16. Remaining minutes = 25.
✅ Arrival time = 16:25
✏️ Worked Example 3 — Crossing Midnight: 23:15 + 1 hour 50 minutes
Q: An event starts at 23:15 and lasts 1 hour 50 minutes. When does it end?
Step 1: Add hours: 23 + 1 = 24 → but 24:xx doesn't exist → 00:15 (next day)
Step 2: Add minutes: 15 + 50 = 65 minutes
Step 3: 65 minutes = 1 hour 5 minutes → carry 1 hour
00 + 1 (carried) = 01. Remaining minutes = 5.
✅ Event ends at 01:05 (next day)
⚠️ The 8:90 pm Mistake

Writing 7:35 + 1:55 = 8:90 pm is a very common error. There is no such time as 8:90 pm. Always convert any minute total over 59 by dividing by 60 and carrying to hours.

Timetables show departure times at each stop for multiple services. You read across rows (one service) and down columns (one stop).

🅒 Cromwell09:0510:0511:2012:35

✏️ Type A — Duration: "How long is Service 1 from Ashford to Cromwell?"
Read Service 1 column: Ashford 08:10, Cromwell 09:05
Jump Method: 08:10 → 09:00 = 50 minutes
09:00 → 09:05 = 5 minutes
Total: 50 + 5 = 55 minutes
✅ Service 1 takes 55 minutes from Ashford to Cromwell.
✏️ Type B — Arrival Planning: "Latest service to arrive at Dalford by 11:30?"
Look at Dalford arrival times: 09:45 / 10:45 / 12:00 / 13:15
Need to arrive BY 11:30 → must arrive at or before 11:30
09:45 ✅ · 10:45 ✅ · 12:00 ✗ (too late) · 13:15 ✗ (too late)
Latest acceptable: Service 2 (arrives 10:45)
✅ Take Service 2, departing Ashford at 09:10.
✏️ Type C — Wait Time: "James arrives at Branton at 09:40. How long until the next service?"
Branton times: 08:32 / 09:32 / 10:47 / 12:02
James arrives 09:40. Service at 09:32 has already gone.
Next service: 10:47
Wait: 09:40 → 10:00 = 20 min. 10:00 → 10:47 = 47 min. Total = 67 minutes
✅ James waits 67 minutes (1 hour 7 minutes).
⚠️ Common Trap — Departure vs Duration

The timetable shows departure times, not journey durations. "09:10" at Ashford means the bus leaves Ashford at 09:10 — it does not mean the journey takes 9 hours 10 minutes. Always use the Jump Method to find duration from two times.

6Speed, Distance and Time

The SDT formula connects three quantities. Cover the one you want to find and the triangle shows you the operation.

D
S
T
D = S × T  ·  S = D ÷ T  ·  T = D ÷ S
⚠️ Units Must Match! Always Convert First.

If speed is in km/h, time must be in hours. If speed is in m/s, time must be in seconds. Always convert time to decimal hours before substituting into the formula. 1 hour 30 minutes = 1.5 hours, NOT 1.30 hours.

✏️ Worked Example a — Find Distance
Q: A train travels at 80 km/h for 2.5 hours. How far does it travel?
Formula: D = S × T
D = 80 × 2.5
D = 200 km
✅ Distance = 200 km
✏️ Worked Example b — Find Speed (with time conversion)
Q: A car travels 90 km in 1 hour 30 minutes. What is its speed?
Convert time first: 1 hour 30 minutes = 1 + 30/60 = 1.5 hours
Formula: S = D ÷ T
S = 90 ÷ 1.5
S = 60 km/h
✅ Speed = 60 km/h
✏️ Worked Example c — Find Time (convert answer to hours and minutes)
Q: How long does it take to travel 150 km at 60 km/h?
Formula: T = D ÷ S
T = 150 ÷ 60 = 2.5 hours
Convert: 0.5 hours = 0.5 × 60 = 30 minutes
✅ Time = 2 hours 30 minutes
✏️ Worked Example d — Multi-step: Which journey is faster?
Q: Journey A: 120 km in 1.5 hours. Journey B: 200 km in 2.5 hours. Which is faster?
Speed A = 120 ÷ 1.5 = 80 km/h
Speed B = 200 ÷ 2.5 = 80 km/h
Compare: 80 = 80
✅ Both journeys travel at the same speed: 80 km/h.

7Calendar Arithmetic
📐 Days in Months — Essential Facts
30 days: September, April, June, November ("SAJN" or knuckle trick)
31 days: January, March, May, July, August, October, December
February: 28 days normally; 29 days in a leap year
Leap year: divisible by 4 → 2024, 2028, 2032 ...
✏️ Worked Example a — Counting days across months
Q: How many days from 14th March to 5th May (non-leap year)?
Remaining in March: 31 − 14 = 17 days
All of April: 30 days
Days in May (up to but NOT including 5th): 4 days
Total: 17 + 30 + 4 = 51 days
✅ 51 days from 14th March to 5th May
✏️ Worked Example b — Finding an end date
Q: A project starts on 20th November and lasts 75 days. What date does it end?
Remaining in November: 30 − 20 = 10 days. Running total: 10
All of December: 31 days. Running total: 10 + 31 = 41
Need 75 − 41 = 34 more days into January: but January only has 31 days.
After January: 41 + 31 = 72. Need 75 − 72 = 3 more days into February.
End date: 3rd February
✅ Project ends on 3rd February
✏️ Worked Example c — Day of the week cycling
Q: Today is Monday. What day is it 100 days from now?
Days cycle every 7. So find the remainder: 100 ÷ 7 = 14 remainder 2
2 days after Monday: Tuesday, Wednesday
✅ 100 days from Monday = Wednesday
⚠️ Off-by-One Trap — Inclusive vs Exclusive Counting

From March 14 to March 20: is that 6 days or 7? If you're counting the gap (intervals between dates), it's 6 days. If you're counting dates including both endpoints, it's 7. GL questions almost always mean the gap (exclusive counting). "How many days from the 1st to the 5th?" = 4 days, not 5.

8Common Pitfalls — Consolidated Warning List
  • ⚠️
    Decimal subtraction for time intervals: Never compute 14:20 − 10:45 = 3:75. This is meaningless. Always use the Jump Method — it is the only safe approach.
  • ⚠️
    12 pm / 12 am confusion: 12 pm = noon = 12:00 (do not add 12 → 24:00). 12 am = midnight = 00:00. 12:30 pm = 12:30. 12:30 am = 00:30. Memorise both.
  • ⚠️
    Minutes carrying (8:90 mistake): If you add minutes and get more than 59, you must divide by 60 and carry to hours. 35 + 55 = 90 minutes = 1 hour 30 minutes. Never write 8:90 pm.
  • ⚠️
    Decimal time in SDT formula: 1 hour 30 minutes = 1.5 hours. NOT 1.30 hours. Using 1.30 gives a completely wrong speed or distance. Always convert minutes by dividing by 60.
  • ⚠️
    Off-by-one in calendar counting: From 14th March to 14th March is 0 days, not 1. Count the gap between dates, not the dates themselves. "How many days from 1st to 5th?" = 4 days.
  • ⚠️
    Forgetting to convert before SDT: If the question gives time in minutes and speed in km/h, convert minutes to hours first. 90 minutes = 1.5 hours. Substituting 90 directly gives an answer 60 times too large.
