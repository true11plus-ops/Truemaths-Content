1. The Place Value Chart

Every digit in a number has two things: a face value (the digit itself) and a place value (what it is worth because of its position). Move one column to the left and the value is ten times bigger. Move one column to the right and it is ten times smaller.

Key Rule

Each column is 10 times the value of the column to its right. The decimal point separates whole numbers from parts of a number.

Millions Hundred Thousands Ten Thousands Thousands Hundreds Tens Ones / Units • Tenths Hundredths Thousandths
1,000,000 100,000 10,000 1,000 100 10 1 . 1/10 1/100 1/1000
4728395 . 602

In the number 4,728,395.602:

  • The digit 4 is in the millions column → worth 4,000,000
  • The digit 7 is in the hundred-thousands column → worth 700,000
  • The digit 9 is in the tens column → worth 90
  • The digit 6 is in the tenths column → worth 0.6
  • The digit 2 is in the thousandths column → worth 0.002

Tip — Decimals

Tenths are bigger than hundredths (1/10 > 1/100). Think of sharing a pizza: one slice out of 10 is bigger than one slice out of 100.

2. Reading and Writing Large Numbers

In the UK, we use commas to separate every group of three digits, counting from the right. This makes large numbers much easier to read.

Key Rule

Group digits in threes from the right: Millions | Thousands | Ones. Write commas between groups. Say each group's number then its group name.

MillionsHundred-ThousandsTen-Thousands ThousandsHundredsTensOnes
————352
———4007

3040050
  • 352 → three hundred and fifty-two
  • 4,007 → four thousand and seven
  • 285,631 → two hundred and eighty-five thousand, six hundred and thirty-one
  • 3,040,050 → three million, forty thousand and fifty

Tip — zeros as placeholders

Never drop zeros in the middle of a number. 3,040,050 has three zeros that act as placeholders. Without them, the other digits would shift into wrong columns.

Worked example 3

Write "five million, two hundred and six thousand, and forty" in digits.

1
Identify the parts: 5 million + 206 thousand + 40.
2
Millions group: 5. Thousands group: 206. Ones group: 040 (we must use 3 digits, so add a leading zero).
3
Combine with commas: 5,206,040.
Answer: 5,206,040

Worked example 4

Write 2,070,305 in words.

1
Split into groups: 2 | 070 | 305
2
Millions: two million. Thousands: seventy thousand. Ones: three hundred and five.
3
Join: two million, seventy thousand, three hundred and five.
Answer: Two million, seventy thousand, three hundred and five.

Common mistake

Forgetting the word "and" before the last part (e.g. "three hundred and five"). Also watch out for numbers like 4,007 — say "four thousand and seven", not "four thousand seven hundred".

3. Ordering and Comparing Numbers

To compare numbers we use the symbols < (less than), > (greater than) and = (equal to). We always compare the highest place value column first and work right.

Key Rule — Comparing decimals

Line up the decimal points. Compare digit by digit from left to right. Do not count the number of digits after the decimal point — 0.3 is bigger than 0.29 because 3 tenths > 2 tenths.

Consider ordering: 0.3   0.29   0.305

Tip — Pad with zeros

Add trailing zeros to make all numbers the same length, then compare like whole numbers:
0.300   0.290   0.305 → compare 300, 290, 305 → order: 290, 300, 305.

Worked example 5

Put these numbers in ascending order (smallest first):  3.07   3.7   3.071   3.1

1
Pad to 3 decimal places: 3.070   3.700   3.071   3.100
2
Ones all the same (3). Compare tenths: 0, 7, 0, 1 → order tenths: 0, 0, 1, 7.
3
Two numbers have 0 tenths (3.070 and 3.071). Compare hundredths: both have 7. Compare thousandths: 0 vs 1 → 3.070 < 3.071.
Answer: 3.07, 3.071, 3.1, 3.7

Worked example 6

Insert < or > between 406,219 and 406,912.

1
Both numbers have 4 in the hundred-thousands and 0 in the ten-thousands and 6 in the thousands. Move to hundreds.
2
First number: hundreds digit is 2. Second number: hundreds digit is 9.
3
2 < 9, so the first number is smaller.
Answer: 406,219 < 406,912

Common mistake

Thinking 0.29 > 0.3 because 29 > 3. Remember: compare column by column, not by the length of the number. The more decimal digits a number has does not make it bigger.

4. Multiplying and Dividing by Powers of 10

When you multiply or divide by 10, 100 or 1000, the digits themselves stay the same — they just shift their position in the place value chart.

Key Rule — the digit shift

× 10 → digits shift one place to the left (number gets bigger)
× 100 → digits shift two places to the left
× 1000 → digits shift three places to the left
÷ 10, ÷ 100, ÷ 1000 → digits shift right by 1, 2 or 3 places (number gets smaller)

5.6 × 10 shift left 1 = 56
5.6 × 100 shift left 2 = 560
5.6 × 1000 shift left 3 = 5,600
3,400 ÷ 10 shift right 1 = 340
3,400 ÷ 100 shift right 2 = 34
3,400 ÷ 1,000 shift right 3 = 3.4

Tip — it's the digits that move, not the decimal point

Think of the decimal point as fixed on the page. The digits slide left or right past it. In 3,400 ÷ 1000: the digits 3–4–0–0 all shift three places right, so 3 ends up in the ones column and 4 in the tenths → 3.4.

Worked example 7

Calculate 0.47 × 1,000.

1
Multiplying by 1,000 means shift all digits 3 places to the left.
2
0.47 has 4 in the tenths and 7 in the hundredths. After shifting 3 left: 4 goes to the hundreds column, 7 to the tens column.
3
Result: 470. (Add a zero to fill the empty ones column.)
Answer: 0.47 × 1,000 = 470

Worked example 8

A factory makes 235 screws per minute. It runs for 100 minutes. How many screws is that? Then, 10 screws are packed in each box — how many boxes are needed?

1
Screws made: 235 × 100 → shift left 2 → 23,500 screws.
2
Boxes needed: 23,500 ÷ 10 → shift right 1 → 2,350 boxes.
Answer: 23,500 screws; 2,350 boxes needed.

Common mistake

"Adding zeros" works for whole numbers (35 × 10 = 350) but fails for decimals (3.5 × 10 ≠ 3.50 — it equals 35). Always use the digit-shift method.

5. Rounding Numbers

Rounding makes numbers simpler while keeping them close to their exact value. GL Assessment tests rounding to the nearest 10, 100, 1,000 and to decimal places.

The Rounding Rule

Look at the digit immediately to the right of the column you are rounding to.
• If it is 5 or more → round up
• If it is 4 or less → round down (keep the digit the same)

Boundary case — exactly halfway

When a number is exactly on the halfway point (e.g. 250 when rounding to the nearest 100, or 3,500 when rounding to the nearest 1,000), the rule is always to round up.

So: 250 → 300  |  3,500 → 4,000  |  0.45 to 1 d.p. → 0.5

Worked example 9

Round 47,836 to the nearest 1,000.

1
Identify the thousands digit: 7 (in 47,836).
2
Look at the digit to its right (hundreds): 8.
3
8 ≥ 5 → round up. The thousands digit goes from 7 to 8. Replace all digits to the right with zeros.
Answer: 47,836 rounded to nearest 1,000 = 48,000

Worked example 10

Round 6.349 to 2 decimal places.

1
Rounding to 2 d.p. means we keep two digits after the decimal point. The second decimal place is the hundredths column: digit is 4.
2
Look at the digit to the right (thousandths): 9.
3
9 ≥ 5 → round up. Hundredths digit goes from 4 to 5. Drop the thousandths digit.
Answer: 6.349 rounded to 2 d.p. = 6.35

Worked example 11 — estimation

Estimate 4,871 + 3,214 by rounding each number to the nearest 1,000.

1
Round 4,871: hundreds digit is 8 ≥ 5 → round up → 5,000.
2
Round 3,214: hundreds digit is 2 < 5 → round down → 3,000.
3
Estimate: 5,000 + 3,000 = 8,000. (Exact answer: 8,085 — very close!)
Answer: Estimated answer = 8,000

Common mistake — chain rounding

Always round from the original number. Do not round 3,461 to the nearest 10 first (3,460) and then use that to round to the nearest 100 (3,500 — wrong!). The correct answer from the original is 3,500... wait — 3,461 → hundreds digit 4 → round down → 3,400. Chain rounding gives the wrong answer.

Practice Questions — GL Assessment Style

Question 1 — Digit identification

Which digit is in the ten-thousands place of 472,839?

A)  4
B)  7
C)  2
D)  8

Question 2 — Place value

What is the value of the digit 6 in the number 3,265,104?

A)  6,000
B)  600
C)  60,000
D)  6,000,000

Question 3 — Missing digit

Find the missing digit:   7 ☐ 4,500 rounded to the nearest 10,000 is 760,000. What is the missing digit?

Question 4 — Ordering decimals

Put the following numbers in descending order (largest first):
0.52    0.5    0.519    0.6    0.509

Question 5 — Powers of 10

A scientist measures a cell as 0.035 mm wide. How wide is it in micrometres, given that 1 mm = 1,000 micrometres?

Question 6 — Rounding (boundary case)

A school tuck shop sold 1,250 items in a term. A pupil rounds this to the nearest 100 and says the answer is 1,200. Are they correct? Give a reason.

Question 7 — Word problem (place value in context)

A charity raises £304,072. The local newspaper rounds this amount to the nearest thousand for a headline. What figure should the headline show?

A)  £300,000
B)  £304,000
C)  £305,000
D)  £310,000

Question 8 — Missing digit problem

The number below has one digit hidden. The number rounded to the nearest 100 is 5,700 and rounded to the nearest 1,000 is 6,000.

5, ☐ 8 3

What is the hidden digit?

Question 9 — Multiply/divide by powers of 10

A bottle of juice holds 1.25 litres. A factory fills 10,000 bottles. How many litres of juice does the factory use in total?

Question 10 — Reading and writing / comparison

Match each number in words to its digit form. Then write them in ascending order.

(a) Four million and fifty    (b) Four hundred and five thousand    (c) Forty thousand and five

Q1 — Answer: B) 7
472,839: count from right — ones(9), tens(3), hundreds(8), thousands(2), ten-thousands(7). The ten-thousands digit is 7.
Q2 — Answer: C) 60,000
3,265,104: the 6 is in the ten-thousands column (3 | 265 | 104). 6 × 10,000 = 60,000.
Q3 — Answer: 5
The number is 7☐4,500. Rounded to nearest 10,000 → 760,000. The ten-thousands digit must be 5 or 6. For the round-up to give 760,000, the ten-thousands digit is 5 → 5 ≥ 5 → round up → 754,500 → 760,000. Missing digit = 5.
Q4 — Descending order
Pad: 0.600, 0.520, 0.519, 0.509, 0.500. Order largest first: 0.6, 0.52, 0.519, 0.509, 0.5.
Q5 — Answer: 35 micrometres
0.035 × 1,000 → shift digits left 3 places → 35 micrometres.
Q6 — Answer: No, it is 1,300
1,250 is exactly halfway between 1,200 and 1,300. The rounding rule says round up when exactly halfway. So 1,250 → nearest 100 = 1,300. The pupil is wrong.
Q7 — Answer: B) £304,000
£304,072: thousands digit is 4. Look at hundreds digit: 0 < 5 → round down. Result: £304,000.
Q8 — Answer: 6
The number is 5,☐83. Rounded to nearest 100 = 5,700 → hundreds digit rounds to 7. That means the real hundreds digit is 6 or 7. Rounded to nearest 1,000 = 6,000 → thousands digit rounds up → must be ≥ 5. If ☐ = 6: 5,683 → nearest 100 = 5,700 ✓; nearest 1,000 = 6,000 ✓. Missing digit = 6.
Q9 — Answer: 12,500 litres
1.25 × 10,000 → shift left 4 places → 12,500 litres.
Q10 — Ascending order
(a) 4,000,050   (b) 405,000   (c) 40,005.
Ascending: 40,005 → 405,000 → 4,000,050.
