Factors — Finding & Counting

A factor of a number divides into it exactly, leaving no remainder. Factors always come in pairs and are always equal to or smaller than the original number.

Prime Numbers — Definition & Testing

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. A number with more than two factors is called composite.
⚠

1 is NOT prime. It has only one factor (itself), not two. 0 is NOT prime either — it has infinitely many factors.

2 is the only even prime. Every other even number has 2 as a factor, giving it at least three factors (1, 2, and itself), making it composite.

Method: Testing if a number is prime (trial division up to √n)
  1. Estimate √n. (e.g. for 97: √97 ≈ 9.8, so test primes up to 9.)
  2. Try dividing by each prime in order: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, …
  3. If none divide exactly, the number is prime.
  4. You only need to test primes (not all numbers) — if 4 doesn't divide it, neither does 8, etc.

Ex 2 Is 91 prime?
Test whether 91 is a prime number. (A common exam trap!)
1
√91 ≈ 9.5 → test 2, 3, 5, 7.
2

⚠

91 = 7 × 13. This is a classic GL trap — it looks prime but isn't. Always test up to √n.

Using Prime Factorisation to find HCF and LCM — the Venn Method

Write the prime factorisations of both numbers. Draw a Venn diagram: shared primes go in the overlap; primes unique to each number go in the outer sections.

HCF = product of primes in the overlap only.
LCM = product of all primes in the entire diagram (overlap + both outer sections).

HCF & LCM — Two & Three Numbers

Highest Common Factor (HCF) — the largest number that divides exactly into all of the given numbers.

Use for: "largest equal groups", "longest equal pieces", "biggest square tiles".
Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) — the smallest number that all of the given numbers divide into exactly.

Use for: "when do events next coincide", "bus timetables", "shared timing".
Ex 1 HCF and LCM of two numbers — listing method
Find HCF(18, 30) and LCM(18, 30) by listing.
1
Factors of 18: {1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18}  |  Factors of 30: {1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 10, 15, 30}
2
Common factors: {1, 2, 3, 6}. Largest = 6.
3
Multiples of 18: 18, 36, 54, 90, …  |  Multiples of 30: 30, 60, 90, …
4
First common multiple = 90.
HCF = 6
LCM = 90
Ex 2 HCF and LCM of two numbers — Venn method
Find HCF(24, 40) and LCM(24, 40) using prime factorisation.
1
24 = 2³ × 3  |  40 = 2³ × 5
2
Shared: 2³ (three 2s).  Unique to 24: 3.  Unique to 40: 5.
3
HCF = overlap = 2³ = 8
4
LCM = all = 3 × 2³ × 5 = 3 × 8 × 5 = 120
HCF(24, 40) = 8
LCM(24, 40) = 120
Ex 3 HCF and LCM of THREE numbers
Find HCF(12, 18, 24) and LCM(12, 18, 24).
1
12 = 2² × 3  |  18 = 2 × 3²  |  24 = 2³ × 3
2
HCF — take the lowest power of each prime that appears in ALL three: 2¹ (lowest of 2, 1, 3) and 3¹ (lowest of 1, 2, 1).
3
HCF = 2¹ × 3¹ = 6
4
LCM — take the highest power of each prime that appears in ANY one: 2³ and 3².
5
LCM = 2³ × 3² = 8 × 9 = 72
★

Three-number rule: HCF → lowest power of shared primes. LCM → highest power of any prime across all numbers.

HCF(12, 18, 24) = 6
LCM(12, 18, 24) = 72
Ex 4 HCF and LCM of THREE numbers — larger values
Find HCF(30, 42, 66) and LCM(30, 42, 66).
1
30 = 2 × 3 × 5  |  42 = 2 × 3 × 7  |  66 = 2 × 3 × 11
2
Primes in all three: 2¹ and 3¹. HCF = 2 × 3 = 6.
3
All primes anywhere: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11. LCM = 2 × 3 × 5 × 7 × 11 = 2310.
HCF = 6
LCM = 2310

Word Problems — HCF & LCM in Context

Spotting whether to use HCF or LCM is the key skill.
Use HCF when you're dividing or splitting into equal groups — the answer gets smaller.
Use LCM when events or cycles repeat and you want when they next coincide — the answer gets larger.
HCF Word Problem 1
A ribbon 96 cm long is cut into equal pieces with no ribbon wasted. A second ribbon 60 cm long is also cut into equal pieces the same length as the first. What is the longest possible length of each piece?
1
"Equal pieces, same length, no waste" → HCF problem.
2
96 = 2⁵ × 3  |  60 = 2² × 3 × 5
3
Shared: 2² and 3. HCF = 4 × 3 = 12.
4
Check: 96 ÷ 12 = 8 pieces ✓  |  60 ÷ 12 = 5 pieces ✓
Longest piece = 12 cm
HCF Word Problem 2
A hall floor is 560 cm by 420 cm. It will be covered with identical square tiles with no cutting. What is the largest possible side length of each tile?
1
"Largest square tiles that fit exactly" → HCF of the two dimensions.
2
560 = 2⁴ × 5 × 7  |  420 = 2² × 3 × 5 × 7
3
Shared: 2², 5, 7. HCF = 4 × 5 × 7 = 140.
Largest tile = 140 cm × 140 cm
LCM Word Problem 1
Bus A leaves the station every 8 minutes. Bus B leaves every 12 minutes. They both leave at 9:00 am. When is the next time they both leave at the same time?
1
"When do they next coincide" → LCM problem.
2
8 = 2³  |  12 = 2² × 3
3
LCM = 2³ × 3 = 24 minutes.
4
9:00 am + 24 minutes = 9:24 am.
Next coincidence: 9:24 am
LCM Word Problem 2
Three warning lights flash at different intervals: Light A every 6 seconds, Light B every 8 seconds, Light C every 10 seconds. They all flash together at the start. After how many seconds do they next all flash together?
1
"Three repeating events — when do they all coincide?" → LCM of 6, 8, and 10.
2
6 = 2 × 3  |  8 = 2³  |  10 = 2 × 5
3
Highest powers: 2³, 3¹, 5¹. LCM = 8 × 3 × 5 = 120.
Next coincidence: after 120 seconds
HCF Word Problem 3 (three numbers)
Three pieces of rope measure 48 m, 60 m, and 72 m. They are to be cut into pieces all the same length, with no rope left over. What is the longest possible length of each piece?
1
HCF(48, 60, 72).
2
48 = 2⁴ × 3  |  60 = 2² × 3 × 5  |  72 = 2³ × 3²
3
Shared in all three: 2² and 3¹. HCF = 4 × 3 = 12.
Longest piece = 12 m

Targeted Exam Tips

⚡
Use divisibility rules before dividing

When factorising a number, run through the divisibility rules in order (2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9) before doing any long division. This tells you immediately which prime factors to try first and avoids wasted calculations.

🌳
Factor tree: always circle your primes

Circle each prime as you reach it so you don't accidentally stop early. A circled number = done. An uncircled number = keep splitting. Your answer is the product of all circled numbers.

🔵
Listing vs Venn: know when to use each

Use listing for small numbers (under 30) where it's fast. Use the Venn / prime factorisation method for larger numbers — it's reliable and impresses examiners. Never try listing multiples for LCM when the numbers are above 20.

⚠
The square number trap — odd factor count

If a question asks "how many factors does X have?" and the answer is odd, then X must be a square number. Conversely, if you're asked to find a number with exactly 7 factors, look for a sixth power of a prime (e.g. 2⁶ = 64). This pattern is a favourite GL trick.

🎯
HCF or LCM? — the size clue

HCF is always ≤ the smallest number given. LCM is always ≥ the largest number given. If your answer to an HCF question is larger than one of the original numbers, you've made an error. Similarly, if your LCM is smaller than the largest number, check again.

🔢
Three-number HCF/LCM — use exponents carefully

HCF: take the lowest exponent of each prime that appears in ALL numbers. LCM: take the highest exponent of each prime that appears in ANY number. Write the factorisations in a column and compare row by row.

🧪
Testing large primes: always go to √n

√100 = 10, √121 = 11, √144 = 12. Know these. If you need to test whether 143 is prime, √143 ≈ 12, so test primes up to 12: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11. (143 = 11 × 13 — not prime.)

📋
Word problems: underline the key phrase

"largest equal pieces / tiles / groups" → HCF. "when do they meet / coincide / next happen together" → LCM. Underline that phrase in the question and write HCF or LCM next to it before calculating.
