Foundations: Powers & Notation

📖
Key Vocabulary A power (or index) tells you how many times to multiply a number by itself.
The base is the main number. The exponent (or index) is the small raised number.
53  =  5 × 5 × 5  =  125
base = 5  ·  exponent = 3  ·  value = 125

Special Power Rules

💡
Power of 1 Any number to the power of 1 equals itself.
7¹ = 7   100¹ = 100
💡
Power of 0 Any number (except 0) to the power of 0 equals 1.
7⁰ = 1   999⁰ = 1

Powers of 10 & Place Value

Each step up multiplies by 10 — this maps directly onto our place value system.

PowerCalculationValuePlace value name

10³10 × 10 × 101,000Thousands
10⁴10 × 10 × 10 × 1010,000Ten-thousands

🌟
Top-End Extension: Negative Powers of 10 A negative exponent means divide by 10 for each step. So:
10⁻¹ = 1/10 = 0.1 (tenths)  ·  10⁻² = 1/100 = 0.01 (hundredths)
These appear in the hardest GL papers and science contexts.

Index Laws — The Most Tested Rules

🎯
Why Index Laws Matter GL Assessment frequently presents expressions like 3⁵ × 3² = ? or (2³)⁴ = ?. You must be able to simplify these without a calculator. The three laws below cover every case the 11+ will throw at you.
1
Multiplying Powers (Same Base)
am × an  =  am + n
ADD the exponents
✏ Worked Example

3⁵ × 3²

Same base (3), so add the exponents: 5 + 2 = 7

= 3⁷ = 2187

Why? 3⁵ = 3×3×3×3×3 and 3² = 3×3. Multiplied together that's seven 3s.

⚠️
Common Mistake 3⁵ × 3² ≠ 3¹⁰ (don't multiply the exponents here — that's Law 3). Addition only: 3⁵ × 3² = 3⁷.
🎯
Exam Trap This law only works when the base is the same. 3⁵ × 4² cannot be simplified using this law — different bases!
2
Dividing Powers (Same Base)
am ÷ an  =  am − n
SUBTRACT the exponents
✏ Worked Example

2⁸ ÷ 2³

Same base (2), so subtract the exponents: 8 − 3 = 5

= 2⁵ = 32

✏ Special Case — Result is 1

5⁴ ÷ 5⁴ = 5⁰ = 1

This is why anything to the power of zero equals 1 — it's just a division law!

⚠️
Common Mistake 2⁸ ÷ 2³ ≠ 2⁵ ÷ 2 = ? — don't divide the exponents. Always subtract: 2⁸ ÷ 2³ = 2⁵.
3
Power of a Power
(am)n  =  am × n
MULTIPLY the exponents
✏ Worked Example

(2³)⁴

A power raised to another power — multiply the exponents: 3 × 4 = 12

= 2¹² = 4096

Why? (2³)⁴ means (2³) × (2³) × (2³) × (2³) — four groups of three 2s = twelve 2s.

⚠️
Common Mistake (2³)⁴ ≠ 2⁷ (don't add — you're not multiplying two separate terms). It's a power of a power: multiply → 2¹².

Quick Reference Summary

SituationRuleMemory hook
aᵐ × aⁿaᵐ⁺ⁿMultiply → Add exponents
aᵐ ÷ aⁿaᵐ⁻ⁿDivide → Subtract exponents
(aᵐ)ⁿaᵐⁿPower of power → Multiply exponents

Square Numbers

A square number is the result of multiplying an integer by itself: n × n = n². Geometrically, it represents the area of a square with side length n.

⚡
Exam Critical: Instant Recall Required GL papers are timed. Not knowing these immediately costs you marks on sequence, geometry, and estimation questions. Memorise 1² to 12² cold — then the bonuses.

Must-Know List: 1² to 12²

1² = 1
2² = 4
3² = 9
4² = 16
5² = 25
6² = 36
7² = 49
8² = 64
9² = 81
10² = 100
11² = 121
12² = 144

Bonus List: 13² to 15²

13² = 169
14² = 196
15² = 225

Geometric Connection

If a square has side length s, its area = s². Conversely, if you know the area, the side length = √area.

🎯 Pythagorean Triple Awareness

🎯
GL Trap: Pythagorean Triples These are sets of three integers where a² + b² = c². GL papers use them to test whether you recognise a right triangle without calculation. Memorise both:

3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5² → triple: 3, 4, 5
5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13² → triple: 5, 12, 13

Multiples also work: 6, 8, 10 (= 2 × 3,4,5) and 10, 24, 26 (= 2 × 5,12,13).

Square Roots

📖
Definition The square root of a number asks: "What number, multiplied by itself, gives this result?"
Symbol: √    Example: √49 = 7, because 7 × 7 = 49.

The ± Convention

Technically, both +7 and −7 square to give 49, because (−7) × (−7) = 49 as well.

🌟
When GL Asks for "All Possible Values" If a question says "find all possible values of x if x² = 49", the answer is x = 7 or x = −7 (written as x = ±7). If it just asks for the square root in a length/area context, always give the positive value only — lengths cannot be negative.

GL Context: Area → Perimeter

✏ Worked Example

"A square garden has an area of 81 m². What is its perimeter?"

  1. Find side length: √81 = 9 m
  2. Perimeter = 4 × side = 4 × 9 = 36 m

Estimation — Perfect Squares

If the number is a perfect square you don't recognise immediately, use this 3-step method:

✏ Worked Example — √729
  1. Bracket it: 20² = 400 and 30² = 900, so √729 is between 20 and 30.
  2. Last digit test: 729 ends in 9. Which squares end in 9? Only 3² and 7². So the answer ends in 3 or 7.
  3. Test both: 23² = 529 (too small). 27² = 729 ✓

√729 = 27

Estimation — Non-Perfect Squares (Most Common GL Ask)

GL more often asks: "between which two consecutive integers does √n lie?" This is a different skill.

✏ Worked Example — Estimate √50
  1. Find the two perfect squares either side of 50:
    7² = 49 (just below 50) and 8² = 64 (above 50)
  2. So √50 lies between 7 and 8.
  3. Since 50 is very close to 49, √50 is just slightly above 7 — approximately 7.07.
7
7² = 49
√50 ≈ here
8
8² = 64
💡
Strategy: Always check the squares either side For any √n, find the largest perfect square below n and the smallest above n. The square root lies between those two integers. GL usually only asks for this — not the decimal.
✏ Another Example — Between which integers does √130 lie?

11² = 121 and 12² = 144

121 < 130 < 144, so √130 lies between 11 and 12.

Cube Numbers & Cube Roots

📖
Definition A cube number is n × n × n = n³. Geometrically, it's the volume of a cube with side n.
Cube numbers are less common than squares in 11+ papers, but they are directly testable — especially cube root recognition.

Must-Know Cube Numbers: 1³ to 5³

1³ = 1
1×1×1
2³ = 8
2×2×2
3³ = 27
3×3×3
4³ = 64
4×4×4
5³ = 125
5×5×5
⚠️
Watch Out: 64 appears twice! 8² = 64 AND 4³ = 64. GL setters exploit this. Read the question carefully to know whether square or cube is intended.

Cube Roots: The ∛ Symbol

The cube root is the inverse (opposite) of cubing. It asks: "What number, multiplied by itself three times, gives this result?"

∛27  =  3    because    3 × 3 × 3  =  27

Must-Know Cube Roots

∛1 = 1
∛8 = 2
∛27 = 3
∛64 = 4
∛125 = 5
💡
Unlike square roots, cube roots of positive numbers are always positive. There is no ± issue — only one value works. (Cube roots of negative numbers exist but are not tested at 11+.)

Sequence Type Taxonomy

Powers appear frequently in number sequences. The key skill is recognising the pattern type before trying to find the next term.

Sequence typeExamplePattern rule
Square numbers 1, 4, 9, 16, 25… nth term = n²
Cube numbers 1, 8, 27, 64, 125… nth term = n³
Triangular numbers 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21… Add 1, then 2, then 3… (differences increase by 1)
Modified: n² + 1 2, 5, 10, 17, 26… Each term = n² + 1
Modified: n² − n 0, 2, 6, 12, 20… Each term = n² − n = n(n−1)
Powers of 2 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32… Each term doubles (×2 each time)

Strategy: How to Identify an Unknown Sequence

🧭
Three-Question Diagnostic
  1. Is it doubling (or tripling)? → Powers of 2 (or 3)
  2. Are the differences constant? → Linear (arithmetic) sequence — not powers
  3. Are the differences themselves increasing? → Check if differences are 1,3,5,7… (square pattern) or 1,2,3,4… (triangular pattern)
If the terms themselves look familiar — compare to your square/cube lists directly.

Worked Identification Example 1

✏ Identify and continue: 3, 6, 11, 18, 27…
  1. Find differences: 3, 5, 7, 9 — these are odd numbers (increasing by 2 each time).
  2. Differences of differences: 2, 2, 2 — constant. This is a square-based pattern.
  3. Compare terms to n²: 1²=1, 2²=4, 3²=9… The terms are n² + 2: 1+2=3, 4+2=6, 9+2=11, 16+2=18, 25+2=27 ✓
  4. Next term: 6² + 2 = 36 + 2 = 38

Worked Identification Example 2

✏ Identify: 1, 3, 6, 10, 15…
  1. Differences: 2, 3, 4, 5 — increasing by 1 each time.
  2. This is not constant (not linear) and not doubling.
  3. Each difference increases by 1 → these are triangular numbers.
  4. Next difference = 6, so next term = 15 + 6 = 21.
  5. Formula check: triangular number T(n) = n(n+1)/2. T(6) = 6×7/2 = 21 ✓

Powers of 10 — Ordering & Comparison

GL papers use powers of 10 to test number sense with very large values. The key skill is comparing them without computing the full numbers.

Comparing Large Numbers

✏ Worked Example — Which is larger: 3 × 10⁴ or 2 × 10⁵?
  1. Expand: 3 × 10⁴ = 3 × 10,000 = 30,000
  2. Expand: 2 × 10⁵ = 2 × 100,000 = 200,000
  3. Compare: 2 × 10⁵ is larger (200,000 > 30,000)

Shortcut: A higher power of 10 always wins — unless the coefficient (the multiplier) is dramatically different. Always expand to be sure.

Ordering Multiple Values

✏ Worked Example — Order from smallest to largest

4 × 10³   ·   8 × 10²   ·   5 × 10³   ·   3 × 10⁴

  1. Convert all: 4000, 800, 5000, 30,000
  2. Order: 800 < 4000 < 5000 < 30,000
  3. In original form: 8×10² < 4×10³ < 5×10³ < 3×10⁴
💡
Quick Ordering Rule Compare the powers of 10 first. If the powers differ, the higher power always gives a larger number (as long as the coefficient is a single digit 1–9). Only when the powers are equal do you compare the coefficients.

Graded Practice Questions

Work through each question independently before checking the answer. Difficulty increases from Q1 to Q8.

1
Simplify each expression using index laws. Leave your answer as a power.
(a) 5⁴ × 5³    (b) 7⁶ ÷ 7²    (c) (3²)⁴
Easy · Index Laws
Show Answer

(a) Same base, multiplying → add exponents: 4 + 3 = 5⁷

(b) Same base, dividing → subtract exponents: 6 − 2 = 7⁴ = 2401

(c) Power of a power → multiply exponents: 2 × 4 = 3⁸ = 6561

2
True or false? Explain your reasoning for any false statement.
(a) 2³ × 2⁵ = 2¹⁵     (b) 4⁶ ÷ 4² = 4³     (c) (6²)³ = 6⁶
Easy · Index Laws
Show Answer

(a) False. Multiplying powers → add, not multiply exponents. 2³ × 2⁵ = 2⁸, not 2¹⁵.

(b) False. Dividing powers → subtract. 4⁶ ÷ 4² = 4⁴, not 4³.

(c) True. (6²)³ = 6^(2×3) = 6⁶ ✓

3
Find the value of:   (a) ∛27    (b) ∛125    (c) ∛1    (d) What is the cube root of 64?
Easy · Cube Roots
Show Answer

(a) ∛27 = 3 (because 3³ = 27)

(b) ∛125 = 5 (because 5³ = 125)

(c) ∛1 = 1

(d) ∛64 = 4 (because 4³ = 64) — not 8 (that's √64).

4
Find all possible values of x in each equation:
(a) x² = 36    (b) x² = 121    (c) x² = 144
Easy · ± Square Roots
Show Answer

Since both positive and negative values square to give the same result:

(a) x = +6 or −6 (written x = ±6)

(b) x = +11 or −11 (written x = ±11)

(c) x = +12 or −12 (written x = ±12)

5
Between which two consecutive integers does each square root lie? Do not use a calculator.
(a) √20    (b) √85    (c) √200
Medium · Estimation
Show Answer

(a) 4² = 16 and 5² = 25. Since 16 < 20 < 25, √20 is between 4 and 5. (Closer to 4.)

(b) 9² = 81 and 10² = 100. Since 81 < 85 < 100, √85 is between 9 and 10. (Closer to 9.)

(c) 14² = 196 and 15² = 225. Since 196 < 200 < 225, √200 is between 14 and 15. (Very close to 14.)

6
Look at this sequence:   0, 3, 8, 15, 24…
(a) What type of sequence is this?   (b) Write the next two terms.   (c) Write a formula for the nth term.
Medium · Sequences
Show Answer
  1. Differences: 3, 5, 7, 9 — odd numbers, increasing by 2. This is a modified square sequence.
  2. Compare to n²: 1,4,9,16,25. The terms are each one less: 0=1−1, 3=4−1, 8=9−1, 15=16−1, 24=25−1.
  3. Formula: nth term = n² − 1
  4. Next two terms: 6²−1 = 35, then 7²−1 = 48
7
A square room has an area of 196 m². (a) What is the side length of the room? (b) What is the perimeter? (c) Skirting board costs £4.50 per metre. How much does it cost to fit skirting board around the entire room?
Medium · Geometry Application
Show Answer
  1. Side = √196 = 14 m (because 14² = 196)
  2. Perimeter = 4 × 14 = 56 m
  3. Cost = 56 × £4.50 = £252
8
GL-Style Multi-Step Problem.
A storage cube has a volume of 125 cm³. A square tile has an area equal to one face of the cube. (a) What is the side length of the cube? (b) What is the area of one face? (c) Nine of these square tiles are arranged in a 3×3 grid. What is the total area of the grid? (d) Using an index law, express the side length of the 3×3 grid as a power, given that the tile side = 5¹.
Hard · Multi-Step
Show Answer
  1. Volume = s³ = 125, so s = ∛125 = 5 cm
  2. Area of one face = 5² = 25 cm²
  3. Total area of 3×3 grid = 9 × 25 = 225 cm²
  4. Side of grid = 3 × 5 = 15 cm. Expressed using index laws:
    Side = 3¹ × 5¹. The area = (3 × 5)² = 15² = 225 cm². ✓
    Alternatively: the grid side = 3 × 5¹, and 15² = 225 confirms our answer.
