What are Negative Numbers?

Negative numbers are numbers less than zero. They have a minus sign (−) in front of them. You meet them in everyday life all the time — temperatures below freezing, floors below ground level in a car park, bank accounts in debt, or a golf score below par.

Numbers greater than zero are called positive numbers. A number with no sign in front of it is always positive — so 5 means +5.

Zero (0) is special: it is neither positive nor negative.

⭐ Key Rule The further left a number is on the number line, the smaller it is.
The further right a number is on the number line, the larger it is.
So −10 < −3 < 0 < 5 < 12.
💡 Quick Check Which is smaller: −6 or −2?
−6 is further left on the number line, so −6 is smaller. A bigger negative number is actually worth less!

Number Lines

A number line is your best friend with negative numbers. Always picture one in your head during a question.

Standard Number Line (−10 to +10)

💡 Moving on the number line When you add, move to the right (→).
When you subtract, move to the left (←).

Worked Example: Using the number line

Worked Example
Work out: −5 + 8
1
Start at −5 on the number line.
2
We are adding 8, so move 8 spaces to the right.
3
−5, −4, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3 — we land on 3.
✓ Answer: −5 + 8 = 3
Worked Example
Work out: 2 − 9
1
Start at 2 on the number line.
2
We are subtracting 9, so move 9 spaces to the left.
3
2, 1, 0, −1, −2, −3, −4, −5, −6, −7 — we land on −7.
✓ Answer: 2 − 9 = −7

Sign Rules for All Four Operations

Addition and Subtraction — Two Signs Next to Each Other

Sometimes a calculation has two signs next to each other, like 4 + (−2) or −7 − (−3). Use this simple rule:

⭐ The Two-Signs Rule Same signs → replace with + (positive)
Different signs → replace with − (negative)

+ + = +    − − = +    + − = −    − + = −
Memory trick: "Same signs, cheer up! Different signs, feel down."
Worked Example
Work out: −7 − (−3)
1
Two signs next to each other: − and −. Same signs → replace with +.
2
Rewrite: −7 + 3
3
Start at −7, move 3 right → −4.
✓ Answer: −7 − (−3) = −4
Worked Example
Work out: 4 + (−9)
1
Two signs next to each other: + and −. Different signs → replace with −.
2
Rewrite: 4 − 9
3
Start at 4, move 9 left → −5.
✓ Answer: 4 + (−9) = −5

Midpoints & Halfway Values

GL Assessment often asks: "What number is halfway between −9 and 3?" There is a simple two-step method.

⭐ Method: Finding the Midpoint Step 1: Add the two numbers together.
Step 2: Divide by 2.
Midpoint = (number 1 + number 2) ÷ 2
Worked Example 1
What is halfway between −9 and 3?
1
Add: −9 + 3 = −6
2
Divide by 2: −6 ÷ 2 = −3
✓ The midpoint is −3.
Worked Example 2
What is the midpoint of −4 and −12?
1
Add: −4 + (−12) = −16
2
Divide by 2: −16 ÷ 2 = −8
✓ The midpoint is −8. (Check: −8 is 4 away from both −4 and −12. ✓)

Ordering Negative Numbers

GL Assessment asks you to put lists of numbers — including negatives, decimals and fractions — in order from smallest to largest (ascending) or largest to smallest (descending).

⭐ Key Idea Think of the number line. Numbers further left are smaller.
−100 < −10 < −1 < 0 < 1 < 10 < 100
Worked Example 1 — Integers
Put these numbers in ascending order (smallest first): 4, −2, 0, −3, −5, 1
1
Find the most negative (smallest): −5.
2
Then: −3, −2, 0, 1, 4.
✓ Answer: −5, −3, −2, 0, 1, 4
Worked Example 2 — Decimals
Put in order from smallest to largest: −0.5, −1.2, 0.3, −0.8
1
All negatives are less than 0.3. Among the negatives, the larger the digit, the smaller the number: −1.2 < −0.8 < −0.5.
2
Then comes 0.3 (the only positive).
✓ Answer: −1.2, −0.8, −0.5, 0.3
Worked Example 3 — Fractions and Decimals Mixed
Put in order from smallest to largest: −¾, −0.5, −1, 0.2
1
Convert everything to decimals: −¾ = −0.75, −0.5 = −0.5, −1 = −1.0, 0.2 = +0.2.
2
Order the decimals: −1.0 < −0.75 < −0.5 < 0.2.
3
Convert back: −1 < −¾ < −0.5 < 0.2.
✓ Answer: −1, −¾, −0.5, 0.2
💡 Top tip for fractions Always convert fractions to decimals before ordering. It avoids confusion every time.

Four-Quadrant Coordinates

A coordinate grid has two axes. The x-axis goes left–right and the y-axis goes up–down. Where they cross is called the origin (0, 0).

Negative numbers let us use all four quadrants — the four regions of the grid.

📍 How to read a coordinate: (x, y) Always read x first (across), then y (up/down).
Memory trick: "Along the corridor, then up (or down) the stairs."

Positive x → right  |  Negative x → left
Positive y → up  |  Negative y → down
Worked Example — Reading Coordinates
Read the coordinates of points A, B, C, and D from the grid above.
1
A (green): Go 3 right, 4 up → (3, 4) — Q1 (both positive).
2
B (red): Go 2 left, 3 up → (−2, 3) — Q2 (negative x, positive y).
3
C (purple): Go 4 left, 2 down → (−4, −2) — Q3 (both negative).
4
D (amber): Go 2 right, 3 down → (2, −3) — Q4 (positive x, negative y).
✓ A(3,4) · B(−2,3) · C(−4,−2) · D(2,−3)
📐 Which Quadrant? Q1: (+, +) — top right | Q2: (−, +) — top left
Q3: (−, −) — bottom left | Q4: (+, −) — bottom right

Real-World Context Problems

GL Assessment almost always puts negative numbers into real life. Here are the five most common contexts — learn the approach for each.

🌡️ Context 1: Temperature

Problem: The temperature in Moscow is −14°C. The temperature in London is 3°C. What is the difference in temperature between the two cities?

Difference = higher temperature − lower temperature
= 3 − (−14) = 3 + 14 = 17°C
✓ Answer: The difference is 17°C.
The temperature drops from 3°C to 0°C (that's 3 degrees) then from 0°C to −14°C (that's 14 more degrees). Total = 17°C. ✓
🏦 Context 2: Bank Balance / Debt

Problem: Priya has £−45 in her bank account (she is overdrawn by £45). She earns £120. What is her new balance?

New balance = −45 + 120 = +£75
✓ Answer: Priya now has £75 in her account.
🌊 Context 3: Depth Below Sea Level

Problem: A submarine is at −120 m (120 metres below sea level). It rises 75 metres. What is its new depth?

New depth = −120 + 75 = −45 m
✓ Answer: The submarine is now at −45 m (45 metres below sea level).
🏢 Context 4: Floors in a Building

Problem: A lift starts at floor −3 (underground car park). It goes up 8 floors. What floor does it reach?

Floor = −3 + 8 = +5
✓ Answer: The lift reaches floor 5.
⛳ Context 5: Golf Scores (Under/Over Par)

Problem: In golf, a score below par is shown as a negative number (better than average), and above par is positive (worse). Player A scores −5 and Player B scores +2. What is the difference between their scores?

Difference = +2 − (−5) = 2 + 5 = 7
✓ Answer: There are 7 strokes between them.

Note: In golf, the lower (more negative) score wins. Player A is winning by 7 strokes.

Missing Number Problems

GL Assessment uses missing number questions where you must work backwards to find the unknown value. These look like: ___ − 7 = −12 or −4 × ___ = 20.

⭐ Method: Inverse Operations Use the opposite (inverse) operation to find the missing number.
Addition ↔ Subtraction    Multiplication ↔ Division
Worked Example 1 — Missing Number with Subtraction
___ − 7 = −12. What is the missing number?
1
The inverse of "subtract 7" is "add 7".
2
Missing number = −12 + 7 = −5.
3
Check: −5 − 7 = −12 ✓
✓ Answer: −5
Worked Example 2 — Missing Number with Multiplication
−4 × ___ = 20. What is the missing number?
1
The inverse of "multiply by −4" is "divide by −4".
2
Missing number = 20 ÷ (−4) = −5.
3
Check: −4 × (−5) = +20. Same signs → positive ✓
✓ Answer: −5
Worked Example 3 — Missing Number with Addition
___ + (−8) = −3. What is the missing number?
1
Rewrite: ___ − 8 = −3 (two signs: + and − → −).
2
Inverse of "subtract 8" is "add 8".
3
Missing number = −3 + 8 = 5.
4
Check: 5 + (−8) = 5 − 8 = −3 ✓
✓ Answer: 5

Finding the Difference

The word "difference" in maths always means subtract the smaller from the larger. With negative numbers, you must be careful about which is actually smaller.

⭐ Method: Finding the Difference Difference = larger value − smaller value
With negative numbers: the number closer to zero is always the larger one.
Always subtract in the direction: higher temperature/value MINUS lower.
Worked Example 1
Moscow is −14°C. London is 3°C. What is the difference?
1
Larger value = 3°C. Smaller value = −14°C.
2
Difference = 3 − (−14) = 3 + 14 = 17°C.
✓ The difference is 17°C.
Worked Example 2
What is the difference between −8 and −2?
1
Larger value = −2 (it is closer to zero). Smaller value = −8.
2
Difference = −2 − (−8) = −2 + 8 = 6.
✓ The difference is 6.
💡 Quick sense-check The difference between two numbers is always a positive number (a distance can't be negative). If you get a negative answer, you subtracted the wrong way around — swap them!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Here are the 6 mistakes GL Assessment students make most often. Study these carefully — knowing the trap means you won't fall into it!

❌ Mistake 1: Thinking a bigger negative number is larger

Students see −8 and think "8 is bigger than 2, so −8 > 2." Wrong! On the number line, −8 is far to the left.

✗ Wrong thinking
−8 > −2 "because 8 is bigger"
✓ Correct
−2 > −8 (−2 is closer to zero, so it's larger)
❌ Mistake 2: Subtracting a negative makes it more negative

Subtracting a negative is the same as adding. Students forget and make the answer smaller instead of bigger.

✗ Wrong
−7 − (−3) = −10
("subtract 3 from −7")
✓ Correct
−7 − (−3) = −7 + 3 = −4
(same signs → +)
❌ Mistake 3: Getting the difference direction wrong

Students subtract in the wrong order and get a negative difference — but a difference (a distance) is always positive.

✗ Wrong
Difference between 3 and −14: 3 − (−14)... student does (−14) − 3 = −17
✓ Correct
Difference = larger − smaller = 3 − (−14) = 3 + 14 = 17
❌ Mistake 4: Sign errors when three negatives are multiplied

Students cancel all the negatives and give a positive answer, forgetting to check the count carefully.

✗ Wrong
(−2) × (−3) × (−5) = +30
("all negatives cancel out")
✓ Correct
3 negatives = odd = negative answer: −30
(−2)(−3) = +6, then 6 × (−5) = −30)
❌ Mistake 5: Confusing "ascending" and "descending" order

Ascending = going up = smallest first. Descending = going down = largest first. Students often reverse these.

✗ Ascending order (wrong)
4, 1, 0, −2, −3, −5
✓ Ascending order (correct)
−5, −3, −2, 0, 1, 4
❌ Mistake 6: Forgetting that negative × negative = positive

Students apply "negative" to the answer just because there are negative signs in the question — even when the rule says positive.

✗ Wrong
−6 × (−7) = −42
("there are minuses so it's negative")
✓ Correct
−6 × (−7) = +42
(same signs → positive)

GL Assessment-Style Practice Questions

These 10 questions use exactly the style, format and difficulty of GL Assessment papers. Each has four options (A–D) with realistic wrong-answer traps. Select your answer, then check the answers section below.

Question 1 — Ordering
Put these numbers in ascending order (smallest first):
−0.5   −1.2   0.3   −0.8   0
Question 2 — Midpoint / Halfway
What is the midpoint of −11 and 5?
Question 3 — Temperature Word Problem
The temperature in Oslo is −9°C. The temperature in Cairo is 22°C. What is the difference in temperature between the two cities?
Question 4 — Missing Number
___ × (−6) = −42. What is the missing number?
Question 5 — Coordinates
Point P is plotted at (−3, −4). In which quadrant does point P lie?
Question 6 — Bank Balance Word Problem
Jake's bank account is overdrawn by £63 (balance = −£63). He receives his pocket money of £15 each week for 6 weeks. What is his bank balance after 6 weeks?
Question 7 — Multiplication Sign Rule
What is (−3) × (−4) × (−2)?
Question 8 — Golf Scores Word Problem
In a golf tournament, Amy scored −7 (7 under par) and Ben scored +3 (3 over par). How many strokes apart are their scores?
Question 9 — Missing Number (Subtraction)
−3 − ___ = 5. What is the missing number?
Question 10 — Depth / Sea Level Word Problem
A fish is swimming at −35 m (35 metres below sea level). A seabird is flying at 28 m above sea level. What is the difference in height between the seabird and the fish?

📋 Answers & Explanations

Q1 — Ordering
Answer: B) −1.2, −0.8, −0.5, 0, 0.3
Negative numbers with larger digits are smaller (further left on the number line). −1.2 < −0.8 < −0.5, then 0, then 0.3.
Q2 — Midpoint
Answer: C) −3
Midpoint = (−11 + 5) ÷ 2 = −6 ÷ 2 = −3. Check: −3 is 8 units from −11 and 8 units from 5. ✓ Trap: A and B come from adding wrong or dividing wrong.
Q3 — Temperature
Answer: D) 31°C
Difference = 22 − (−9) = 22 + 9 = 31°C. Trap B gives −31 (wrong direction). Trap A/C gives 13 (subtracting instead of adding after sign rule).
Q4 — Missing Number
Answer: B) 7
Missing number = −42 ÷ (−6) = +7. Same signs → positive. Check: 7 × (−6) = −42 ✓. Trap A: students forget that −÷− = +.
Q5 — Coordinates
Answer: C) Quadrant 3
Q3 is where both x and y are negative: (−, −). (−3, −4) is left of centre and below centre → Q3.
Q6 — Bank Balance
Answer: D) £27
6 weeks × £15 = £90 received. New balance = −63 + 90 = +£27. Trap B only adds £15 once. Trap A subtracts instead of adds.
Q7 — Three Negatives Multiplied
Answer: B) −24
(−3) × (−4) = +12. Then +12 × (−2) = −24. Three negatives = odd = negative. Trap A is the classic error of thinking all negatives cancel to positive.
Q8 — Golf Scores
Answer: C) 10
Difference = 3 − (−7) = 3 + 7 = 10 strokes. Trap A (4) comes from 7 − 3, ignoring the negative sign entirely.
Q9 — Missing Number
Answer: C) −8
Rearrange: missing number = −3 − 5 = −8. Check: −3 − (−8) = −3 + 8 = 5 ✓. Trap A (8) forgets the sign. Trap D (−2) comes from −3 + 5 (adding instead of subtracting).
Q10 — Depth / Sea Level
Answer: C) 63 m
Difference = 28 − (−35) = 28 + 35 = 63 m. Trap A (7) comes from 35 − 28, ignoring the negative sign. The difference is always positive.
