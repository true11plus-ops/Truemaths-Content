Prime Factorisation — The Foundation

📖
Key Definitions A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. (2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13…)
A factor divides into a number exactly, leaving no remainder.
Prime factorisation means writing a number as a product of only prime numbers.

Method 1: Factor Trees

Split a number into any two factors, then keep splitting until every branch ends in a prime. Circle all the primes at the ends — those are your prime factors.

36 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 3

Blue = prime (stop here)

60 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 5

You can split in any order!

💡
Tip: Write in Index Form 36 = 2² × 3² and 60 = 2² × 3 × 5. This compact form makes the Venn diagram method much faster.

Method 2: Repeated Division (The "Ladder")

Divide by the smallest prime that goes in, then keep dividing the result. Stop when you reach 1.

✏ Worked Example — Prime factors of 60

Always try the smallest primes first: 2, then 3, then 5, then 7…

60 ÷ 2 = 30
30 ÷ 2 = 15
15 ÷ 3 = 5
5 ÷ 5 = 1 ✓

Read off the divisors: 60 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 = 2² × 3 × 5

⚡
Quick Check: Is it prime? Test divisibility only up to √n. For example, to check if 37 is prime, test primes up to √37 ≈ 6. Try 2, 3, 5 — none divide in, so 37 is prime.

Quick Practice — Prime Factorise These

Try these before moving on (answers below):

  1. Find the prime factorisation of 24
  2. Find the prime factorisation of 45
Show Answers

24: 24 = 2 × 12 = 2 × 2 × 6 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 = 2³ × 3

45: 45 = 5 × 9 = 5 × 3 × 3 = 3² × 5

HCF & LCM of Two Numbers

📏
HCF — Highest Common Factor The largest number that divides exactly into two (or more) numbers.

When to use: Splitting or dividing into equal groups — "largest equal sections," "tile a floor," "cut equal lengths."
🔄
LCM — Lowest Common Multiple The smallest number that is a multiple of two (or more) numbers.

When to use: Recurring events happening at different intervals — "next time they meet," "when do they flash together."

Method A: Listing (good for small numbers)

✏ Worked Example — HCF of 18 and 24

Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18

Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24

Common factors: 1, 2, 3, 6 → HCF = 6

✏ Worked Example — LCM of 8 and 12

Multiples of 8: 8, 16, 24, 32, 40…

Multiples of 12: 12, 24, 36, 48…

First match: LCM = 24

Method B: Venn Diagram (best for larger numbers)

  1. Find the prime factors of both numbers (use a factor tree or ladder).
  2. Place factors that appear in both numbers in the overlapping middle section.
  3. HCF = multiply everything in the middle only.
  4. LCM = multiply everything in both circles (left + middle + right).
✏ Worked Example — HCF and LCM of 36 and 60

From Section 1: 36 = 2² × 3²  and  60 = 2² × 3 × 5

Shared prime factors: both have 2×2 and one 3. The extra 3 belongs to 36 only; the 5 belongs to 60 only.

✅
HCF of 36 and 60 Middle only: 2 × 2 × 3 = 12
✅
LCM of 36 and 60 Everything: 3 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 = 180

HCF & LCM of Three Numbers

⚠️
Don't use a Venn diagram for three numbers. A three-circle Venn gets complicated fast. Use the Prime Factorisation Table instead — it's faster and error-proof.

The Prime Factorisation Table Method

  1. Find the prime factors of each number.
  2. List every prime that appears across any of the numbers in the top row.
  3. For each number, write how many times that prime appears (its power).
  4. HCF: For each prime, take the lowest power. Multiply those together.
  5. LCM: For each prime, take the highest power. Multiply those together.
✏ Worked Example — HCF and LCM of 4, 6, and 9

First, prime factorise each:

  • 4 = 2²
  • 6 = 2¹ × 3¹
  • 9 = 3²

Now build the table. Primes that appear are 2 and 3:

Number Power of 2 Power of 3
4 2² = 4 3⁰ = 1 (not present)
6 2¹ = 2 3¹ = 3
9 2⁰ = 1 (not present) 3² = 9
HCF (lowest powers) 2⁰ = 1 3⁰ = 1
LCM (highest powers) 2² = 4 3² = 9
✅
HCF of 4, 6, 9 1 × 1 = 1
(No prime appears in all three numbers.)
✅
LCM of 4, 6, 9 4 × 9 = 36
💡
Verify the LCM: Is 36 divisible by 4? Yes (36÷4=9). By 6? Yes (36÷6=6). By 9? Yes (36÷9=4). ✓
4

The Key Relationship Formula

For any two numbers A and B, there is a beautiful connection between their HCF and LCM:

HCF(A, B) × LCM(A, B) = A × B
✏ Forward Use — Verification

For 36 and 60: HCF = 12, LCM = 180

12 × 180 = 2160   and   36 × 60 = 2160 ✓

⚡ Reverse Use — A Common GL Trap

GL examiners often give you HCF, LCM, and one of the numbers, then ask for the other.

✏ Worked Example — Find the Missing Number

"The HCF of two numbers is 4 and their LCM is 48. One of the numbers is 16. Find the other."

  1. Use the formula: HCF × LCM = A × B
  2. Substitute: 4 × 48 = 16 × B
  3. Calculate: 192 = 16 × B
  4. Solve: B = 192 ÷ 16 = 12

Check: HCF(16, 12) = 4 ✓    LCM(16, 12) = 48 ✓

⚠️
Important Limitation This formula only works for two numbers. It does not extend directly to three numbers.

HCF & LCM in Fraction Work

HCF and LCM are not just standalone topics — they appear inside fraction questions too. Recognise these two patterns:

Pattern 1: Simplifying Fractions → Use HCF

To simplify a fraction to its lowest terms, divide both numerator and denominator by their HCF.

✏ Worked Example — Simplify 36/48
  1. Find HCF(36, 48).
    36 = 2² × 3²   48 = 2⁴ × 3
    Shared: 2² × 3 = 12. So HCF = 12.
  2. Divide top and bottom by 12:
    3648 = 36 ÷ 1248 ÷ 12 = 34
💡
Using the HCF gets you to the simplest form in one step. Dividing by a smaller common factor works too — but you'll need to simplify again afterwards.

Pattern 2: Adding or Subtracting Unlike Fractions → Use LCM

To add fractions with different denominators, find the LCM of the denominators — this is the Lowest Common Denominator (LCD).

✏ Worked Example — Calculate 5/6 + 3/8
  1. Find LCM(6, 8).
    6 = 2 × 3   8 = 2³
    Highest powers: 2³ × 3 = 24. So LCM = 24.
  2. Convert both fractions to 24ths:
    56 = 2024 (×4)    38 = 924 (×3)
  3. Add: 2024 + 924 = 2924 = 1524

Edge Cases — GL Setter Traps

🪤
These are deliberate traps in GL papers. GL setters love to use pairs where one number is a multiple of the other — many students get confused and give the wrong answer.

Trap 1: One Number is a Multiple of the Other

📌
HCF(12, 36) = 12
The smaller number itself is the HCF when it divides exactly into the larger. Factors of 12: 1,2,3,4,6,12. All divide into 36. Largest = 12.
📌
LCM(5, 15) = 15
The larger number itself is the LCM when the smaller divides into it exactly. Multiples of 15: 15, 30, 45… The first multiple of 5 that's also a multiple of 15 is 15 itself.

Trap 2: Numbers with No Common Factors (Co-prime Pairs)

📌 When the HCF is 1

If two numbers share no prime factors (we call them co-prime), their HCF = 1 and their LCM = their product.

  • HCF(7, 11) = 1 (both are primes — no shared factors)
  • LCM(7, 11) = 77 (7 × 11)
  • HCF(8, 15) = 1 (8 = 2³, 15 = 3 × 5 — no overlap)
  • LCM(8, 15) = 120 (8 × 15)
🎯
Exam Strategy Before starting any HCF/LCM question, ask yourself: "Is one number a multiple of the other?" If yes, the answer is one of the two numbers themselves. This saves time and prevents errors.

Exam Problem-Type Taxonomy

GL papers rarely say "find the HCF." Instead, they describe a real situation. Learn to recognise these patterns instantly:

Problem Type → Use HCF → Use LCM
Keyword signal"largest," "equal groups," "split," "identical," "tile""next time," "together again," "both," "common"
Venn memory ruleMiddle of diagramAll of diagram
Size of answer≤ smaller number≥ larger number

Problem Type 1 — Clashing Timers (→ LCM)

✏ Worked Example

"Bell A rings every 8 minutes. Bell B rings every 12 minutes. They ring together at 9:00 AM. What is the next time they ring together?"

  1. We need the smallest time that is a multiple of both 8 and 12. → Find LCM(8, 12).
  2. 8 = 2³   12 = 2² × 3 → LCM = 2³ × 3 = 24 minutes
  3. Add to start time: 9:00 AM + 24 min = 9:24 AM

Problem Type 2 — Equal Groups / Tiling (→ HCF)

✏ Worked Example

"What is the largest square tile that fits exactly on a floor 24 cm by 36 cm, with no cutting?"

  1. The tile side must divide into both 24 and 36 exactly. → Find HCF(24, 36).
  2. 24 = 2³ × 3    36 = 2² × 3² → HCF = 2² × 3 = 12
  3. Largest tile = 12 cm × 12 cm
  4. How many tiles? (24÷12) × (36÷12) = 2 × 3 = 6 tiles

Problem Type 3 — Reverse Formula (→ HCF × LCM = A × B)

✏ Worked Example

"Two numbers have HCF 6 and LCM 90. One number is 18. What is the other?"

  1. Formula: HCF × LCM = A × B
  2. 6 × 90 = 18 × B → 540 = 18B → B = 30
  3. The other number is 30.
  4. Check: HCF(18,30) = 6 ✓   LCM(18,30) = 90 ✓

Problem Type 4 — Multi-Step Problem

✏ Worked Example — Clock Time with AM/PM

"A red light flashes every 15 seconds. A green light flashes every 20 seconds. A blue light flashes every 24 seconds. All three flash together at exactly 8:00 AM. What is the next time all three flash together? Give your answer as a time."

  1. Find LCM(15, 20, 24) using the table method:
    15 = 3 × 5    20 = 2² × 5    24 = 2³ × 3
    Primes: 2, 3, 5 → highest powers: 2³, 3¹, 5¹ → LCM = 8 × 3 × 5 = 120 seconds
  2. Convert: 120 seconds = 2 minutes.
  3. Add to 8:00 AM: 8:02 AM

Sanity-Check Rules & Graded Practice

Sanity-Check Rules

Before writing a final answer, run through these checks:

✔
HCF is always ≤ the smallest number in the set.
✔
LCM is always ≥ the largest number in the set.
✔
If numbers are co-prime (share no factors), HCF = 1, LCM = product.
✔
If one number divides the other: HCF = smaller, LCM = larger.

Graded Practice Questions

Work through these in order. Don't peek at the answer until you've tried!

1
Find the HCF and LCM of 20 and 30 using the listing method.
Easy · Listing
Show Answer

Factors of 20: 1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20

Factors of 30: 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 10, 15, 30

Common factors: 1, 2, 5, 10 → HCF = 10

Multiples of 20: 20, 40, 60…

Multiples of 30: 30, 60…

LCM = 60

Check: 10 × 60 = 600 = 20 × 30 ✓

2
Use the Venn diagram method to find the HCF and LCM of 28 and 42.
Easy · Venn
Show Answer

28 = 2² × 7

42 = 2 × 3 × 7

Shared (middle): 2 × 7 = 14

Left only (28): 2 (one extra factor of 2)

Right only (42): 3

HCF = 2 × 7 = 14

LCM = 2 × 2 × 7 × 3 = 84

Check: 14 × 84 = 1176 = 28 × 42 ✓

3
Find the HCF and LCM of 12, 18, and 30.
Medium · Three Numbers
Show Answer

12 = 2² × 3    18 = 2 × 3²    30 = 2 × 3 × 5

Primes to consider: 2, 3, 5

For 2: powers are 2, 1, 1 → lowest = 2¹, highest = 2²

For 3: powers are 1, 2, 1 → lowest = 3¹, highest = 3²

For 5: powers are 0, 0, 1 → lowest = 5⁰ = 1, highest = 5¹

HCF = 2¹ × 3¹ × 1 = 6

LCM = 2² × 3² × 5 = 4 × 9 × 5 = 180

4
The HCF of two numbers is 8 and their LCM is 96. One number is 32. Find the other number.
Medium · Reverse Formula
Show Answer

HCF × LCM = A × B

8 × 96 = 32 × B

768 = 32B

B = 768 ÷ 32 = 24

Check: HCF(32, 24) = 8 ✓   LCM(32, 24) = 96 ✓

5
Simplify the fraction 5684 to its lowest terms using HCF.
Medium · Fractions
Show Answer

56 = 2³ × 7    84 = 2² × 3 × 7

HCF = 2² × 7 = 28

5684 = 56 ÷ 2884 ÷ 28 = 23

6
A school timetable has PE every 6 school days, Art every 8 school days, and Music every 9 school days. All three subjects fall on the same day (Day 1). On which school day will all three subjects next fall on the same day? If school starts on 3rd September (Monday) and runs 5 days a week, what is the date of that day?
Hard · Multi-Step
Show Answer

Step 1: Find LCM(6, 8, 9)

6 = 2 × 3    8 = 2³    9 = 3²

LCM = 2³ × 3² = 8 × 9 = 72

All three subjects next meet on Day 72.

Step 2: Count school days from Day 1

We need to reach Day 72, which means 71 school days after Day 1.
71 ÷ 5 = 14 full weeks (70 days) + 1 extra day.

Day 1 = Monday 3rd September.
+14 weeks = Monday 16th December.
+1 day = Tuesday 17th December

(Note: this assumes no holidays — in a real exam, ignore half-terms unless told otherwise.)

7
Calculate 56 + 78 using LCM to find the common denominator. Give your answer as a mixed number.
Medium · Fractions
Show Answer

LCM(6, 8): 6 = 2 × 3   8 = 2³ → LCM = 24

56 = 2024    78 = 2124

Sum = 4124 = 11724

8
A rectangular garden measures 56 m by 84 m. A gardener wants to plant identical square flower beds, as large as possible, to exactly fill the garden with no gaps and no overlapping. How many flower beds will there be?
Hard · Tiling + Calculation
Show Answer

Step 1: Largest square side = HCF(56, 84)

56 = 2³ × 7    84 = 2² × 3 × 7

HCF = 2² × 7 = 28 m

Step 2: Count beds along each side:

Along 56 m side: 56 ÷ 28 = 2 beds

Along 84 m side: 84 ÷ 28 = 3 beds

Total = 2 × 3 = 6 flower beds

9
The HCF of two numbers is 12 and their LCM is 144. Given that both numbers are between 20 and 100, find both numbers.
Hard · Reasoning
Show Answer

Since HCF = 12, both numbers must be multiples of 12.

Multiples of 12 between 20 and 100: 24, 36, 48, 60, 72, 84, 96.

We need a pair where HCF = 12 and LCM = 144.

Using the formula: A × B = 12 × 144 = 1728.

Which pairs of multiples of 12 multiply to 1728?
36 × 48 = 1728 ✓

Check: HCF(36, 48) = 12 ✓   LCM(36, 48) = 144 ✓

The two numbers are 36 and 48.
