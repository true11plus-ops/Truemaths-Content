What are Inverse Operations?

Every mathematical operation has an opposite — a partner that undoes it completely. We call these inverse operations.

Think of it like getting dressed in the morning. You put your socks on first, then your shoes. To undo it at the end of the day, you take your shoes off first, then your socks — in reverse order. Inverse operations work exactly the same way: they undo a step by going backwards.

OperationIts InverseExampleCheck (inverse)
Addition (+)↔Subtraction (−)8 + 5 = 1313 − 5 = 8 ✓
Subtraction (−)↔Addition (+)13 − 5 = 88 + 5 = 13 ✓
Multiplication (×)↔Division (÷)6 × 4 = 2424 ÷ 4 = 6 ✓
Division (÷)↔Multiplication (×)24 ÷ 4 = 66 × 4 = 24 ✓
⭐ The Big Idea If you know what happened to a number, you can always find where it started by using the inverse (opposite) operation. This is the key to every missing number question you will ever meet.
💡 Remember Addition and subtraction are inverses of each other.
Multiplication and division are inverses of each other.
These two pairs are all you need for 11+ missing number questions!

The Balance Diagram — Why Inverse Operations Work

An equation is like a perfectly balanced set of weighing scales. Whatever is on the left side equals whatever is on the right side. If you do the same thing to both sides, the scales stay balanced. Inverse operations use this idea to isolate the unknown.

📐 The Balance Rule Whatever you do to one side of an equation, you must do the same to the other side.
This keeps the scales balanced — and that's exactly what inverse operations do!
Worked Example — Balance Method
Solve: □ + 4 = 11
1
The unknown (□) has 4 added to it. The inverse of adding 4 is subtracting 4.
2
Subtract 4 from both sides: □ + 4 − 4 = 11 − 4
3
The +4 and −4 cancel on the left, leaving: □ = 7
✓ □ = 7
🔍 Check: 7 + 4 = 11 ✓ The scales are still balanced!

Function Machines

A function machine is a diagram used in GL Assessment where a number goes in, one or more operations happen inside a "machine box," and a result comes out. You might be asked to find the output, the input, or the missing operation.

What a function machine looks like

Single-Step Function Machines

Worked Example 1 — Find the Output
Input: 8  |  Operation: × 5  |  Output: ?
1
Apply the operation to the input: 8 × 5 = 40.
✓ Output = 40
Worked Example 2 — Find the Input (work backwards!)
Input: ?  |  Operation: + 9  |  Output: 16
1
We know the output (16) and the operation (+9). To find the input, use the inverse: − 9.
2
Input = 16 − 9 = 7.
✓ Input = 7
🔍 Check: 7 + 9 = 16 ✓
Worked Example 3 — Two-Step Function Machine (find the input)
Input: ?  |  Step 1: × 3  |  Step 2: − 4  |  Output: 11
1
Work backwards from the output. The last operation was −4, so undo it: 11 + 4 = 15.
2
The first operation was ×3, so undo it: 15 ÷ 3 = 5.
✓ Input = 5
🔍 Check: 5 × 3 = 15, then 15 − 4 = 11 ✓
Worked Example 4 — Find the Missing Operation
Input: 20  |  Operation: ?  |  Output: 4
1
We need to get from 20 to 4. Ask: what single operation on 20 gives 4?
2
20 ÷ 5 = 4, or 20 − 16 = 4. For one-step machines, both are valid — but ÷ 5 is simpler and most likely intended.
✓ Missing operation: ÷ 5
🔍 Check: 20 ÷ 5 = 4 ✓
💡 Key tip for function machines If you need to find the input, always work right to left, applying the inverse of each operation in reverse order. This is the most important function machine skill for GL Assessment!

Missing Numbers — Standard Position

In these questions, the unknown (□) is at the start of the calculation. Use the inverse operation to isolate it.

⭐ Method for every type Identify what operation was done to □. Apply the inverse operation to the other side. That gives you □.
Type 1 — Addition: □ + 7 = 15
□ + 7 = 15
1
7 is being added to □. Inverse: subtract 7.
2
□ = 15 − 7 = 8.
✓ □ = 8
🔍 Check: 8 + 7 = 15 ✓
Type 2 — Subtraction: □ − 9 = 4
□ − 9 = 4
1
9 is being subtracted from □. Inverse: add 9.
2
□ = 4 + 9 = 13.
✓ □ = 13
🔍 Check: 13 − 9 = 4 ✓
Type 3 — Multiplication: □ × 6 = 42
□ × 6 = 42
1
□ is being multiplied by 6. Inverse: divide by 6.
2
□ = 42 ÷ 6 = 7.
✓ □ = 7
🔍 Check: 7 × 6 = 42 ✓
Type 4 — Division: □ ÷ 4 = 9
□ ÷ 4 = 9
1
□ is being divided by 4. Inverse: multiply by 4.
2
□ = 9 × 4 = 36.
✓ □ = 36
🔍 Check: 36 ÷ 4 = 9 ✓

Missing Numbers — Non-Standard Positions

These are trickier because the unknown isn't at the start. GL Assessment uses these often. You need to think carefully about exactly what role the □ plays in the calculation.

Type A — Division with unknown divisor: 12 ÷ □ = 3
12 ÷ □ = 3
1
Ask yourself: "12 divided by what number gives 3?" Rearrange using multiplication: □ × 3 = 12.
2
Now solve: □ = 12 ÷ 3 = 4.
✓ □ = 4
🔍 Check: 12 ÷ 4 = 3 ✓
⚠️ Common trap: 20 − □ = 13 This is one of the most common errors in 11+ papers. Many students write □ = 13 − 20 = −7. That's wrong! Read below carefully.
Type B — Subtraction with unknown being subtracted: 20 − □ = 13
20 − □ = 13
1
Think of this as: "20 take away something gives 13." The something must be the difference between 20 and 13.
2
□ = 20 − 13 = 7. (NOT 13 − 20. We subtract the answer from the starting number.)
3
Another way to see it: add □ to both sides → 20 = 13 + □, so □ = 20 − 13.
✓ □ = 7
🔍 Check: 20 − 7 = 13 ✓
Type C — □ being subtracted in position two: □ − 8 = 4
□ − 8 = 4
1
8 is being subtracted from □. To undo "subtract 8," we add 8 to both sides.
2
□ = 4 + 8 = 12.
✓ □ = 12
🔍 Check: 12 − 8 = 4 ✓
Type D — Multiplication with unknown as multiplier: 6 × □ = 54
6 × □ = 54
1
6 multiplied by □ gives 54. Inverse of ×6 is ÷6.
2
□ = 54 ÷ 6 = 9.
✓ □ = 9
🔍 Check: 6 × 9 = 54 ✓
💡 Tip for non-standard positions When you see a − □ = b, always calculate □ = a − b (not b − a).
When you see a ÷ □ = b, rearrange to □ × b = a, then divide: □ = a ÷ b.

Two-Step and Three-Step Missing Number Chains

These questions combine two or three operations. The secret is to work backwards, undoing the operations in reverse order — just like the function machine section.

⭐ The "Onion" Method Peel back the operations one at a time, starting from the outermost (last) operation and working inward toward □.
Always undo the last thing that happened first.
Worked Example 1
(□ × 3) + 2 = 11
1
The last operation is +2. Undo it: 11 − 2 = 9. So now □ × 3 = 9.
2
Undo ×3: 9 ÷ 3 = 3.
✓ □ = 3
🔍 Check: (3 × 3) + 2 = 9 + 2 = 11 ✓
Worked Example 2
3 × □ − 4 = 11
1
The last operation is −4. Undo it: 11 + 4 = 15. So now 3 × □ = 15.
2
Undo ×3: 15 ÷ 3 = 5.
✓ □ = 5
🔍 Check: 3 × 5 − 4 = 15 − 4 = 11 ✓
Worked Example 3
□ ÷ 3 + 5 = 11
1
Last operation is +5. Undo it: 11 − 5 = 6. So now □ ÷ 3 = 6.
2
Undo ÷3: 6 × 3 = 18.
✓ □ = 18
🔍 Check: 18 ÷ 3 + 5 = 6 + 5 = 11 ✓
Worked Example 4 — Three-Step Chain
(□ + 4) × 2 − 3 = 13
1
Last operation: −3. Undo it: 13 + 3 = 16. Now (□ + 4) × 2 = 16.
2
Next last operation: ×2. Undo it: 16 ÷ 2 = 8. Now □ + 4 = 8.
3
Last operation: +4. Undo it: 8 − 4 = 4.
✓ □ = 4
🔍 Check: (4 + 4) × 2 − 3 = 8 × 2 − 3 = 16 − 3 = 13 ✓

Missing Numbers with Decimals & Fractions

GL Assessment also uses decimals and simple fractions in missing number questions. The method is exactly the same — just be careful with your arithmetic!

Decimals

Worked Example 1 — Decimal multiplication
□ × 4 = 2.8
1
□ is multiplied by 4. Inverse: divide by 4.
2
□ = 2.8 ÷ 4 = 0.7. (Think: 28 ÷ 4 = 7, so 2.8 ÷ 4 = 0.7)
✓ □ = 0.7
🔍 Check: 0.7 × 4 = 2.8 ✓
Worked Example 2 — Decimal addition
□ + 3.5 = 8.2
1
3.5 is added to □. Inverse: subtract 3.5.
2
□ = 8.2 − 3.5 = 4.7.
✓ □ = 4.7
🔍 Check: 4.7 + 3.5 = 8.2 ✓

Fractions

💡 Fractions tip Treat the fraction just like any other number — the inverse operation method is identical. Just make sure to add or subtract fractions carefully using a common denominator when needed.
Worked Example 3 — Fraction addition
□ + ½ = 2
1
½ is added to □. Inverse: subtract ½.
2
□ = 2 − ½ = 1½ = 1.5.
✓ □ = 1½
🔍 Check: 1½ + ½ = 2 ✓
Worked Example 4 — Fraction multiplication
□ × ¼ = 3
1
□ is multiplied by ¼. Inverse: divide by ¼, which is the same as multiplying by 4.
2
□ = 3 × 4 = 12.
✓ □ = 12
🔍 Check: 12 × ¼ = 12 ÷ 4 = 3 ✓

Unknowns on Both Sides

Sometimes a number sentence has operations on both sides, like □ + 9 = 5 + 12. The good news is that these are straightforward — just follow a two-step method.

⭐ Method for unknowns on both sides Step 1: Simplify the known side (the side without the □) by working it out.
Step 2: Solve the simpler equation using inverse operations as normal.
Worked Example 1
□ + 9 = 5 + 12
1
Simplify the right side: 5 + 12 = 17. Rewrite: □ + 9 = 17.
2
Solve: □ = 17 − 9 = 8.
✓ □ = 8
🔍 Check: 8 + 9 = 17 = 5 + 12 ✓
Worked Example 2
□ × 3 = 4 × 9 − 6
1
Simplify the right side (using order of operations — multiplication first): 4 × 9 = 36, then 36 − 6 = 30.
2
Rewrite: □ × 3 = 30.
3
Solve: □ = 30 ÷ 3 = 10.
✓ □ = 10
🔍 Check: 10 × 3 = 30 = 4 × 9 − 6 ✓
Worked Example 3
24 − □ = 3 × 6 + 1
1
Simplify the right side: 3 × 6 = 18, then 18 + 1 = 19.
2
Rewrite: 24 − □ = 19.
3
Solve (careful — non-standard position!): □ = 24 − 19 = 5.
✓ □ = 5
🔍 Check: 24 − 5 = 19 = 3 × 6 + 1 ✓

The Substitution Check Method

After solving any missing number question, you should always check your answer. This takes just a few seconds and can save you from losing easy marks.

⭐ How to Check: Substitution Replace the □ in the original question with your answer.
Work out both sides of the equation.
If both sides are equal, your answer is correct. ✓
If they don't match, you've made an error — go back and find it!
Check Example 1 — Substitution works
Original question: (□ × 3) + 2 = 11. We got □ = 3.
1
Replace □ with 3: (3 × 3) + 2
2
Work out the left side: 9 + 2 = 11.
3
Does 11 = 11? Yes! ✓
✓ Answer confirmed: □ = 3 is correct.
Check Example 2 — Catching an error
Original question: 20 − □ = 13. A student got □ = −7 (incorrect!).
1
Replace □ with −7: 20 − (−7) = 20 + 7 = 27.
2
Does 27 = 13? No! ✗ The check has caught the error.
3
Correct working: □ = 20 − 13 = 7. Check: 20 − 7 = 13 ✓.
✓ Correct answer: □ = 7
💡 Make checking a habit In GL Assessment, you only have a few minutes per question. A quick substitution check (5 seconds!) can turn a wrong answer into a right one. Always do it!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Here are the 6 mistakes students most often make on this topic in GL Assessment. Knowing these traps before the exam is like having a map of where the pitfalls are!

❌ Mistake 1: Reversing the subtraction direction in a − □ = b

When the unknown is being subtracted, students often subtract the wrong way and end up with a negative number that doesn't make sense.

✗ Wrong working
20 − □ = 13
□ = 13 − 20 = −7
✓ Correct working
20 − □ = 13
□ = 20 − 13 = 7
(Check: 20 − 7 = 13 ✓)
❌ Mistake 2: Skipping the substitution check

Students solve the problem and move on without checking. A quick 5-second check would have revealed the error!

✗ No check
□ × 8 = 56 → □ = 6
(No check done — wrong!)
✓ With check
□ = 56 ÷ 8 = 7
Check: 7 × 8 = 56 ✓
(Caught: 6 × 8 = 48 ≠ 56)
❌ Mistake 3: Undoing two-step operations in the wrong order

In a two-step problem, students undo the first operation first instead of the last. Always work backwards — undo the last operation first.

✗ Wrong order
(□ × 3) + 2 = 11
Undo ×3 first: □ = 11 ÷ 3 ≈ 3.67
Then undo +2: 3.67 − 2 = 1.67 ✗
✓ Correct order
Undo +2 first: 11 − 2 = 9
Undo ×3 next: 9 ÷ 3 = 3
(Check: (3 × 3) + 2 = 11 ✓)
❌ Mistake 4: Using the wrong inverse operation

Students apply the inverse of the wrong operation — for example, subtracting when they should divide.

✗ Wrong inverse
□ × 7 = 63
Inverse of ×7 is… −7?
□ = 63 − 7 = 56 ✗
✓ Correct inverse
Inverse of ×7 is ÷7
□ = 63 ÷ 7 = 9
(Check: 9 × 7 = 63 ✓)
❌ Mistake 5: Not simplifying both sides first when unknowns appear on both sides

Students try to solve immediately without simplifying the known side, and make arithmetic errors as a result.

✗ Rushing in
□ + 9 = 5 + 12
Tries to "undo" the right side piecemeal → confusion
✓ Simplify first
5 + 12 = 17
□ + 9 = 17
□ = 17 − 9 = 8 ✓
❌ Mistake 6: Working a function machine forwards when you need to go backwards

When asked to find the input (not the output), students apply the operations forwards instead of backwards with inverses.

✗ Going forwards
? → ×3 → −4 → 11
Student does: 11 × 3 − 4 = 29 ✗
✓ Going backwards
Start at 11, undo −4: +4 → 15
Undo ×3: ÷3 → 5
Input = 5. Check: 5×3−4=11 ✓

Question 4 — Function machine (find the input)

Question 5 — Word problem
Amara thinks of a number. She multiplies it by 5 and then subtracts 7. Her answer is 33. What was Amara's number?
Question 6 — Missing number with decimals
□ × 5 = 3.5. What is the value of □?
Question 7 — Unknown on both sides
□ + 14 = 3 × 8 + 2. What is □?
Question 8 — Non-standard division
56 ÷ □ = 7. What is the value of □?
Question 9 — Three-step chain word problem
Marcus thinks of a number, adds 3, doubles the result, and then subtracts 4. His final answer is 18. What was his number?
Question 10 — Function machine (find missing operation)
A function machine has: Input = 24 → [?] → Output = 6. Which operation does the machine perform?

📋 Full Answers & Explanations

Q1 — □ × 8 = 72
Answer: C) 9
Inverse of ×8 is ÷8. □ = 72 ÷ 8 = 9. Trap A: 8 gives 8×8=64≠72. Trap B: 64 is 8², not the answer here. Trap D: 576 = 72×8 (went the wrong direction).
Q2 — 30 − □ = 11
Answer: C) 19
□ = 30 − 11 = 19. Trap A: −19 comes from doing 11 − 30 (wrong direction). Trap B: 41 comes from 30 + 11 (adding instead of using the right method). Trap D: 21 is a simple arithmetic slip.
Q3 — (□ × 4) + 5 = 29
Answer: B) 6
Step 1: undo +5: 29 − 5 = 24. Step 2: undo ×4: 24 ÷ 4 = 6. Trap A: 8.5 comes from undoing ×4 first (29÷4) before dealing with +5 — wrong order. Check: (6×4)+5 = 29 ✓
Q4 — Function machine: ÷6 then +3 → output 8
Answer: C) 30
Work backwards: undo +3: 8 − 3 = 5. Undo ÷6: 5 × 6 = 30. Trap A: 66 comes from applying operations forwards (8×6+3). Trap D: 11 comes from only undoing the last step.
Q5 — Word problem: ×5 then −7 → 33
Answer: D) 8
Work backwards: undo −7: 33 + 7 = 40. Undo ×5: 40 ÷ 5 = 8. Trap C: 26 comes from only adding 7 then dividing by something else. Check: 8×5−7 = 33 ✓
Q6 — □ × 5 = 3.5
Answer: C) 0.7
□ = 3.5 ÷ 5 = 0.7. Think: 35 ÷ 5 = 7, so 3.5 ÷ 5 = 0.7. Trap B: 7 is the answer to 35 ÷ 5 (ignoring the decimal). Trap A: 17.5 comes from multiplying instead of dividing.
Q7 — □ + 14 = 3 × 8 + 2
Answer: C) 12
Simplify right side: 3×8 = 24, then 24+2 = 26. Solve: □ = 26 − 14 = 12. Trap D: 36 is 3×8+12 (adding 14 instead of subtracting). Check: 12+14 = 26 = 3×8+2 ✓
Q8 — 56 ÷ □ = 7
Answer: D) 8
Rearrange: □ × 7 = 56, so □ = 56 ÷ 7 = 8. Trap A: 49 = 7² (confusing the numbers). Trap C: 63 = 56+7. Check: 56 ÷ 8 = 7 ✓
Q9 — Word problem: +3, ×2, −4 → 18
Answer: C) 8
Work backwards: undo −4: 18+4 = 22. Undo ×2: 22÷2 = 11. Undo +3: 11−3 = 8. Check: (8+3)×2−4 = 11×2−4 = 22−4 = 18 ✓. Trap D: 5 comes from undoing in the wrong order.
Q10 — Function machine: 24 → [?] → 6
Answer: C) ÷ 4
24 ÷ 4 = 6 ✓. Trap A: 24 − 18 = 6 also works arithmetically, but GL Assessment prefers the multiplicative operation here since input and output are in a 4:1 ratio. Trap B: 24 × 4 = 96, not 6.
