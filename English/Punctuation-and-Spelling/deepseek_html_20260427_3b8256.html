📘 Colon [:] — The Announcer

🔹 Core rule: A colon comes AFTER a complete sentence. It announces: a list, an explanation, a quotation, or emphasis.

✅ 1. Colon before a list (complete sentence needed)

✔ We need three items: rope, duct tape, and a torch.
❌ I packed: socks, shoes, hat. (colon after verb – wrong)

✅ 2. Colon before an explanation / definition

The truth was unbearable: he had lost everything.

✅ 3. Colon before a quotation (critical for GL)

Shakespeare wrote: “All the world’s a stage.”

✅ 4. Colon in time, titles, and biblical references

The train leaves at 7:45. • Genesis 2:7 • Luke 4:18

⚠️ 5. The “NO colon after a verb or preposition” rule

❌ She wants: chocolate and vanilla. → ✔ She wants chocolate and vanilla.
❌ He is interested in: art and music. → ✔ He is interested in art and music.

🎯 6. Colon for emphasis (single word or phrase)

He knew the answer: victory.

🔹 Semicolon [;] — The Super Comma & Bridge

🔸 Golden test: Replace the semicolon with a full stop. If both sides are complete sentences, the semicolon works!

✅ 1. Between two related main clauses (no conjunction)

The sun set; the stars appeared.

⚡ 2. Semicolon + Conjunctive Adverb (MAJOR 11+ TRAP)

Pattern: Main clause; however / therefore / nevertheless / meanwhile / consequently / otherwise / furthermore , main clause.
✔ The traffic was terrible; however, we arrived on time.
✔ It was raining; therefore, the match was cancelled.

✅ 3. Semicolon in complex lists (super comma)

We visited Paris, France; Rome, Italy; and Berlin, Germany.

✅ 4. Elliptical constructions (rare but GL style)

Some voted for pizza; others, for burgers.

❌ The “no semicolon before a dependent clause” rule

❌ Although it rained; we played on. → ✔ Although it rained, we played on.

📝 Mixed Punctuation – Multiple Choice & Gap-Fill (GL Style)

Q1. I have only one ambition ___ to become a pilot.




✅ Correct: colon. "I have only one ambition" is a complete sentence introducing the explanation → colon.

Q2. The library is closed ___ we cannot return our books today.




✔ Semicolon or colon? Both possible but semicolon is neutral: two related main clauses. GL accepts either? colon works for explanation, but safest: semicolon (equal clauses). Correct answer: semicolon (;).

Q3. The race was exhausting ___ nevertheless, Maria finished first.




✔ Semicolon before conjunctive adverb "nevertheless". Correct: semicolon (;).

Q4. Which sentence is punctuated CORRECTLY?




✔ B) He said: “Let’s go.” Colon before quotation is standard GL style.

Q5. Insert a semicolon or colon: The recipe requires three ingredients ___ flour, sugar, and eggs.



✔ Colon. The first clause is a complete sentence introducing a list. Semicolon would be incorrect because second part is not an independent clause.

✍️ Rewriting + Error Spotting (exactly like GL papers)

✏️ Task 1 (Rewrite): Join these two sentences using a semicolon.
The storm damaged the roof. The family found shelter in the basement.

✔ The storm damaged the roof; the family found shelter in the basement.

✏️ Task 2 (Rewrite with conjunctive adverb): Combine using however + semicolon.
She studied diligently. She failed the exam.

✔ She studied diligently; however, she failed the exam.

🔍 Error Spotting: Find the punctuation error and correct it.
I love reading; but I have no time today.

❌ Semicolon before "but" is wrong because "but" is a conjunction. ✅ Correct: I love reading, but I have no time today.

🔍 Error Spotting (colon after verb trap):
We need: milk, bread, and cheese.

❌ Colon after verb "need". ✅ Correct: We need milk, bread, and cheese. (delete colon).

📌 Insert punctuation (colon + semicolon mixed):
The trip includes three cities Paris, France London, England and Rome, Italy however the schedule is tight.

✔ The trip includes three cities: Paris, France; London, England; and Rome, Italy; however, the schedule is tight.

🎯 Mark Scheme Insight – When Both Colon & Semicolon Work

In GL assessments, if two main clauses are connected and the second explains the first, both colon or semicolon can be acceptable.
✔ He was terrified: the shadow moved. (colon emphasizes explanation)
✔ He was terrified; the shadow moved. (semicolon shows link)
GL guidance: Colon is preferred for explanation/definition. But both often accepted unless question explicitly tests distinction.
