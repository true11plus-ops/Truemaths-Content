01
The Foundation
Question Type Framework

Every poetry question in a GL or grammar school exam belongs to one of three categories. Identifying the category tells you exactly how to construct your answer — and more importantly, what the examiner is actually looking for.

A. Evidence Questions — "Quote and Locate"

These ask you to find a specific word or phrase in the poem that demonstrates something. Your job is to select the most precise quotation — not the first line that comes to mind.

Find a phrase…locate + quote
Which word suggests…identify + quote
Write down a line…select evidence
Give an example of…locate a technique
Worked Example
"The sea snarled at the cliff face, dragging stones back into its throat."

Q: Find a phrase that suggests the sea is aggressive.

A: "snarled at the cliff face" — the verb "snarled" implies an animal's aggression, making the sea seem predatory and violent.

Strategy: Read the question, cover the poem, form your answer, then scan for the most precise evidence. Avoid copying whole stanzas — one powerful phrase is worth more than three vague lines.

B. Effect Questions — "Technique + Impact"

These are the most common and most rewarded question type. They ask why the poet made a specific choice. Never say a technique "makes it sound nice" or "makes it more interesting." Describe the precise emotional or sensory impact.

Why does the poet use…name + explain effect
What effect does…describe the impact
How does the poet create…technique → reader response
Explain the effect of…technique + feeling
Worked Example
"Silent, sliding snakes of smoke rose from the chimney."

Q: Why does the poet use alliteration in this line?

A: The repeated 's' sounds in "silent, sliding snakes" create a sinister, whispering atmosphere. The softness of the sound mirrors the slow, stealthy movement of the smoke, making it feel unsettling rather than peaceful.

C. Meaning Questions — "Inference, Deduction, Vocabulary in Context"

These ask what the poet means — not just what they say. You must interpret metaphors, infer attitudes, and explain vocabulary by using the surrounding lines as evidence.

What is meant by…interpret the image
What does this suggest…infer the meaning
What does X tell us about…deduce + explain
How does the poet feel about…infer tone + attitude
Worked Example
"The golden coin of the sun sank slowly below the hill."

Q: What is meant by the metaphor "the golden coin of the sun"?

A: This metaphor compares the sun to a coin to suggest that it is perfectly round, brilliantly bright, and incredibly precious or valuable. It implies the light of day is something rare and worth treasuring — which makes its "sinking" feel like a loss.

02
Systematic Deconstruction
The SLIFFT Framework (Revised)

SLIFFT is a systematic method for working through any unseen poem. Each letter prompts a specific analytical question. Work through the layers in order — the framework stops you from making vague comments and forces you to generate precise, examiner-ready observations.

S — Structure

Ask: How is the poem physically arranged, and what does that arrangement tell me?

Count the stanzas. Note whether they are equal in length — if they suddenly shorten or lengthen, that is a deliberate choice. Short lines create tension, speed, or fragmentation. Long, flowing lines can suggest ease, calm, or an overwhelming rush of feeling. Look also at whether the poem breaks rules: an irregular final stanza often signals a shift in the speaker's state of mind.

L — Language

Ask: What specific word choices carry the most weight?

Focus on powerful verbs first — they do the most work. Then look for emotive adjectives and nouns that cluster around a theme (semantic fields). Consider connotations: a word's associations beyond its dictionary meaning. "Thin" and "slender" mean similar things, but "thin" often implies fragility or poverty, while "slender" implies elegance. Poets choose words for their full weight.

I — Imagery

Ask: What mental picture is the poet creating, and why?

Imagery is any language that appeals to the senses — sight, sound, smell, taste, touch. This layer focuses on the content of comparisons and descriptions: what is being compared to what, and what qualities are being highlighted? A simile that compares grief to a stone in the chest is doing something quite different from one that compares it to a fog — the stone is heavy and permanent, the fog is diffuse and obscuring.

F — Form

Ask: Is this poem following a recognised form, and what rules is it keeping or breaking?

Form covers rhyme scheme (does it rhyme at all? regularly or irregularly?), metre (the rhythmic beat of syllables), and any named forms: sonnet (14 lines, turning point), ballad (tells a story, often ABCB rhyme), ode (extended praise), elegy (lament for the dead). When a poet chooses a strict form, it can create a sense of control or inevitability. When they break it, that break is almost always significant.

F — Feeling

Ask: What emotions does this poem produce in the reader, and how do they shift?

This is distinct from tone (the poet's attitude). Feeling is the reader's emotional experience — the mood the poem creates. A poem can be written with a cold, detached tone but produce a deeply unsettling feeling in the reader. Trace how the feeling changes across stanzas: does it move from hope to despair? from calm to sudden panic? Identifying the shift helps you locate the volta.

T — Tone

Ask: What is the poet's attitude toward their subject?

Tone is the voice behind the poem — what the poet seems to feel or think. It is separate from the content: two poets writing about the same war could have entirely different tones (bitter vs. heroic; ironic vs. solemn). Identify the tone in each stanza and notice if it changes — this often marks the volta. Use precise vocabulary: not "happy" but euphoric; not "sad" but elegiac or mournful.

+ Voice / Perspective

Ask: Who is speaking, to whom, and from what position?

Voice is the additional layer often missed by students. Consider: Is the poem in first person (I/we), second person (you), or third person (he/she/they)? First person creates intimacy; second person implicates the reader directly; third person can feel distant or observational. Also consider whether the speaker is reliable — do you trust their account? Some poets create a persona whose perspective we are meant to question.

Sonic Techniques
Sound Devices — Complete Section
The Core Principle: In poetry, sound should mirror meaning. The sounds a poet chooses create an atmosphere that works beneath the level of conscious understanding. When you comment on a sound device, always explain what the sound feels like and how that feeling matches what the poem is describing.

Alliteration

Definition: The repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of nearby words.

Example
"The bold, black bird broke from the branches."

Effects it can create:

  • Emphasis: draws the reader's attention to a key idea or image
  • Rhythm and musicality: creates a pleasing beat that makes lines memorable
  • Mood through sound: hard consonants (b, d, k, p) feel harsh and violent; soft consonants (l, m, n) feel gentle and soothing

Sibilance (a named subset of alliteration)

Definition: The repetition of 's', 'sh', and 'z' sounds — hissing sounds — in close proximity. Sibilance is a specific named technique and examiners reward naming it precisely.

Example
"Soft, sinister sounds slid across the surface of the swamp."

Effects it can create:

  • Menace and danger: the hissing sound associates with snakes, whispers, conspiracies
  • Softness and stealth: something moving quietly, perhaps deceptively
  • Unease: the sound itself can feel slippery or untrustworthy

Assonance

Definition: The repetition of the same vowel sound in nearby words (not necessarily the same letters — the same sound).

Example
"The eagling reaches for the bleaching sky." (long 'ee' sound repeated)

Effects it can create:

  • Musicality and internal rhyme: creates a musical quality without end rhyme
  • Pace control: long vowels (oo, ah, ee) slow the reader down; short vowels (i, u) speed up
  • Mood through sound: long, open sounds feel expansive or melancholic; closed sounds feel urgent or tight

Consonance

Definition: The repetition of consonant sounds within or at the end of words (not just at the start — that would be alliteration).

Example
"The black track backed into the dark." (repeated 'k' sound)

Effects it can create:

  • Texture and density: creates a rich, woven sonic quality
  • Harshness: repeated hard consonants can feel grating or brutal
  • Unity: links words across a line, binding images together

Onomatopoeia

Definition: A word whose sound imitates what it describes.

Example
"The bees buzzed; the brook babbled; somewhere a door slammed."

Effects it can create:

  • Immediacy: the sound makes the reader feel present in the scene
  • Vividness: activates the reader's auditory imagination
  • Contrast: a single jarring onomatopoeic word amid quiet description creates shock

Line and Pause
Enjambment & Caesura — Paired Teaching

Enjambment and caesura are opposite techniques: one keeps moving, the other stops. Both are deliberate structural choices, and both are regularly tested.

Enjambment

Definition: When a sentence or phrase continues from one line into the next without punctuation at the end of the line — the thought flows across the line break.

Example
"She walked through every door that opened
into corridors that seemed to lead
to other corridors, endlessly
folding inward."

Four distinct effects enjambment can create:

  • Speed and momentum: the reader is pulled forward without pause, creating breathless urgency
  • Ambiguity: a word at the end of an enjambed line temporarily holds two meanings before the next line resolves it. In the example, "lead" is briefly a verb (lead = guide) before "to other corridors" completes the thought.
  • Linked ideas: enjambment across a stanza break can connect two otherwise separate ideas or images
  • Mirroring loss of control: when a character is overwhelmed or panicking, enjambment mirrors that spilling-over quality — the thought cannot be contained within neat lines

Caesura

Definition: A deliberate pause within a line, usually created by punctuation (a full stop, comma, dash, or semicolon) in the middle of the line rather than at the end.

Example
"She stopped. The world kept moving.
He was gone; she stayed."

Three distinct effects caesura can create:

  • Pause for emphasis: the stop forces the reader to linger on what immediately precedes it, giving that word or phrase extra weight. "She stopped." is isolated and powerful.
  • Mimicking shock or hesitation: a sudden full stop mid-line captures the feeling of breath being knocked away — time momentarily halting
  • Creating contrast: a caesura can split a line into two opposing halves, placing contrasting ideas in tension with each other: "He was gone; she stayed."

05
The Turn
Volta / Turning Point — Full Section

The volta is one of the most important and most tested features of poetry at 11+ level. Missing it costs marks; identifying and explaining it earns them.

What Is the Volta?

The volta (Italian for "turn") is the moment in a poem when something shifts: the tone changes, the argument reverses, the mood darkens or lightens, or a new perspective is introduced. It is the poem's emotional turning point — the moment the poet changes direction.

Important: The volta is not always signalled by "But" or "Yet." It can be structural (a new stanza), tonal (the language suddenly changes register), or imagistic (a new metaphor replaces the previous one). Train yourself to ask: "Where does this poem change direction, and why?"

How to Identify the Volta

Look for any of these signals:

  • Connective words: but, yet, however, although, and yet, still, nevertheless
  • A change in tense: from past to present, or from present to future
  • A change in person: from "I" to "you," or from singular to plural
  • A change in tone vocabulary: words that shift from positive to negative connotations, or vice versa
  • A change in structure: a shorter or longer stanza, a change in rhyme scheme, a sudden use of caesura where there was enjambment before
  • A new image or symbol: the poem was using one extended metaphor; suddenly a different one appears

What to Explain When You Find the Volta

A complete volta analysis answers three questions:

  1. What changes? (tone, mood, argument, perspective, structure)
  2. How does it change? (the specific language or structural signal)
  3. Why does the shift matter? (what does it reveal about the poem's theme or the speaker's experience?)
Worked Example
"The garden in those years was full of bees,
warm bread, and the sound of my mother singing.
Summer lasted a century. The plums were heavy.
But I am writing this at fifty-two,
and the garden is a car park now,
and she has been gone twelve years."

Q: Where is the volta, and what changes?

A: The volta occurs at "But I am writing this at fifty-two" — the connective "But" signals the shift from past memory to present reality. The tone changes from nostalgic and sensory (bees, bread, singing) to bleak and factual (a car park, twelve years gone). The volta is also structural: the second part moves to the present tense. The shift matters because it reveals that the warmth of the first stanza exists only in memory — the contrast makes the loss feel devastating.

Rhyme Scheme — Identification Tutorial

How to Assign Letters to End Sounds

1
Read the last word of each line only. Write them in a vertical list.
2
Give the very first end sound the letter A.
3
If the next line's end sound rhymes with A, it is also A. If it is new, call it B.
4
Continue: each new sound gets the next letter; matching sounds keep the same letter.
5
Repeat the pattern for each stanza.
Step-by-Step Example
"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? → A
Thou art more lovely and more temperate: → B
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May, → A
And summer's lease hath all too short a date." → B

End sounds: day (A) / temperate (B) / May (A) / date (B) → Scheme: ABAB

Beat and Stress
Rhythm & Metre — Primer

What Is Metre?

Metre is the pattern of stressed (/) and unstressed (×) syllables in a line of poetry. When you read a poem aloud, some syllables naturally receive more emphasis — these are stressed. The pattern these stresses create is the metre.

Reading for Stress

"She WALKED / through FIELDS / of GOLD- / en GRAIN" — the stressed syllables are capitalised. Notice they fall in a regular pattern: unstressed-STRESSED, unstressed-STRESSED, unstressed-STRESSED, unstressed-STRESSED. This pattern has a name.

Iambic Pentameter

An iamb is the most common unit of metre in English poetry: one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed syllable (da-DUM). Pentameter means five of these units per line (penta = five). So iambic pentameter = 10 syllables in a da-DUM × 5 pattern.

Classic Example
"Shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY?"
× / × / × / × / × /
5 iambs = iambic pentameter

Why poets use it: iambic pentameter closely mirrors the natural rhythm of English speech — it sounds like a heartbeat (da-DUM, da-DUM). This makes it feel both formal and natural simultaneously. Shakespeare used it to show characters speaking eloquently but believably.

Trochaic Inversion

A trochee is the opposite of an iamb: stressed then unstressed (DUM-da). When a poet places a trochee at the start of an otherwise iambic line, this is called a trochaic inversion — also called a "trochaic substitution." It is always deliberate.

Example
"RUN-ning THROUGH the DARK be-FORE the DAWN"
The first foot is stressed-unstressed (RUN-ning) — a trochee where an iamb was expected.

Effect: A trochaic inversion at the start of a line creates sudden energy and emphasis — the first word is hit hard, creating urgency or surprise. It can also signal a moment of emotional intensity where the speaker's control breaks down.

Why a Poet Might Disrupt Their Own Metre

  • To create emphasis: the disruption draws attention to the word or moment that breaks the pattern
  • To mirror emotional breakdown: a character losing control may be represented by a metre that loses control
  • To mark the volta: a rhythmic shift can signal the turning point of the poem
  • To create unease: a regular pattern that suddenly fails feels unsettling, much like a heartbeat that skips

Two Distinct Concepts
Mood vs. Tone — The Critical Distinction

The Key Difference

Tone is the poet's attitude toward their subject — what the poet thinks or feels. It is present in the voice behind the poem: their word choices, level of formality, use of irony.

Mood is the emotional atmosphere the poem creates in the reader — how it makes you feel while reading. Tone and mood are not the same thing and they can pull in different directions.

How They Can Differ

A poet writing about a war atrocity might use a deliberately clinical, detached tone — recording facts without visible emotion, as if filing a report. Yet this very coldness creates an unsettling, disturbing mood in the reader. The absence of emotion in the poet's voice makes the horror feel more total, not less.

Another example: a poet writes about death with a sardonic, almost amused tone — yet the subject matter creates a melancholic mood in the reader, even if the poet seems untroubled.

Word Clusters
Semantic Field — Taught and Practised

What Is a Semantic Field?

A semantic field is a cluster of words in a poem that all relate to the same theme or concept. Identifying it is a high-value analytical move because it shows you can see the poem's thematic architecture, not just its surface content.

Why poets create semantic fields:

  • To establish mood: a cluster of words from one domain (darkness, cold, silence) builds the poem's atmosphere systematically
  • To reinforce theme: if the poem is about war, a field of battle-related words threads the theme through every stanza
  • To create unity: semantic fields bind the poem together; the reader feels the theme is everywhere, not just in an obvious line
Why examiners reward this: Commenting on a semantic field shows you can analyse the poem as a whole, not just line by line. It demonstrates sophisticated thinking about how poets construct meaning over the full text.

Three Worked Examples

Example 1 — Semantic Field of War
"The orders came like artillery fire. She was ambushed by questions. Her composure retreated. By evening, she had surrendered."

Identification: artillery, ambushed, retreated, surrendered — all words from the field of military conflict

Comment: The semantic field of warfare throughout this extract transforms an ordinary difficult meeting into a battle. This suggests the speaker experiences professional life as a constant fight for survival — always under attack, always losing ground.

Example 2 — Semantic Field of Decay
"The summer rotted slowly into autumn. The leaves fell, riddled and brown. The garden grew hollow. The light thinned and withdrew."

Identification: rotted, riddled, hollow, thinned, withdrew — words of decomposition and absence

Comment: The semantic field of decay runs through the entire stanza. Rather than celebrating autumn's colours, the poet frames the season as a form of corruption — reinforcing a theme of inevitable loss and the death of summer's joys.

Example 3 — Semantic Field of Music
"Her words had a rhythm I had not expected. They arrived in cadences, paused, swelled again like a chorus, and left me in silence — an audience of one."

Identification: rhythm, cadences, chorus, audience — words from music and performance

Comment: By using a semantic field of music to describe speech, the poet elevates the woman's words to the level of art. Her conversation becomes a performance — suggesting the narrator is captivated, passive, and perhaps overwhelmed by her presence.

Full Worked Poem Analysis

Below is William Blake's "The Sick Rose" (1794) — a short, dense poem ideal for 11+ analysis. Read it first for sense, then study the SLIFFT annotations alongside.

William Blake — "The Sick Rose" (1794)

O Rose, thou art sick!
The invisible worm
That flies in the night,
In the howling storm,

Has found out thy bed
Of crimson joy,
And his dark secret love
Does thy life destroy.
O Rose, thou art sick!
The invisible worm
That flies in the night,
In the howling storm,

Has found out thy bed
Of crimson joy,
And his dark secret love
Does thy life destroy.
Structure (S) Two equal stanzas of four short lines. The brevity creates intensity — each word carries enormous weight. The short lines feel like short, ragged breaths, creating a sense of dread.
Language (L) Powerful contrasting words: "crimson joy" vs. "dark secret love." The verb "destroy" is placed last in the poem — its position gives it devastating finality. "Invisible" and "secret" belong to a semantic field of concealment.
Imagery (I) The rose is a conventional symbol of beauty, love, perfection. The worm is associated with death, corruption, the serpent (evil). The image of the worm "finding" the rose's bed merges violation with corruption.
Form (F) The rhyme scheme is ABCB / ABCB — ballad rhyme. This gives the poem a deceptively simple, song-like feel, making the darkness of the content more shocking. The metre is irregular, creating unease.
Feeling (F) Stanza 1: ominous, threatening. Stanza 2: the ominous threat becomes a reality — the mood turns harrowing. The poem leaves the reader with a sense of irreversible corruption.
Tone (T) The opening apostrophe ("O Rose") is direct, urgent — almost panicked. The tone is elegiac: Blake mourns the rose's destruction. There is also a quality of moral outrage: the worm's "secret love" is presented as predatory.
Voice (V) First person implied — the poet addresses the rose directly (apostrophe). This creates intimacy and grief. The poet positions himself as a witness to the rose's destruction, unable to prevent it.
Model Examiner Comment — Symbolism

The rose is a symbol of natural beauty, innocence, and love — and it is conventionally linked to joy and perfection. By introducing the worm, which carries connotations of decay, death, and the biblical serpent, Blake places corruption at the very heart of beauty. The poem suggests that beauty is always vulnerable to hidden, "secret" forces of destruction — that nothing perfect can survive. This is reinforced by "crimson joy": crimson is the colour of both pleasure and blood, suggesting that joy always contains the seeds of its own destruction.

Written Response Structure — P.E.E.L. for Poetry

For schools requiring written answers (not multiple choice), every paragraph of your poetry response should follow the P.E.E.L. structure. Poetry P.E.E.L. differs from prose P.E.E.L. in one key way: the Evidence step must include the name of the technique used.

The Poetry P.E.E.L. Framework

P — Point: Make a clear claim about what the poet does or how they make you feel. This is your topic sentence.

E — Evidence: Quote the relevant word or phrase. Then name the technique. Both are required.

E — Effect: Explain the specific impact of that technique on the reader — the precise emotion or idea it creates.

L — Link: Connect your comment to the poem's wider theme, or link forward to your next point. This earns the final marks.

Model Answer: "How does Blake create a sense of menace in 'The Sick Rose'?"

Full Model Response

[Point] Blake creates menace through his use of language that suggests concealment and predatory intent.

[Evidence + Technique] He describes the worm as "invisible" and acting with "dark secret love" — the adjectives "invisible" and "secret" create a semantic field of concealment, while "dark" has connotations of evil and threat.

[Effect] This combination makes the worm feel predatory rather than merely present — it hides, it seeks, it acts in the night where it cannot be seen. The reader feels the menace of a threat that cannot be confronted or identified, which is more frightening than open danger.

[Link] This sense of hidden corruption reinforces the poem's wider theme: that beauty and innocence are always vulnerable to forces they cannot perceive, let alone resist.

Exam Conditions
Unseen Poem Strategy — Step by Step

An unseen poem is one you have never encountered before. It appears in the exam with no prior preparation possible. The following method, used consistently, will ensure you always have something precise and intelligent to say.

Read 1 — Sense and Subject (2 minutes)

Read the poem through completely without stopping. Ask: What is literally happening here? Who is the speaker? What situation are they in? What does the title tell you? Do not write anything yet. Just understand the surface narrative.

Title check: The title is the poet's first choice — it is never accidental. A title like "The Veteran" immediately tells you the poem will be about someone with experience of war. A title like "Photograph" tells you time, memory, and loss may feature. Always read the title before the poem.

Read 2 — Craft (3 minutes)

Read again, this time pencil in hand. Look for the poet's choices. Annotate in this order:

1
Circle the most powerful verb in each stanza. Ask: why this verb, not a safer one?
2
Underline every comparison (simile or metaphor) and note what quality is being highlighted.
3
Mark end sounds and identify the rhyme scheme quickly: does it rhyme? Regularly or irregularly?
4
Look for the volta. Where does the poem change direction? Mark it.
5
Note the tone of each stanza in the margin with one word (e.g. "angry," "tender," "ominous").
6
Check for sound devices: any alliteration, sibilance, assonance, or onomatopoeia that links to the poem's mood.

Handling Difficult Vocabulary

If a word is archaic or unfamiliar, do not panic. Use these strategies in order:

  1. Context: what do the surrounding lines suggest the word means?
  2. Root: can you break the word into parts? (e.g. "indomitable" — "in" = not, "domit" from "dominate" = so something that cannot be dominated = unconquerable)
  3. Connotation: even if you are unsure of the exact meaning, what feeling does the word carry? (Long, Latinate words often feel formal or grand; short, Anglo-Saxon words feel direct or harsh)

Timing Guidance

Read 1 (sense/narrative)~2 min
Read 2 (craft/annotation)~3 min
Answer evidence questions~5 min
Answer effect/meaning questions~8 min
Check and review~2 min
Total~20 min
