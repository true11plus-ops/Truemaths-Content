① The I.E.E. Framework

Every extended language analysis answer — whether it is two sentences or a full paragraph — must contain all three of the following components. Missing even one will cost you marks.

I
Identify
Name the technique. Be precise: "personification" not "language."
E
Evidence
Quote the specific word or phrase from the text. Keep it short — a few words, not a whole sentence.
E
Explain
Explain exactly what this does to the reader's mind. Say: "This makes the reader feel / think / picture..." and connect to the writer's purpose.

The Golden Rule

Naming the technique alone earns zero marks for explanation questions. The examiner needs to know what the technique does — to the reader, in this specific context, given what the writer is trying to achieve.

② Question Types & Mark Allocation

GL Assessment papers contain different question types that require very different lengths and depths of response. Recognising which type you are answering is crucial — over-answering a one-mark question wastes time; under-answering a three-mark question loses marks.

One-Mark: Technique Identification

Question wording: "Identify a technique used in paragraph 2." / "Name the literary device in line 7."

What to write: One or two words only. Name the technique. You do not need to explain anything.

Example answer: "Personification."
— Full marks. That's all that's needed.
Three-Mark: Extended Language Analysis

Question wording: "How does the writer use language to describe the storm?" / "Explain the effect of the phrase 'iron bones' on the reader."

What to write: A full paragraph: technique + quote + detailed effect linked to writer's purpose.

Must include: Named technique · Short quote · Effect on reader (at least 2 sentences) · Link to mood or purpose.

③ Atmosphere vs Viewpoint Questions

These two question types look similar but are asking for very different things. Students who confuse them lose marks on both.

Atmosphere / Mood QuestionWriter's Viewpoint Question
What it asksHow does the writer create a specific feeling, atmosphere, or mood?What does the writer think or believe about the topic? How do they reveal their opinion?
FocusThe reader's emotional experience of the textThe writer's personal attitude or bias
Key question words"Atmosphere," "mood," "feeling," "how does the reader feel""Viewpoint," "opinion," "attitude," "perspective," "what does the writer think"

Example: Atmosphere Question

Question: How does the writer create a sense of menace in this extract? [3 marks]

Model answer: The writer uses personification in the phrase "the shadows crept towards the door," giving the darkness a deliberate, predatory movement. This makes the reader feel as though the house itself is threatening — as if the dark is hunting the character. The short sentence that follows, "Silence," amplifies this unease by cutting off sound entirely, leaving the reader in the same suspended, breathless state as the character. Together, these techniques build a tense, menacing atmosphere that makes the reader feel unsafe and alert.

Example: Viewpoint Question

Question: What is the writer's viewpoint on urban development? How do you know? [3 marks]

Model answer: The writer holds a critical viewpoint toward urban development. This is revealed through their choice of the verb "gouged" to describe how construction has changed the landscape — a word normally associated with pain and violence, which suggests the writer sees development as destructive rather than progressive. They further reveal their bias through selection of detail: they describe only what has been lost (open fields, hedgerows, birdsong) without acknowledging any benefits. The rhetorical question "How long before there is nothing left?" confirms their alarm, implying the answer is obvious — that this level of destruction cannot continue.

④ Imagery Techniques

These techniques help the reader visualise or feel something vividly. They work by connecting the unknown to the known.

Simile
A comparison using "like" or "as" — explicitly stating the resemblance.
Effect: Creates a precise, vivid mental image by borrowing qualities from something familiar. The more unexpected the comparison, the more striking the image.
"The old woman's hands were like knotted driftwood — pale, worn, and shaped by years of invisible tide."
Student model: The writer uses a simile, comparing the woman's hands to "knotted driftwood." This makes the reader picture something weathered, pale, and shaped by forces beyond the person's control — suggesting a life of hard, invisible labour.
Metaphor
States that one thing IS another — a direct comparison without "like" or "as." More powerful than simile because there is no distance between the two things.
Effect: Forces the reader to see the subject through the qualities of the comparison. The stronger the metaphor, the more it changes how the reader understands the subject.
"The classroom was a battlefield by midday — desks overturned, voices raised, no corner left untouched."
Student model: The writer uses a metaphor, calling the classroom "a battlefield." This makes the reader picture chaos and conflict as if it were warfare, suggesting that the situation has escalated completely beyond normal order.
Personification
Giving human qualities, emotions, or actions to something non-human: objects, animals, nature, or abstract ideas.
Effect: Makes the non-human feel alive, purposeful, or even threatening. It creates empathy or tension depending on the quality assigned.
"The old house groaned with each footstep, as if protesting the intrusion."
Student model: The writer uses personification, saying the house "groaned" and "protesting." This gives the building a sense of discomfort and agency — making the reader feel as though the house itself is hostile to visitors, which builds a threatening atmosphere.
Pathetic Fallacy
When the weather or natural environment reflects or amplifies a character's mood or the atmosphere of a scene.
Effect: The world becomes an extension of the character's inner state. This deepens the reader's emotional connection to the character's feelings and makes the atmosphere feel inevitable.
"As she received the news, the sky split open and rain hammered the windows without mercy."
Student model: The writer uses pathetic fallacy — the violent storm mirrors the character's shock and grief. The word "hammered" emphasises relentlessness, suggesting that even the natural world is responding to her devastation, which amplifies the reader's sense of her pain.

⑤ Sound Techniques

Sound techniques use the music of language — the physical sensation of words in the mouth and ear — to create atmosphere and texture.

Alliteration
The repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of nearby words.
Effect: Creates rhythm and musicality, making phrases memorable and highlighting the words that share the sound. The type of consonant matters: harsh sounds create aggression; soft sounds create calm.
"The bold, brutal bear broke through the barrier."
Student model: The writer uses alliteration with the "b" sound. The harsh, plosive repetition of "bold, brutal" and "broke" creates a sense of unstoppable power and aggression — the reader almost hears the force of the bear's movement.
Sibilance
A specific form of alliteration using the "s," "sh," or soft "c" sounds repeatedly.
Effect: Creates a hushed, hissing, or sinister quality. Often used for secretive, threatening, or snake-like descriptions. The sound itself feels slippery and untrustworthy.
"The snake slid silently across the stones, seeking shelter."
Student model: The writer uses sibilance — the repeated "s" sound across "slid silently...stones, seeking shelter" — to mimic the sound of the snake moving. This creates an eerie, whispered quality that makes the reader feel unsettled, as if the snake is close by and unseen.
Onomatopoeia
Words that sound like the noise they describe: crash, hiss, murmur, crackle, buzz.
Effect: Places the reader inside the physical world of the text by recreating real sounds. Makes descriptions immediate and sensory rather than abstract.
"The fire crackled and spat, and the logs hissed as the sap escaped."
Student model: The writer uses onomatopoeia — "crackled," "spat," and "hissed" all recreate the actual sounds of burning wood. This places the reader beside the fire, making the scene feel immediate and real rather than merely described.

⑥ Structural Techniques

These techniques concern how the writer arranges words and sentences on the page — the shape and rhythm of the writing itself.

Rule of Three (Tricolon)
Grouping three words, phrases, or clauses together in a series.
Effect: Feels rhythmically complete and emphasises the listed qualities. Three items feel comprehensive; fewer feel insufficient; more feel excessive.
"The room was cold, dark, and utterly silent."
Student model: The rule of three in "cold, dark, and utterly silent" emphasises the room's emptiness from three different angles — temperature, light, and sound — making the isolation feel total and inescapable to the reader.
Short Sentences
Sentences of only a few words — sometimes just one.
Effect: Creates tension, drama, or shock. The sudden change from longer sentences stops the reader dead — mimicking a physical jolt of surprise or fear.
"She ran down the corridor. Turned left. Pushed the door. Nothing."
Student model: The short sentences create a rapid, breathless pace that mirrors the character's panicked running. Each sentence is a beat — and the final one-word sentence "Nothing" stops everything, hitting the reader with the same sudden, hollow shock the character experiences.
Juxtaposition
Placing two contrasting things directly side by side to highlight the difference between them.
Effect: Makes the contrast between the two things more striking. Forces the reader to see both things more clearly because of what they are placed next to.
"A single bright daffodil grew from a crack in the bombed-out wall."
Student model: The writer juxtaposes the bright, living daffodil against the destruction of the bombed wall. This contrast makes both elements more powerful — the flower's resilience seems miraculous precisely because of the devastation surrounding it.
Contrast
Highlighting the difference between two things — more broadly than juxtaposition, which requires physical side-by-side placement.
Effect: Emphasises the gap between two ideas, times, or states. Often used to show change, hypocrisy, or inequality.
"Where there had once been laughter, there was now only the echo of it."
Student model: The writer contrasts the past (laughter) with the present (only its echo), which highlights the depth of what has been lost. The word "echo" is itself a fading repetition — reinforcing how the joy has been reduced to a ghost of itself.

⑦ Rhetorical & Persuasive Techniques

Rhetorical Question
A question that does not expect an answer — the answer is already implied.
Effect: Engages the reader directly; forces them to mentally formulate the implied answer, which makes them feel as though they arrived at the writer's conclusion independently.
"How many more children must suffer before we act?"
Student model: The rhetorical question "How many more children must suffer?" does not expect an answer — it implies the answer is already too many. This forces the reader to confront their own sense of moral urgency, making the call for action feel like the reader's own conclusion rather than the writer's demand.
Hypophora
The writer asks a question AND immediately answers it themselves.
Effect: Controls the reader's thinking completely — the question creates curiosity, and the immediate answer satisfies it on the writer's terms. Prevents the reader from forming a different conclusion.
"Why do libraries matter? Because they are the one place where knowledge belongs to everyone."
Student model: The writer uses hypophora — asking "Why do libraries matter?" and immediately answering it. This controls the reader's response: the question creates engagement, then the answer guides them to the writer's specific conclusion before they can form their own.
Anaphora
Repeating the same word or phrase at the start of successive sentences or clauses.
Effect: Builds rhythm, intensity, and cumulative emotional power. Each repeated phrase adds weight to the next. Often used in speeches for maximum impact.
"We will not rest. We will not falter. We will not surrender."
Student model: The writer uses anaphora, repeating "We will not" three times. Each repetition adds weight and determination — by the third clause, the reader feels the absolute resolution of the speaker. The structure also links all three clauses, making the commitment feel unified and unbreakable.
Direct Address
Using "you" or "we" to speak directly to the reader, making them part of the text.
Effect: Creates a personal connection; the reader cannot remain detached. "We" creates shared identity and responsibility; "you" creates individual accountability.
"You have seen the news. You know what is happening. Now it is time to act."
Student model: The writer uses direct address, repeating "You" at the start of each sentence. This implicates the reader personally — they cannot pretend this is happening to someone else. The effect is to make inaction feel like a personal moral failure.
Repetition
Deliberately repeating a word, phrase, or idea for emphasis (not necessarily at the start of sentences).
Effect: Drives the repeated idea into the reader's mind with increasing force. The more times something is repeated, the more it feels central and inarguable.
"The waiting was agony. Every hour was agony. Every minute was a new kind of agony."
Student model: The repetition of "agony" escalates the character's suffering with each iteration — it moves from the hour to the minute, showing the pain intensifying rather than fading, and the reader feels trapped in this endless discomfort alongside them.
Emotive Language
Words chosen specifically to provoke a strong emotional reaction in the reader.
Effect: Bypasses rational analysis and appeals directly to feeling — sympathy, fear, outrage, or hope. The more specific and concrete the emotive language, the stronger the effect.
"The defenceless child crouched alone in the rubble of what had been her home."
Student model: The writer uses emotive language — "defenceless," "alone," and "rubble" — to build maximum sympathy. "Defenceless" highlights vulnerability; "alone" isolates the child; "rubble" replaces the word "home," emphasising destruction. Together, they make the reader feel outrage and sorrow.

⑧ Further Techniques

Hyperbole
Deliberate, extreme exaggeration — never meant to be taken literally.
Effect: Amplifies emotion or urgency; conveys the scale of a feeling rather than a fact. Often creates dark humour or highlights absurdity.
"He had been standing in that queue for approximately ten thousand years."
Student model: The hyperbole "ten thousand years" exaggerates the wait to absurd proportions, conveying the character's frustrated sense that time itself has slowed. The humour makes the frustration vivid without requiring literal description.
Oxymoron
Two contradictory words placed directly together to create a paradoxical effect.
Effect: Creates a sense of complexity, contradiction, or confusion. Suggests that the subject cannot be captured in simple terms — it is two opposite things simultaneously.
"The deafening silence after the explosion was almost worse than the noise itself."
Student model: The oxymoron "deafening silence" places two contradictions together — silence cannot be loud, and yet the phrase captures the overwhelming, ringing emptiness that follows extreme sound. This paradox makes the reader feel the strangeness of the moment more powerfully than either word alone could.
Irony
When the literal meaning is the opposite of the intended meaning — or when events turn out contrary to expectations.
Effect: Creates distance between what is said and what is meant, inviting the reader to be "in on it." Can be humorous, bitter, or cutting depending on context.
"The health inspector who condemned the restaurant was himself found eating there the next day."
Student model: The irony of the health inspector eating at the restaurant he condemned reveals hypocrisy — the person enforcing standards fails to uphold them. This makes the reader feel a mixture of amusement and contempt, which reinforces any critical viewpoint the writer holds toward authority figures.
Symbolism
When an object, colour, or image is used to represent an abstract idea beyond its literal meaning.
Effect: Adds depth and layers of meaning. A reader who spots the symbol understands the text on two levels — the literal and the metaphorical.
"She left the key on the table and walked out without looking back."
Student model: The key is symbolic — keys represent access, belonging, and ownership. Leaving it behind symbolises a permanent departure, a surrender of everything the house represented. The detail "without looking back" reinforces her decision, and the reader understands she is severing more than a tenancy.
Euphemism
A mild or indirect expression used in place of something harsh, blunt, or uncomfortable.
Effect: Reveals what the speaker is trying to avoid saying — the choice of softer language makes the reader aware of the reality being obscured. Often used to reveal bias, evasion, or hypocrisy.
"The council announced that the library would be 'rationalised' by the end of the year."
Student model: The euphemism "rationalised" replaces "closed" — a word the council clearly wishes to avoid. The word choice reveals that they are aware closing the library is unpopular and are attempting to make the decision sound reasonable and bureaucratic rather than harmful.

⑨ Word-Level Analysis

Not every technique has a name. Some of the strongest exam answers are built on the analysis of a single word — a verb, an adjective, or a noun — without naming any literary device at all.

Connotation

Every word carries not just a definition but a set of associations — feelings, images, and social meanings that come with it. These are its connotations.

"Home" and "house" both describe a building — but "home" carries connotations of warmth, safety, belonging, and family. "House" is neutral: just bricks and windows. A writer who chooses "home" is doing something very different from a writer who chooses "house."

Rule: Ask yourself: what feelings, images, or social associations does this word bring? Why did the writer choose this word rather than a simpler or more neutral option?

Register

Register is the level of formality of language choices. A writer who uses formal language signals authority, seriousness, or distance. A writer who uses informal language signals intimacy, accessibility, or perhaps condescension.

FormalInformalEffect of the Contrast
"The individual declined to comment.""He wouldn't say anything."Formal creates distance and authority; informal creates immediacy and personality.
"She is fatigued.""She's exhausted." / "She's shattered.""Fatigued" is clinical; "shattered" carries an image of breaking, making the tiredness feel more physical.
"The matter is under consideration.""We're thinking about it."Formal language often obscures meaning; informality exposes it.

Diction: Analysing Single Words

This is the most important skill for top-mark answers. You do not need a technique name to analyse a word — you just need to ask: what does this specific word do that another word would not?

Compare these verbs:

"walked" — neutral; simply describes movement. No emotional colouring.
"trudged" — implies heaviness, exhaustion, reluctance. The reader feels the weight in every step.
"crept" — implies stealth, fear, or deliberate quietness. The reader senses secrecy or danger.
"stormed" — implies anger, force, and speed. The reader sees aggression and urgency.

All four words describe someone moving through a space. But each one creates a completely different picture and emotional tone.

Compare these colour adjectives:

"red" — neutral; just a colour.
"crimson" — darker, richer, more violent; associated with blood, danger, passion. Feels deliberate and intense.
"scarlet" — brighter, associated with shame (Scarlet Letter) and vivid displays. Feels theatrical.
"ruby" — warm, precious, associated with gems and wealth. Feels romantic or valuable.

When a writer writes "crimson" instead of "red," they are making a choice — and that choice carries meaning.

Worked Word-Level Examples

Extract: "The child clutched her teddy bear as the ambulance doors closed."

Analysis: The verb "clutched" carries connotations of desperate, fearful holding — as if something precious might be taken away. A neutral verb like "held" would suggest calm security; "clutched" suggests the child is terrified and is using the bear as a lifeline against the overwhelming situation. The reader feels the depth of her fear through this single verb choice.

Extract: "The politician described the policy as a bold step forward."

Analysis: "Bold" carries connotations of courage, confidence, and decisiveness — all positive qualities. However, in the context of a policy that many citizens opposed, the word choice reveals spin: the politician is using a word that sounds heroic to frame a controversial decision as brave leadership rather than recklessness. The connotation does the persuasive work that a straightforward defence could not.

Extract: "Where the mansion once stood, there was nothing."

Analysis: "Mansion" carries connotations of wealth, status, and excess — far beyond "house" or "home." By using this specific noun, the writer ensures the reader imagines something grand and valuable being destroyed. The loss is magnified precisely because of what the building represented — power and permanence — and "nothing" is made more devastating by contrast.

⑩ Weak vs Strong: Worked Examples

Read the following extract, then compare the two responses below. The difference between them is the difference between one mark and three marks.

"The factory had eaten this town alive. Its chimneys were black fingers pointing accusations at a sky it had permanently stained. The workers filed in at dawn — grey, silent, hunched — and the gates swallowed them without ceremony, as if they had never existed at all."

Question: How does the writer use language to present the factory? Use evidence from the extract to support your answer. [3 marks]

⚠ Weak Response — Likely to score 0–1 mark

"The writer uses personification when they say the factory 'eaten this town alive.' This is effective. The writer uses lots of good imagery. There is also a simile when the chimneys are compared to 'black fingers.' This is a good description."

Annotations: "This is effective" — no explanation of what effect. "Lots of good imagery" — vague and unspecific. "This is a good description" — the examiner does not care if you like it; explain what it does to the reader.

✓ Strong Response — Full marks

The writer uses personification TECHNIQUE throughout the extract to present the factory as predatory and alive. The opening metaphor "eaten this town alive" EVIDENCE gives the factory the qualities of a creature that consumes — suggesting it has destroyed not just buildings but the community's entire way of life. EFFECT This makes the reader feel a sense of violation, as if the town were helpless prey rather than a place of human habitation. LINKS TO PURPOSE

The chimneys as "black fingers pointing accusations" EVIDENCE extends this personification, making the factory appear morally aware of its own damage — and defiant about it. EFFECT The word "accusations" implies the factory knows it has wronged the town, which adds a layer of bitter irony: the thing causing harm is presented as though it is blaming others for it. ⑪ Unseen Extract & Practice Questions

Read the extract carefully twice before attempting any question. Pay attention to specific word choices, imagery, and the writer's overall purpose.

The market had been there for three hundred years. Not the same stalls, not the same faces — but the same stone flags, worn smooth as worry beads by generations of feet, and the same particular noise: a human tide of voices bargaining, laughing, arguing, greeting. It was the kind of noise that only a living place makes.

Now the council's decision sat like a stone in the chest of the town. The new development would "revitalise" the area, they said. It would bring "investment" and "footfall." The language was smooth and careful, each word chosen to slide past resistance. But nobody who had ever stood in the market on a cold Tuesday morning, hands wrapped around a paper cup of tea while a stranger's dog investigated their shopping bag, could read these words without a hollow, sinking sensation.

The traders had operated here in some cases for four decades. Mrs Farida, who sold spices and called everyone "love," had taught half the town how to cook. Old Tom, whose vintage tools occupied an entire corner, had salvaged memories that would otherwise have rusted into nothing. The market was not footfall. It was not investment. It was a place where the city came to remember that it was made of people, not of profit.

Q1 · One Mark · Technique Identification

Q2 · Two Marks · Effect Explanation

Modelled Top-Mark Responses

Model Answer — Q3 (3 marks)

The writer reveals a critical viewpoint toward the council through the sustained use of euphemism TECHNIQUE in paragraph two. The words "revitalise," "investment," and "footfall" EVIDENCE are placed in inverted commas, signalling to the reader that these are the council's words — not the writer's. This creates immediate irony: the reader is invited to distrust the very language being used, and the inverted commas become a kind of silent editorial comment. EFFECT The phrase "smooth and careful, each word chosen to slide past resistance" EVIDENCE uses the metaphor of language as something physically slippery to suggest the council is deliberately evasive — they are not communicating but performing communication, choosing words that prevent opposition rather than enable understanding. This confirms the writer's viewpoint: VIEWPOINT LINK the council is presented as calculating and disingenuous, treating the community's concerns as obstacles to be avoided rather than objections to be addressed.

Throughout the passage, the writer builds a portrait of the market as irreplaceable human infrastructure through contrasting language registers. TECHNIQUE: ANAPHORA + CONTRAST The repetition builds momentum, and the final clause lands with the force of a conclusion: "made of people, not of profit." EFFECT ON READER The reader is left feeling that something irreplaceable is being lost, and that the language used to justify its loss is itself part of the injustice. VIEWPOINT LINK
