① The P.I.E.E. Framework

Every text you encounter in an 11+ exam has been written for a reason. The P.I.E.E. framework gives you a reliable method for identifying that reason. Every text falls into one of four categories — sometimes more than one at once.

P
Persuade
To convince the reader to take action or agree with an opinion.
Adverts · opinion columns · letters of complaint · campaign leaflets
I
Inform
To give factual information about a topic without taking a strong side.
News reports · encyclopaedia entries · recipes · instruction manuals
E
Entertain
To provide enjoyment through vivid description, characters, or story.
Novels · short stories · poems · travel writing · humorous columns
E
Explain
To make a complex process or idea accessible and easy to understand.
Science textbooks · how-to guides · explainer articles · leaflets
Key Insight
Many texts have more than one purpose. A charity advertisement both informs you about a problem and persuades you to donate. When this happens, identify the primary purpose — the dominant reason the writer produced the text.

② Identifying the Author's Viewpoint

The viewpoint (or perspective) is the author's personal opinion or attitude. Even in texts that appear neutral, writers reveal their bias through the language they choose. Your job is to act like a detective — finding the clues hidden in plain sight.

The Four Clue Types

1. Adjectives & Adverbs

Example
Compare: "the grand and historic building" vs "the imposing and crumbling building."
Both describe the same place — but the word choices reveal very different viewpoints. Grand = admiration. Crumbling = criticism or concern.

2. Selection of Detail

Example
An article about a new supermarket mentions only the 200 jobs it creates — never the independent shops it will destroy. What the writer leaves out is as revealing as what they include.

3. Rhetorical Questions

Example
"Should we really allow our ancient forests to be sacrificed for a new car park?"
No answer is expected — the question itself declares the writer's stance.

4. Direct Address

Example
"You have the power to change this. We must act now."
"You" and "We" pull the reader into the argument, creating a sense of shared responsibility and urgency.

④ Literary Techniques Glossary

When a question asks you to identify a technique and explain its effect, you must do three things: name it, quote from the text, and explain the effect on the reader. Never just spot the technique — always connect it to meaning and impact.

Simile
A comparison using "like" or "as" to describe something by connecting it to something else.
Effect: Creates a vivid mental image that makes abstract qualities concrete and memorable.
"Her voice was as cold as a winter morning on the moor."
Metaphor
A direct comparison stating that one thing IS another, without using "like" or "as."
Effect: More powerful than simile — forces the reader to see one thing through the qualities of another, creating strong associations.
"The city was a machine that never slept."
Personification
Giving human qualities, emotions, or actions to something non-human (objects, nature, abstract ideas).
Effect: Creates empathy or emotion toward non-human things; makes nature feel alive and responsive.
"The ancient oak tree watched the village change over centuries."
Alliteration
The repetition of the same consonant sound at the beginning of nearby words.
Effect: Creates rhythm and musicality; makes phrases more memorable; can mimic sounds (e.g., "sibilant s" sounds like hissing).
"The storm swept across the silent, silver sea."
Onomatopoeia
A word that sounds like the noise it describes.
Effect: Places the reader inside the scene by recreating actual sounds; makes descriptions more immediate and sensory.
"The logs crackled and hissed in the grate."
Repetition
Deliberately repeating a word, phrase, or sentence structure for effect.
Effect: Emphasises the repeated idea; builds rhythm; can create urgency, intensity, or emotional weight.
"We must act. We must change. We must not wait."
Hyperbole
Deliberate exaggeration for dramatic or humorous effect — not meant to be taken literally.
Effect: Amplifies emotion or urgency; can make the reader feel the extreme scale of something.
"I've told you a thousand times to tidy your room."
Rhetorical Question
A question that does not expect an answer — the answer is implied, or the question is used to make a point.
Effect: Engages the reader directly; reveals the writer's viewpoint; forces the reader to consider the implied answer.
"How long must we wait before our voices are heard?"
Emotive Language
Words chosen specifically to provoke an emotional response in the reader — often sympathy, anger, or fear.
Effect: Bypasses rational thinking and appeals directly to the reader's feelings; can manipulate as well as move.
"The defenceless child stood alone in the rubble of what had been her home."
Rule of Three
Grouping three words, phrases, or examples together for rhythm and impact.
Effect: Feels complete and satisfying; three items feel more comprehensive than one or two; builds to a persuasive climax.
"This school values courage, kindness, and curiosity."

⑤ Model Answer Structure

The "Why did the writer use…?" question is one of the highest-mark question types. It requires a three-part response. Memorise this structure and use it every time.

The Formula
1. State the purpose → 2. Identify the technique → 3. Explain the effect on the reader

Example Question

"The old bridge groaned under the weight of another winter, its iron bones aching with the cold."
Why does the writer use the phrase "iron bones aching with the cold"? [3 marks]

Full-Mark Model Answer

1
The writer uses this phrase to create sympathy for the bridge and to emphasise the harshness of the winter conditions. → States the purpose clearly
2
The technique used is personification — the bridge is given human anatomy ("bones") and a human sensation of pain ("aching"). → Names and explains the technique
3
This makes the reader feel as if the bridge is a living, suffering creature, which creates a sense of fragility and age. We feel the cold more intensely because it is described through pain rather than temperature. → Explains the effect on the reader in detail

⑥ Inference & Deduction

Inference means reading between the lines — working out what the writer implies but does not directly state. It is the most frequently tested higher-order skill in 11+ English.

Literal vs Inferential
Literal: "The boy put on his coat and left without saying goodbye."
Literal answer: He left without saying goodbye. (It says so directly.)
Inferential answer: He was upset, in a hurry, or deliberately being cold. (We deduce this from his behaviour.)

Three Worked Examples

"Mrs Hargreaves did not look up from her marking when the headteacher entered the room."
What does this suggest about Mrs Hargreaves' feelings toward the headteacher?
❌ Literal (Not enough)
"Mrs Hargreaves was marking work when the headteacher came in."
✅ Inferential (Full marks)
"Not looking up is a deliberate act of disregard. This suggests Mrs Hargreaves feels contempt for, or is unhappy with, the headteacher — she is either making a point or is entirely unintimidated by authority."
"He had not mentioned the letter to anyone. It sat in the inside pocket of his jacket, where he pressed it with his palm occasionally, as if to check it was still there."
What does the detail about pressing the letter suggest about the character?
❌ Literal
"He kept the letter in his pocket and touched it sometimes."
✅ Inferential
"The repeated action of checking suggests the letter is emotionally significant — perhaps frightening, precious, or both. He is preoccupied with it and anxious: pressing it 'as if to check it was still there' implies he needs reassurance of its existence."
"The new shopping centre has brought prosperity to some and heartbreak to others."
What does the word 'heartbreak' suggest about the writer's attitude?
❌ Literal
"Some people are sad about the shopping centre."
✅ Inferential
"'Heartbreak' is a deeply emotional word — far stronger than 'disappointment' or 'concern.' The writer's choice of this word suggests they sympathise with those harmed and believe the damage done is profound and personal, not merely financial."

⑦ Text Structure

How a text is organised is as important as what it says. Skilled writers make deliberate choices about where to place information, how to begin and end, and how to signal what is important.

Opening Paragraphs

A strong opening does three things: hooks the reader, establishes the tone, and signals the purpose. Look out for: shocking statistics, anecdotes, rhetorical questions, or vivid scene-setting.

Closing Paragraphs

Endings often return to an idea from the opening (a circular structure), deliver the writer's final verdict, or issue a call to action. The final sentence is usually the most carefully crafted sentence in the whole text.

Topic Sentences

The first sentence of each paragraph tells you what that paragraph is about. In well-organised writing, you could read only the topic sentences and still understand the shape of the argument.

Headlines & Subheadings

Headlines are written to grab attention and often use techniques (alliteration, puns, emotive language). Subheadings act as signposts — they tell you what is coming and help readers navigate a longer text.

Exam Tip
When asked "How does the writer structure this text?", consider: where information appears (beginning, middle, end), how each section links to the next, and why the writer made these choices.

⑨ Vocabulary in Context

When you encounter an unfamiliar word, use these three strategies — in order — to work out its meaning.

Strategy 1: Context Clues

Look at the words, phrases, and ideas surrounding the unknown word. The sentence often provides the meaning through contrast, example, or explanation.

Example
"The politician's speech was mendacious — every statistic she quoted turned out to be fabricated."
"Fabricated" (= made up) tells us mendacious means dishonest or lying.

Strategy 2: Word Family Inference

Break the word into parts — prefix, root, suffix. Latin and Greek roots appear frequently in 11+ vocabulary.

Example
Malevolent = mal- (Latin: bad) + vol- (Latin: to wish) + -ent (adjective suffix)
→ One who wishes harm on others = malevolent means wishing evil on others.

Strategy 3: Elimination

When given multiple choice options, eliminate what clearly doesn't fit — using tone, grammar, and context — until the best option remains.

⑩ Unseen Passage & Questions

Read the following passage carefully at least twice before attempting the questions. Pay attention to the writer's choices of language, structure, and viewpoint.

There is a particular silence that belongs only to libraries, and it is different from every other kind of quiet. It is not the hush of an empty church, which carries with it a faint suggestion of emptiness; nor is it the enforced quiet of a cinema, where the silence is borrowed and must be returned when the credits roll. The silence of a library is inhabited. It is full of words not yet read, arguments not yet settled, and worlds not yet discovered.

When the council announced last spring that Thornfield's Central Library would close by December, they framed it as an "administrative rationalisation" — a phrase so bloodless it might have been chosen precisely to avoid provoking a response. And yet the response came. Within a fortnight, over four thousand residents had signed a petition, and the library's reading rooms hosted protest meetings so packed that people spilled into the car park and stood in the drizzle, holding handwritten placards.

What the council failed to account for is what a library is actually for. It is not a building that lends books, in the way that a petrol station sells fuel. It is a place where a fourteen-year-old can sit in warmth and safety with her A-level revision, away from a crowded home. It is where an elderly man who lives alone comes three times a week, not primarily for books, but because the librarian knows his name. It is where newly arrived families, navigating a new country in a language not their own, find patient help at no cost.

To call this "rationalisation" is not merely inaccurate. It is unkind. And if our councils are permitted to be unkind — if they are allowed to weigh the worth of a building by the number of books it issues per square metre — then something far more important than a library is at risk of closing.

Below is a full-mark response for the language analysis question (Q3), annotated to show exactly why it scores full marks.

This word is normally used to describe living creatures occupying a space, but here it is applied to silence — something intangible. EXPLAINS THE TECHNIQUE The writer's purpose is to persuade the reader that the library is a living, breathing place full of human potential, rather than an empty building. Acceptable answers: "four thousand" / "over four thousand" / "4,000" / "more than 4000." Must include the number. "Many" is insufficient.
Q2 — Inference 2 marks

Q3 — Language Analysis 3 marks
1 mark: identifies personification or metaphor (either accepted). 1 mark: explains what the technique does (silence given a human/living quality). 1 mark: explains the effect on the reader (makes silence feel alive, closure feel like death). All three parts required for full marks.
