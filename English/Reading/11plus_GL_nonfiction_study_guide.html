01
The Foundation
Question Type Framework

Every question in a GL comprehension paper belongs to one of four categories. Once you can identify the category, you know exactly how to answer it. This is the single most important skill in the exam.

A. Retrieval — "Look and Find"

The answer is stated directly in the passage. Your job is to locate it, not invent it. However, the question almost never uses the same words as the passage — it uses synonyms.

The Synonym Trap: If the passage says the mountain was "colossal," the question might ask which word tells us the mountain was "very large." Train yourself to look for meaning, not identical words.
Worked Example
"The expedition team set off at dawn, carrying provisions for three weeks. Their leader, a stoical woman named Dr Farris, had completed this route twice before without incident."

Question: According to the passage, how many times had Dr Farris completed the route previously?

Answer: Twice. The word "previously" in the question tells you the answer is stated outright — find it, quote it.

Strategy: Underline keywords in the question (how many? what colour? who?). Scan the passage for those keywords or their synonyms. Copy the answer exactly as it appears; do not add your own interpretation.

B. Inference — "Read Between the Lines"

The answer is not stated directly. You must use clues in the text to work out what the writer implies — their attitude, a character's feelings, or an unstated meaning. The text hints; you conclude.

Key signal words in questions: "suggest," "imply," "indicate," "what does this tell us about," "how does the writer feel."
Worked Example
"The city sprawled endlessly in every direction — glass towers that scraped the sky, roads choked with exhaust, pigeons pecking at discarded food. Marcus had grown up beside open fields. He did not speak for the first hour."

Question: What does the final sentence suggest about Marcus's feelings?

Answer: It suggests Marcus is overwhelmed or unsettled by the city. His silence implies he is struggling to process what he sees, especially given his rural background. The writer does not say he is shocked — we infer it.

C. Deduction — "Use Logic from the Text"

Deduction is different from inference. In deduction, the answer can be logically proven using stated facts — you are reaching a certain conclusion, not a probable one. Think of it as arithmetic with words: if A + B is true, then C must follow.

Deducereach a logical conclusion
Comparefind similarities
Contrastfind differences
Suggestfind implied meaning
Inferread between lines
Explaingive reason from text
Worked Example
"The museum opens at 9 am and closes at 5 pm. Admission is free for children under 12. Amir is nine years old and arrives at half past ten."

Question: Deduce whether Amir will be charged for entry.

Answer: No — we can deduce this with certainty. He is nine (under 12) so admission is free. This is deduction: the facts prove it beyond doubt.

D. Language and Structure — "How and Why"

These questions ask about the choices the writer made. Why did they use a particular word? What effect does a literary device create? How does the structure of the text affect the reader?

For language questions, you must: (1) Name the device or technique. (2) Quote the relevant word or phrase. (3) Explain the effect on the reader.

Worked Example
"The river devoured everything in its path, swallowing trees whole and spitting out soil like broken teeth."

Question: Identify one language technique and explain its effect.

Answer: The writer uses personification ("devoured," "swallowing," "spitting"). This makes the river seem aggressive and predatory, as though it has a violent will of its own — increasing the reader's sense of danger.

Easy Retrieval — find the stated fact
"The blue whale is the largest animal ever known to have lived on Earth. It can reach lengths of up to 30 metres and weigh as much as 200 tonnes."
According to the passage, what is the maximum weight of a blue whale? (Give the number and unit, e.g. "X tonnes")
"Florence Nightingale was born in 1820 and is celebrated as the founder of modern nursing. She became famous for her work during the Crimean War, when she dramatically reduced death rates in military hospitals."
In which conflict did Florence Nightingale do her most famous work?
"The bakery opened its doors at six o'clock every morning, filling the narrow street with the scent of warm bread and cinnamon."
What two smells filled the street?
Intermediate Inference — read between the lines
"Priya walked into the examination hall and placed her pencil on the desk with trembling hands. She had revised every topic twice, but now, looking at the rows of silent students, she could not recall a single formula."
What does "trembling hands" suggest about Priya's emotional state?
"The old soldier rarely spoke about the war. On Remembrance Day, he would sit by the window for hours, watching the rain, turning his medal over and over in his fingers."
What does the soldier's behaviour on Remembrance Day suggest about how he feels?
Advanced Deduction — logic from stated facts
"The library charges 10p per day for overdue books. Reuben borrowed a book on Monday and returned it the following Friday."
How much will Reuben be charged? (Write your answer in pence, e.g. "40p")
"Only members who joined before 2020 are eligible for the Legacy Discount. Subscribers who joined in 2020 or later pay full price. Yusuf has been a member since 2018."
Will Yusuf receive the Legacy Discount? Write "yes" or "no" and one reason.
Challenging Language & Structure — explain the technique
"The storm was a living beast — it breathed, it roared, it hunted. No house was safe."
Name the language technique used in this passage (one word is sufficient).
"Can we really afford to ignore the crisis any longer? Every day we delay, the damage grows. Every hour we wait, another chance is lost."
What technique is used in the final two sentences?

Word Meaning Questions
Vocabulary in Context

These questions appear in almost every GL paper. The question gives you a word or phrase from the passage and asks what it means, or asks you to choose the word "closest in meaning." The golden rule: a word's meaning depends entirely on the context around it.

The Substitution Method

When asked what a word means, replace it with your answer and check the sentence still makes sense. If it does, you have a strong candidate.

Step-by-Step Example
"The scientist remained impassive as her colleagues celebrated the result."

Question: What does "impassive" mean in this context?

1
Try possible answers: "impassive" might mean calm, expressionless, or angry.
2
Substitute each: "The scientist remained angry as her colleagues celebrated." — possible, but odd. "Remained expressionless" — this fits perfectly. She is not joining in, but her face gives nothing away.
3
Confirm with surrounding context: "remained" suggests her state didn't change despite the excitement around her. "Expressionless" or "unmoved" fits the full sentence.

Using Surrounding Sentences

Unfamiliar words often have clues in the sentence before or after. Look for words that are explained, contrasted, or restated nearby.

Clue-Hunting Example
"The jungle was utterly impenetrable. No path existed through the dense tangle of roots and vines; even machetes could not cut a passage."

Even without knowing "impenetrable," the next sentence explains it: nothing could get through — machetes failed, there was no path. Impenetrable = impossible to pass through. The surrounding sentences gave you the definition.

Tip: In GL papers, the word in the question will almost always have its meaning explained or strongly hinted at within 1–2 sentences of where it appears. Never guess blind — look around it first.

"Closest in Meaning" Multiple-Choice Strategy

GL comprehension uses multiple choice. For vocabulary questions, you typically have four options. Two will be obviously wrong; two will seem plausible. Here is how to choose between the final two:

1
Eliminate the impossible. Cross out any option that cannot possibly fit the context (wrong part of speech, completely wrong meaning).
2
Substitute both remaining options. Read the sentence aloud with each one in place of the target word.
3
Check the tone. If the passage is formal, the answer will likely be a formal synonym, not a casual one.
4
Watch for near-synonyms that shift the meaning. "Hesitant" and "terrified" both describe something negative, but only one fits a character who pauses before speaking vs. one who screams and runs.
Easy Vocabulary substitution
"The child was ravenous after the long hike; she ate three sandwiches before her mother could even sit down."
What does "ravenous" mean in this context? (One word)
"The new policy was controversial; supporters praised its ambition while critics called it reckless and poorly planned."
What does "controversial" suggest about the policy?
"Despite his frail appearance, the mountaineer proved remarkably resilient, recovering from every setback on the route."
Which word is closest in meaning to "resilient" as used here? (Type: tough / cowardly / impatient / cheerful)
Intermediate Context clues from surrounding sentences
"Her speech was eloquent. Every word seemed chosen with care; the audience was hushed, leaning forward as she spoke."
Using the surrounding context, what does "eloquent" most likely mean?
"The ancient document was illegible in places; years of damp had blurred the ink until entire paragraphs were impossible to make out."
What does "illegible" mean? Use the surrounding sentence as your clue.
Advanced Near-synonym discrimination
"The explorer felt a deep yearning for home as she gazed at the distant mountains."
Which is closer in meaning to "yearning" — "longing" or "hatred"? One word only.
"He was known for his candour — he never softened his opinions to spare people's feelings, even when the truth was uncomfortable."
What does "candour" mean? (One word)
Challenging Tone-aware vocabulary
"The chairman delivered a perfunctory apology — two sentences, no eye contact, then a swift change of subject."
Which best describes "perfunctory": (a) heartfelt (b) routine and careless (c) dramatic (d) silent? Type the letter.

Spelling, Punctuation & Grammar
SPaG — The Third of the Paper

The GL English paper includes approximately 36 SPaG questions alongside the comprehension. They use two formats: Complete the Sentence (choose the correct word/phrase) and Spot the Error (find the mistake). These questions test grammar rules, punctuation, and spelling accuracy.

Key Grammar Rules Tested in GL

1. Subject–Verb Agreement

A singular subject needs a singular verb; a plural subject needs a plural verb.

Common Errors

❌ "The team are playing tomorrow." → ✅ "The team is playing tomorrow." (team = singular collective noun)

❌ "Neither of the boys are ready." → ✅ "Neither of the boys is ready." (neither = singular)

2. Apostrophe Rules

Contraction: Combine two words, replacing missing letters with an apostrophe: it is → it's, they are → they're, would not → wouldn't.

Possession: Add apostrophe + s after the owner noun: the girl's hat (one girl), the girls' hats (several girls — apostrophe after the s).

Never use an apostrophe for a simple plural: ❌ "three apple's" → ✅ "three apples".

3. Comma Use

Commas are required: (a) between items in a list; (b) after an introductory phrase; (c) around a parenthetical clause.

Examples

List: "She packed a map, a compass, a torch, and a first-aid kit."

Introductory: "Despite the rain, the match continued."

Parenthetical: "The scientist, who had worked for twenty years, finally succeeded."

4. Speech Punctuation Rules

Speech marks go around the spoken words only. The punctuation before closing speech marks depends on what follows: comma if a reporting verb follows ("said," "whispered"), full stop if the sentence ends.

Correct Speech Punctuation

✅ "Come quickly," she whispered.

✅ "Come quickly." She turned and ran.

❌ "Come quickly". She turned and ran.

5. Tense Consistency

Once a piece of writing is in a particular tense, it should remain in that tense throughout. Shifting unexpectedly from past to present (or vice versa) is a common error in "Spot the Error" questions.

6. Pronoun Agreement

A pronoun must match the noun it replaces in number and gender: "Every student must bring their own pencil" — correct modern usage. "Each of the boys lost his way" — formal, but also correct.

Spot the Error Format

GL gives you a sentence with one error — it might be spelling, punctuation, or grammar. Read every word carefully. The error is often subtle.

How to Approach

Step 1: Read the whole sentence first for overall sense.

Step 2: Check each individual word: is it spelled correctly? Is it the right word (their/there/they're)?

Step 3: Check punctuation: does the sentence start with a capital? Are apostrophes used correctly? Is a comma missing or incorrectly placed?

Step 4: Check subject-verb agreement.

Easy Punctuation identification
What punctuation mark is used to show that letters are missing in a contraction such as "can't" or "it's"?
Which sentence uses an apostrophe correctly? Type A or B.
A: "The dogs bowl was empty."    B: "The dog's bowl was empty."
What punctuation mark introduces a list? For example: "She needed three things___ courage, patience, and luck."
Intermediate Spot the Error
Find the error. Type the incorrect word: "The childrens' coats were left in the corridor."
Find the grammar error: "Neither of the teachers were available to help." What is the correct form of the verb?
Which word correctly completes this sentence? "We need to decide ______ we leave now or later." (whether / weather / wether)
Advanced Complete the sentence — grammar
Choose the correct option: "The scientist and her assistant ______ been working all night." (has / have)
Identify the punctuation error: "She said, 'I'm coming tomorrow'." Is this correct? Type "correct" or describe the error.
Challenging Complex error detection
This sentence has one error. Name the type of error (spelling / grammar / punctuation): "Everyday, the postman would arrive at precisely 8am; however he never rang the bell."
Correct the apostrophe: "Its a well-known fact that its wings give it its speed." — how many apostrophe errors are there?

15 Essential Techniques
Literary Devices Glossary

In Language & Structure questions, you must name the device, quote the relevant text, and explain the effect on the reader. This table gives you everything you need. Study both the definition and the example sentence — then practise explaining why a writer might have chosen each one.

DeviceDefinitionExample sentenceTypical effect
Simile A comparison using "like" or "as" "The fog moved like a slow grey tide." Creates a vivid picture; makes the unfamiliar familiar
Metaphor States something IS something else (no "like" or "as") "The city was a furnace in July." Creates a stronger, more direct comparison than a simile
Personification Gives human qualities to a non-human thing "The wind howled through the empty streets." Makes objects or forces seem alive; increases drama
Alliteration Repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of close words "Seven silver stars shimmered softly." Creates rhythm; draws attention to key ideas; memorable
Rhetorical Question A question asked for effect, not expecting an answer "How can we stand by and do nothing?" Engages the reader; makes them consider the writer's view
Onomatopoeia A word that sounds like what it describes "The leaves rustled; branches cracked and snapped." Makes description more vivid and immediate
Hyperbole Deliberate exaggeration for effect (not meant to be taken literally) "I've told you a million times." Emphasises extreme emotion; can create humour or urgency
Repetition Deliberately repeating a word, phrase, or structure "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds." Creates rhythm and emphasis; drives home a key point
Rule of Three Three words, phrases, or clauses used together for emphasis "The plan was bold, brilliant, and beautiful." Feels complete and satisfying; memorable; persuasive
Direct Address Speaking directly to the reader using "you" "You know the feeling — that moment when everything changes." Creates intimacy; involves the reader personally
Contrast Placing opposite ideas or images next to each other "He was born in poverty; he died a billionaire." Highlights differences; creates drama or irony
Oxymoron Two contradictory words placed together "A deafening silence fell over the crowd." Creates a striking paradox; captures complex ideas
Assonance Repetition of the same vowel sound in nearby words "The evening breeze seemed to freeze the trees." Creates musicality; slows or speeds the reading pace
Imagery Language that appeals to the senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) "The market smelled of copper coins, crushed herbs, and river mud." Helps the reader experience the scene directly
Juxtaposition Placing two contrasting ideas side by side to highlight their differences "The mansion overlooked a row of crumbling cottages." Creates irony or social commentary; forces comparison
How to answer a language technique question: Name it → Quote it → Explain the effect. Never say a technique "makes the reader interested" or "paints a picture." Be specific: which emotion does it create? What does it make the reader think or feel?
Easy Device identification
"Her voice was like warm honey — smooth, golden, irresistible."
What literary device is used in this sentence? (One word)
"The thunder crashed and the rain hammered the roof."
Which device is used by "crashed" and "hammered"? (One word)
"The wind whispered warnings through the winter willows."
What device is used here? (One word)
Intermediate Device + effect
"Can we really call ourselves civilised when millions go to bed hungry?"
What device is this, and what is its purpose? Name the device only.
"The old library was alive with secrets — every shelf breathed history, every page held a whispered confession."
Name two devices used in this sentence.
Advanced Contrast and juxtaposition
"At the gala, diamonds glittered under the chandeliers while, three streets away, children slept on cardboard in the cold."
What device is used by placing these two images next to each other?
"It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness."
Name TWO devices used in this famous opening. (Separate with comma)
Challenging Effect explanation
"The crowd roared, cheered, surged — a single creature with a thousand hearts."
What is the device used in "a single creature with a thousand hearts"?
"A deafening silence greeted his confession."
What is the device where two contradictory words are placed together?

Grammar Building Blocks
Parts of Speech Quick Reference

GL comprehension questions may ask you to identify the part of speech of a word in context. The key skill is identifying function: the same word can be different parts of speech depending on how it is used.

Part of SpeechDefinitionExamplesIdentification Tip
Noun A person, place, thing, or idea river, courage, Amelia, London Can follow "the" or "a". Can be made plural.
Verb An action or state of being ran, is, consider, had eaten Changes tense (run → ran → will run)
Adjective Describes or modifies a noun enormous, silent, three, northern Answers "What kind?" or "How many?" about a noun
Adverb Modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb quickly, very, never, surprisingly Often ends in -ly; asks "How?" "When?" "Where?" "How much?"
Pronoun Replaces a noun to avoid repetition he, she, it, they, who, which, mine Takes the place of a noun already mentioned
Preposition Shows the relationship between a noun and other words (position, time, direction) in, on, under, after, despite, beside Usually followed by a noun phrase: "under the bridge"
Conjunction Joins words, phrases, or clauses and, but, because, although, so, however Coordinating: FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So). Subordinating: because, although, when, if, since
Determiner Specifies which noun is referred to the, a, an, this, those, every, each Always followed by a noun; cannot stand alone
Watch out: "Light" can be a noun (the light), a verb (to light a candle), or an adjective (a light bag). Always look at how the word is used — its function in the sentence — not just the word itself.
Easy
In the sentence "The old man walked slowly," what part of speech is "slowly"?
In "She placed the book beside the lamp," what part of speech is "beside"?
What part of speech is "every" in the sentence "Every student passed the test"?
Intermediate
In "Although it rained, they played outside," what part of speech is "Although"?
In "The light was fading fast," what part of speech is "light"?
Advanced
In "She was remarkably calm," what part of speech is "remarkably"?
In "The teacher gave her the highest mark," what part of speech is "her"?
Challenging
"They fast on the first day of the month." What part of speech is "fast" here?

IDAPA / PEDAL Framework
Author's Purpose Taxonomy

Every non-fiction text is written for a reason. Understanding that reason helps you answer "Why has the writer written this?" questions and helps you understand why certain language choices were made. Learn the IDAPA framework — then justify your answer from the text.

PurposeDefinitionLanguage CluesText Type Examples
InformGive the reader facts and knowledgeThird person; present tense; factual statements; statistics; definitionsTextbook, encyclopaedia, news article
DescribeCreate a vivid picture of something through sensory detailAdjectives; imagery; sensory language; metaphors and similes; slow, detailed paceTravel writing, autobiography, nature writing
ArguePresent a logical case for or against a positionModal verbs ("should," "must"); counter-arguments addressed; evidence and statistics; logical connectivesEssay, opinion column, letter to newspaper
PersuadeConvince the reader to agree or act using emotional appealsEmotive language; direct address ("you"); rhetorical questions; exaggeration; appeals to fear or sympathyAdvertising, charity appeals, speeches, campaign leaflets
AdviseGuide the reader on how to do something or make decisionsSecond person ("you should"); imperative verbs ("try," "remember"); helpful tone; often uses lists or bullet pointsSelf-help books, advice columns, information leaflets
ExplainClarify how or why something works or happensCausal connectives ("because," "therefore," "as a result"); present tense; logical order; definitions of technical termsHow-to guides, science writing, explainer articles
Key exam skill: Many texts have MORE than one purpose. A charity leaflet might both INFORM (statistics about poverty) and PERSUADE (emotional language about suffering). Always name the primary purpose first, then mention secondary purposes if relevant.
Justifying Purpose from the Text
"Every year, 3.1 million children die from malnutrition — that's one child every ten seconds. You could change this. A donation of just £5 a month provides a family with enough food for a week. Don't wait. Act now."

Question: What is the primary purpose of this text? Justify your answer with evidence.

Model Answer: The primary purpose is to persuade the reader to donate money. The statistic ("one child every ten seconds") creates an emotional impact; "you could change this" uses direct address to make the reader feel personally responsible; "Don't wait. Act now" are imperative sentences demanding immediate action. These are all persuasive techniques, not simply informative ones.

Easy
"The Moon is approximately 384,400 km from Earth. It orbits our planet roughly every 27 days and has no atmosphere."
What is the primary purpose of this text? (One word)
"To make a strong cup of tea, first boil the kettle. Next, place a teabag in your mug. Pour in the hot water and leave for three minutes. Finally, remove the bag and add milk if desired."
What is the purpose of this text? (One word)
Intermediate
"The old lighthouse stood alone on the cliff, its white walls stained with salt and age. At night its beam swept the dark sea like a long, searching arm — patient, solitary, enduring."
What is the main purpose? (One word)
"Social media causes anxiety because constant comparison with others\' highlight reels leads to unrealistic expectations. As a result, teenagers who use social media for more than three hours daily report significantly higher levels of dissatisfaction."
What is the purpose? (One word) Clue: the writer is building a logical case.
Challenging
"Rainforests are disappearing at a rate of one football field every second. These are not just trees — they are the lungs of our planet. How can we breathe if we destroy what breathes for us? You can help by signing our petition today."
Name TWO purposes evident in this text. (Separate with comma)

GL Exam Strategy
Multiple-Choice Technique

GL comprehension is entirely multiple choice. Every question has four options, and only one is correct. Knowing how to eliminate wrong answers is just as important as knowing the right one — and it's a learnable skill.

The Four-Step Elimination Process

1
Cover the options first. Read the question and form your own answer before looking at the choices. This protects you from being led astray by plausible-sounding wrong answers.
2
Eliminate the impossible. Cross out any option that is clearly wrong — factually impossible, completely off-topic, or contradicted by the text.
3
Eliminate the almost-right. Distractor options are designed to look tempting. They often mix one true detail with one false one. Check every word of the option against the text.
4
Choose the option most supported by the text. The correct answer to a comprehension question is always provable from the passage. If you cannot point to the supporting text, you may have the wrong answer.

Common Distractor Types

Distractor TypeHow It WorksHow to Beat It
The True-but-IrrelevantThe option is factually correct from the passage, but doesn't answer the specific question askedAlways re-read the question. Does this option actually answer what's being asked?
The OverreachThe option goes further than the text supports — makes a claim the text hints at but doesn't confirmAsk: Is this stated or proven in the text, or am I adding my own knowledge?
The Near-Synonym TrapFor vocabulary questions, an option has a similar meaning but with a different nuance (e.g. "angry" vs "frustrated")Substitute each option into the sentence and check which fits the context most precisely
The OppositeOne option is the exact opposite of the correct answer — designed to catch students who misreadSlow down. Check whether the text says something positive or negative about the subject.

Handling "Which Word is Closest in Meaning?"

For these questions, the trap is choosing a word you know rather than the word that fits this specific context. "Resolute" and "stubborn" both mean unwilling to change, but resolute has a positive connotation and stubborn a negative one — context determines which is correct.

Checklist: Same part of speech? Same connotation (positive/negative)? Does it fit when substituted into the original sentence?
Intermediate Elimination practice
"The ancient Roman city of Pompeii was buried by the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD. Thousands of residents perished, preserved forever beneath layers of volcanic ash. The site was rediscovered in 1748 and remains one of the most visited archaeological sites in the world."
Which option is NOT supported by the passage? A: Pompeii was buried in ash. B: The eruption killed people. C: Romans knew the eruption was coming. D: The site was rediscovered in 1748. Type the letter.
"She spoke with quiet confidence — not arrogance, but the calm certainty of someone who had prepared thoroughly."
Which word is closest in meaning to "arrogance" as implied in this sentence? A: pride B: modesty C: nervousness D: experience. Type the letter.
Advanced Near-synonym discrimination
"The headteacher was resolute in her decision to expand the school day, despite protests from parents and staff."
Which word best replaces "resolute"? A: stubborn B: determined C: angry D: confused. Type the letter.

Tone and Register Vocabulary

How Texts Are Built
Text Structure Patterns

Structure questions ask you about the organisation of a text rather than its language. Understanding how a text is built helps you locate information quickly and answers questions like "Why does the writer end the article with this paragraph?"

StructureDefinitionSignal WordsUsed In
ChronologicalEvents ordered by time, from earliest to latestfirst, then, next, after, finally, in 1987, the following yearAutobiography, history articles, news reports
Cause and EffectExplains why something happened and what resulted from itbecause, therefore, as a result, consequently, this led toScience writing, analysis, argument essays
Problem and SolutionIdentifies an issue then proposes how to fix itthe problem is..., one solution..., however, this can be addressed byReports, advice articles, campaign writing
Compare and ContrastExamines similarities and differences between two or more subjectssimilarly, however, on the other hand, in contrast, both, whereasReview articles, travel writing comparing destinations
General to SpecificOpens with a broad statement, then narrows to detail and evidencefor example, such as, specifically, in particular, to illustrateTextbooks, explanatory journalism
Question and AnswerRaises a question then answers it — sometimes explicitly, sometimes implicitlySo why...? The answer lies in... / What causes...? Scientists have found...Magazine features, explainer articles, FAQs

Structural Features as Answerable Elements

Subheadings

Subheadings divide a text into sections and tell you the topic of each. In an exam, a question asking "What is the purpose of the subheading 'The Hidden Costs'?" expects you to explain what it signals to the reader about the content that follows.

Topic Sentences

The first sentence of a non-fiction paragraph usually states the main point of that paragraph. This is the topic sentence. The rest of the paragraph provides evidence, examples, or elaboration. Scanning topic sentences lets you locate information without reading every word.

Concluding Paragraphs

In non-fiction, the final paragraph often: (a) summarises the key argument; (b) issues a call to action; (c) returns to an image or idea from the opening (a "circular structure"); or (d) leaves the reader with a thought-provoking question or statement.

Exam question: "Why does the writer end with this sentence?" → Your answer should explain what effect this structural choice has on the reader.

GL Two-Page Format
Active Reading Protocol

The GL comprehension passage is approximately two A4 pages long. Below is a step-by-step method calibrated to this format. Practice it until it becomes automatic.

1
Read the title and any blurb (30 seconds)

The title immediately tells you the text type, topic, and sometimes purpose. "An Extract from 'Wild: A Journey Across Antarctica'" tells you this is travel writing/memoir, it is set in Antarctica, and it is likely descriptive. Adjust your expectations accordingly.

2
Skim the questions before reading the passage (60 seconds)

Read through all 13 questions without trying to answer them. You are hunting for: what topics they cover, any vocabulary words they highlight, and which question types appear. This tells you what to look for while reading.

3
Read the passage actively (3–4 minutes)

Read the full passage at a steady pace — not rushing, not dawdling. As you read: underline or circle key ideas; note the tone of each paragraph in the margin (e.g. "descriptive," "argumentative," "nostalgic"); mark any striking language techniques; note the structural pattern.

4
Scan the first and last sentences of each paragraph

If you are short on time, topic sentences (first sentence) and clincher sentences (last sentence) contain the most important information. A quick scan confirms your understanding of the text's structure.

5
Answer retrieval questions first

Return to the questions. Retrieval questions (look-and-find) can be answered with a quick scan and are the fastest marks to earn. Do these first to build confidence and momentum.

6
Answer inference and deduction questions

These require more thought. Return to the relevant section of the text; re-read the two or three sentences around the focus area; ask yourself "what is the writer implying?" or "what conclusion do the facts lead to?"

7
Answer language/structure questions last

These require the most time and thought. Identify the device, quote it mentally, and think about its specific effect. Use the formula: Device + Quote + Effect.

8
Review any skipped questions

If you skipped a question, return to it now. Use elimination to narrow down the options. Never leave a multiple-choice question blank — an educated guess is better than no answer.

The cardinal rule: Every answer must come from the text. Do not use general knowledge. Do not use personal experience. The passage is the only source of truth.

Exam Strategy
Timing and Paper Management

The GL English paper is 50 minutes long and contains 25 questions: 13 comprehension questions and approximately 12 SPaG questions. Efficient time management is the difference between completing the paper comfortably and running out of time.

Time Allocation Per Section

Reading the title + skimming questions~2 min
Reading the full passage (actively)~5 min
Answering 13 comprehension questions~18 min
Answering ~12 SPaG questions~18 min
Reviewing skipped / uncertain answers~7 min
Total50 min
Target pace: Approximately 80–90 seconds per comprehension question. If you are spending more than 2 minutes on a single question, mark it and move on. Retrieve those marks at the end.

The Skip-and-Return Strategy

Some questions are harder than others. Spending 4 minutes on one hard question when you could have answered 3 easier questions in the same time is poor strategy. Mark difficult questions clearly (circle the number) and return after completing the rest.

Mark answer sheets carefully: If you skip a question on the answer sheet, be careful not to shift all subsequent answers one line down. Double-check the question number matches the row you are filling in.

Reading vs Answering Split

Many students make the mistake of reading the passage too slowly (spending 10+ minutes reading) and then running out of time for questions. The reading is only there to enable the answering — it is not an end in itself.

Practise reading the passage in 4–5 minutes with active annotation (underline key ideas, note paragraph purposes). The goal is comprehension sufficient to locate answers, not memorisation of every detail.

When You Don't Know the Answer

1
Re-read the question — have you understood it correctly?
2
Return to the passage and scan the relevant section again.
3
Use elimination to remove impossible options.
4
If still unsure, choose the option most supported by the text and move on. Do not leave it blank.
