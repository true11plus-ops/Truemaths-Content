The Inference Equation

When an exam question asks you to infer something, it is asking you to figure out information that the writer has hinted at but not directly stated. Think of it as detective work. Every inference follows the same formula:

Textual Clue
+
Prior Knowledge
=
Inference
✦ Worked Example

Clue from the text: "Priya's hands were shaking as she held the sealed envelope."

Prior knowledge: People's hands shake when they are nervous or very excited about something unknown.

Inference: Priya is anxious and probably anticipating important or life-changing news.

💡 Remember

Your inference must be grounded in the text. You are not inventing — you are reading between the lines using clues the author has deliberately placed there.

Easy Practice — The Equation
Q1. Read the clue below. Which word best completes the inference?
"Marcus slammed the door and threw his bag across the room."

The inference is: Marcus is feeling very _____. (Choose one word: happy / angry / bored)

Q2. What is the 'prior knowledge' part of the inference equation?
"Layla pulled her coat tighter and buried her chin in her scarf."

Inference: It is cold outside. What is the prior knowledge that links the clue to this inference? (Answer in one sentence.)

Q3. Complete the inference equation for this clue.
"The dog's tail wagged furiously as Noah walked through the front door."

What can you infer about how the dog feels? (One word is enough.)

Intermediate Practice — Inference from Clues
Q4. Read the extract and answer the inference question.
"The classroom fell silent the moment Mr Hargreaves stepped through the door. Every student straightened in their chair and eyes fixed on their textbooks."

What does this suggest about Mr Hargreaves? (Write a full inference.)

Q5. What can you infer about where this scene is set?
"Neon signs reflected in the puddles below. Taxis honked without pause, and the smell of roasting chestnuts drifted from a nearby stall as commuters rushed past without a glance at one another."
Advanced Practice — Applying the Equation
Q6. What two separate inferences can you draw from this single clue?
"She put the letter back in the envelope without finishing it and slid it beneath the stack of unopened post."

Inference 1 should be about her feelings. Inference 2 should be about her relationship with the letter-sender. Write at least one of them.

"Every morning, Theo walked to the end of the pier, stared at the boats on the horizon, and then turned back towards town without ever stepping aboard one."

Three Types of Question: Retrieval, Deduction, and Inference

Not every reading comprehension question asks you to infer. It is important to identify which type of thinking a question requires — because using the wrong approach loses marks.

📖 Reference Passage — used for all three examples below
The old lighthouse stood at the very edge of the cliff. A sign nailed to its door read: CLOSED TO THE PUBLIC — STRUCTURAL DAMAGE. Inside, Finn could see a spiral staircase through the cracked window pane. A thin layer of grime covered every step, and a single spider's web, thick and undisturbed, stretched from one wall to the other. He had heard from the village elder that no keeper had lived here for almost forty years.

🔍 Literal Retrieval

The answer is directly stated in the text. Find it and copy or paraphrase it. No extra thinking needed.
Q: What does the sign on the door say?
A: "CLOSED TO THE PUBLIC — STRUCTURAL DAMAGE." (directly in the text)

🔗 Deduction

You combine two or more stated facts to reach a logical conclusion. The answer is not stated but follows directly from facts that are.
Q: How long has the lighthouse been abandoned?
A: Almost forty years. (stated, but requires identifying the right fact and linking it to "abandoned")

💭 Inference

The answer is not stated anywhere. You use clues + prior knowledge to figure out something the writer has left unsaid.
Q: What does the spider's web tell us about the lighthouse?
A: Nobody has entered for a very long time — an undisturbed web indicates complete absence of activity.
💡 How to Spot Which Type

Ask yourself: Can I find the exact answer by re-reading? → Retrieval. Can I calculate it by combining stated facts? → Deduction. Do I need to "read between the lines"? → Inference.

Easy Practice — Identify the Question Type
Use the lighthouse passage above for these questions.

Q10. "What atmosphere does the lighthouse have?" — Is this Retrieval, Deduction, or Inference?

Intermediate Practice
Q12. Answer this inference question: What does the "thick and undisturbed" spider's web suggest about the lighthouse?

Advanced Practice

"Detective Morse arrived at precisely 8:47 am, as she always did. Her badge was polished, her notebook already open. She had solved eleven cases this year alone."

Four Types of Inference

Inference is not just about how a character feels. It can reveal different kinds of information. Recognising the type being asked about helps you give a focused answer.

🧑

Character Inference

What a person is like based on their actions, dialogue, or choices.

🏛️

Setting Inference

Where or when a story takes place, deduced from details in the scene.

✍️

Attitude Inference

How the writer or narrator feels about the subject they are describing.

📚

Vocabulary Inference

Working out the meaning of an unfamiliar word from its context.

Character Inference

📖 Extract
When the younger children crowded round the vending machine with their coins, Jasper quietly counted his own and slipped two to the smallest boy without being asked.
✦ Worked Example

Question: What does this tell us about Jasper's character?

Answer: Jasper is generous and perceptive. He notices the younger boy's need without being prompted and gives up his own money — suggesting he is selfless and kind-hearted.

Setting Inference

📖 Extract
The cobbled street was lit by flickering gas lamps, and the clip-clop of a horse-drawn carriage echoed past the rows of soot-blackened terraced houses.
✦ Worked Example

Question: When is this story set? How do you know?

Answer: The story is set in the Victorian era (19th century). Gas lamps, horse-drawn carriages, and soot-blackened terraces are all features associated with industrial Victorian England, before the widespread use of electricity or motor vehicles.

Vocabulary Inference

📖 Extract
The new student was ebullient — she burst into every room with a wide grin, striking up conversations with everyone she met and filling the corridors with laughter.
✦ Worked Example

Question: What does the word "ebullient" suggest about the student?

Answer: From the context — bursting in, grinning, talking to everyone, filling space with laughter — "ebullient" means enthusiastically cheerful or lively.

Writer's Attitude Questions

When a question asks "What is the writer's attitude towards…?" it is asking you to identify how the author feels about the subject they are describing. The writer rarely states their attitude directly — instead, you must read it from three signals:

🎭

Tone Words

The emotional quality of the language — e.g. admiring, mocking, sorrowful, enthusiastic.

⚖️

Word Connotation

The feeling a word carries beyond its basic meaning — e.g. "home" feels warm; "dwelling" feels cold and clinical.

🎯

Selection of Detail

What the writer chose to include (or leave out) tells you what they want you to notice — and feel.

📖 Extract for Attitude Question
The new shopping centre sprawled across what was once a quiet meadow, its glass and steel bulk casting long shadows over the remaining hedgerows. Families filed in through its wide automatic doors, exchanging birdsong for the tinny chime of tills. Somewhere above, a skylark sang — but no one paused to listen.
✦ Full Worked Example — Attitude Question

Question: What is the writer's attitude towards the shopping centre? Use evidence from the passage.

Step 1 — Tone words: "sprawled" (aggressive, ungainly expansion), "cast long shadows" (ominous), "tinny" (cheap, unpleasant).

Step 2 — Connotation: "quiet meadow" and "birdsong" have warm, peaceful connotations; replacing them with "glass and steel bulk" signals loss. "Tinny chime of tills" implies shallowness versus the natural world.

Step 3 — Selection of detail: The writer chooses to end on the image of a skylark singing while nobody listens — this detail is not neutral. It is a deliberate contrast to highlight what has been lost.

Full Answer: The writer is critical of and saddened by the shopping centre. By contrasting the lost "quiet meadow" and "birdsong" with the "glass and steel bulk" and "tinny chime of tills," the writer presents the development as something that has driven out natural beauty. The final image of the unheard skylark suggests the writer believes something irreplaceable has been sacrificed.

💡 Attitude Can Be Nuanced

Attitude is rarely just "positive" or "negative." It can be: affectionate mockery, cautious admiration, nostalgic sadness, angry resignation, ironic detachment. The more precise your word, the more marks you earn.

Easy Practice — Tone Words

"Mr Gripp had amassed his fortune through what he called 'efficiency' — a word that, in his mouth, seemed to mean the removal of everything inconvenient, including the workers who might object to it."
Intermediate Practice
Q24. Identify three words or phrases that reveal the writer's attitude, and state what that attitude is.

Writer's Craft and Authorial Intent

Some questions ask not just what you infer, but why the writer made a specific language choice. These questions follow a three-step move:

Language Choice
What word or technique did the writer use?
→
Purpose
Why did the writer choose it?
→
Effect on Reader
What does it make the reader feel or think?
📖 Extract
The river did not flow that winter — it crawled, thick and grey, between banks of frost-hardened mud.
✦ Full Worked Example — Craft Question

Question: Why has the writer used the word "crawled" instead of "flowed"? What effect does this create?

Language Choice: "Crawled" — a verb usually associated with something slow, weak, or struggling.

Purpose: The writer replaces the neutral "flowed" with "crawled" to suggest the river has been reduced to something laboured and barely alive — it personifies the river as a creature in distress.

Effect on Reader: The reader senses a bleakness and oppressive heaviness to the scene. The river's struggle mirrors the wider mood of the passage — something that should be powerful and free has been reduced and slowed by the harsh winter.

💡 The Question Stem

Craft questions often use stems like: "Why has the writer chosen…?", "What is the effect of…?", "How does the writer create a sense of…?"

Easy Practice — Language Choice and Effect
Q26. Why might a writer use the verb "thundered" instead of "said" in a piece of dialogue?
Q27. What is the effect of calling a character's smile "blade-thin"?
Intermediate Practice
Q28. Apply the three-step move (Choice → Purpose → Effect) to the underlined word.
"The children swarmed into the playground at the sound of the bell."
Advanced Practice
Q29. Compare the effect of the two versions. What does the original word choice add that the replacement removes?
Original: "The old house brooded at the end of the lane."
Replacement: "The old house stood at the end of the lane."
Challenging Practice
Q30. Write a full craft-question answer (all three steps) for the highlighted phrase below. Use at least three sentences.
"He had not laughed in three years — not a real laugh, at least. The kind that starts somewhere deep and arrives at your face as a surprise."

The Point + Evidence Model

On open-ended inference questions, you must not only state your inference — you must prove it. The Point + Evidence model is the structure every full-mark answer uses.

Make your Point
(your inference)
+
Give Evidence
(a quote or detail)
Question ❌ Weak Answer (0–1 marks) ✅ Strong Answer (2 marks)
How is Sarah feeling? She is sad. Sarah is feeling deeply upset (Point) because the author tells us she had "puffy, red eyes" (Evidence), which suggests she has been crying for some time.
What is the atmosphere of the house? It is scary. The atmosphere is ominous and neglected (Point) as the gate is described as "hanging by a single rusted hinge" (Evidence), suggesting the property has been abandoned and is in a state of decay.
What does this tell us about Mr Kelvin? He is strict. Mr Kelvin is exacting and demands high standards (Point) — the text states he "returned the report without a word, simply pointing to a single misplaced comma" (Evidence), which shows he will not overlook even the smallest error.
⚠️ Common Mistake

Do not quote a long passage and then fail to explain it. Evidence without explanation scores no more than a bare point. Always add a brief "which suggests/shows/tells us…" after your quote.

Easy Practice — Point + Evidence
Q31. Does this answer contain both a Point and Evidence? (Yes/No)

"The dog is friendly because it wagged its tail."

Q32. Upgrade this weak answer using Point + Evidence + Explanation:
"She ran away from the noise." — Original weak answer: "She is scared."
Intermediate Practice
Q33. Write a full Point + Evidence + Explanation answer for this question.
"The old farmer looked at the cracked earth, ran a hand through the dust where seedlings should have been, and turned away without speaking."

Question: How is the farmer feeling?

Advanced Practice
Q34. Write TWO separate Point + Evidence answers — one about the character's feelings, one about the character's personality.
"Every time the door opened, Nadia flinched. But when the youngest child in the shelter began to cry, she was on her feet immediately, whispering calm words and making up a story about a rabbit who was very brave."

The Substitution Trick

When a question asks what a specific word suggests, try replacing it with a simple, neutral alternative. The difference between the two versions reveals exactly what the original word adds.

✦ Worked Example

Original: "The wind howled through the rafters."

Substitution: "The wind blew through the rafters."

Analysis: "Blew" is neutral — it tells us the wind moved. "Howled" suggests the wind made a loud, almost mournful, animal sound. It creates a sense of wildness, eeriness, and discomfort that "blew" completely lacks. The writer chose "howled" to make the setting feel threatening and alive.

💡 How to Use This Trick in Exams

Mentally swap the key word for "went", "moved", "looked", "said", or "walked" — whichever neutral equivalent fits. Then ask: what did I just lose? That loss is your answer.

Easy Practice — The Substitution Trick
Q35. Replace "glared" with "looked." What does "glared" add that "looked" doesn't?
"The inspector glared at the suspect across the table."
Q36. What does "devoured" suggest that "ate" would not?
"She had not eaten since morning, so she devoured the meal in minutes."
Intermediate Practice
Q37. Apply the substitution trick. Replace the underlined word and explain what is lost.
"The factory belched black smoke into the pale morning sky."
Advanced Practice
Q38. Use the substitution trick on ALL THREE underlined words. What overall mood does the writer create?
"The market buzzed, the colours clashed and the smell of spices ambushed every passer-by."

Two-Step and Layered Inference

⭐ This Is What Separates 8/10 from 10/10

Most students can make one inference. Top-band students chain inferences together — each step leads to a deeper insight. This is called a layered inference.

The structure works like this:

Character does X
→
…which means they feel Y
→
…which reveals they believe Z
📖 Extract
Every evening, when the rest of the family had gone to bed, Elara would take out the old photograph and set it carefully on the table beside her cup of tea. She never spoke of it to anyone.
✦ Full Worked Example — Layered Inference

Single-step (average answer): Elara is secretive because she hides the photograph from her family.

Layered (top-band answer):

Step 1 (Action): Elara takes out the photograph only when alone and never mentions it.

Step 2 (Feeling): This suggests she has a strong emotional attachment to whoever is in the photograph — strong enough to need private time with it.

Step 3 (Belief / Deeper Meaning): Her secrecy implies she believes her grief or longing is something the family would not understand, or perhaps something too painful to share — suggesting she carries a private sorrow she has chosen to bear alone.

Easy Practice — Identify the Chain
Q39. Complete the chain for the clue below.
"When the teacher praised someone else's painting, Daniel quietly turned his own canvas face-down."

Daniel does X (turns canvas down). This means he feels Y (_____). Which reveals he believes Z (_____).

Intermediate Practice
Q40. Write a two-step layered inference from this clue.
"When the letter arrived confirming her scholarship, Mira folded it carefully and placed it in the drawer without telling anyone."
Advanced Practice
Q41. Write a full three-step layered inference. Label each step clearly.
"At the end of each shift, before leaving the hospital, Dr Varma would stop at the window of the children's ward and watch the children sleeping — just for a moment — before turning off the corridor light."
Challenging Practice
Q42. This passage contains a layered inference about a relationship. Write a full three-step chain about what the relationship between the two characters might be like.
"When Clem entered the room, her uncle rose from his chair. It was the kind of standing up a person does not plan — quick, reflexive, as if some part of him expected something he had learned not to ask for."

Negative Inference — What the Writer Left Out

Advanced inference is not only about what a character does — it is also about what they don't do, what the writer chose not to describe, or what a character's silence or inaction tells us.

📖 Extract
The awards ceremony ended. Everyone cheered as each name was called. When they read out the winner of the writing prize — the competition Theo had entered every year for six years — he did not move. He did not clap. He did not look up.
✦ Worked Example — Negative Inference

Question: What does Theo's inaction tell us about how he is feeling?

Answer: Theo's complete stillness — not moving, not clapping, not looking up — speaks more than any action could. The writer deliberately withholds movement or speech from Theo; this silence tells us he is stunned or crushed by the result. The detail that he has entered for six years shows how much it mattered, making his inaction a measure of profound disappointment. His refusal to even look up suggests he is either close to tears or too hurt to let anyone see his face.

💡 Key Phrase to Use

In your answer, you can write: "The writer's decision not to describe X is significant because…" or "The absence of Y suggests that…"

Easy Practice — Negative Inference
Q43. What does the mother's silence tell us?
"When Rafi told his mother he had failed the test, she said nothing. She just turned back to the sink and continued washing the dishes."
Intermediate Practice
Q44. What does the writer's choice NOT to name the other person tell us?
"There was someone standing at the edge of the wood. Iris saw them. She did not call out. She did not wave. She walked the other way."
Advanced Practice
Q45. What does the writer's decision NOT to describe the outcome tell us about the mood they want to create?
"The surgeon placed the scalpel down. The room was quiet. The nurses looked at each other. Nobody spoke for a long time. Then someone reached for the telephone."

GL-Format Multiple Choice — With Distractor Analysis

In the actual GL Assessment exam, inference questions are multiple choice. You choose from five options (A–E). Two options will seem plausible — but only one is fully supported by the text. Learning to eliminate is as important as knowing the right answer.

📖 GL-Style Passage — Read carefully before answering all four questions
The mountain path grew narrower with every step. Surrounded by pine trees so tall that they blocked the afternoon light, Kira paused and checked her compass for the third time in an hour. The map showed a refuge shelter two kilometres ahead, but the map had been wrong before. She adjusted the straps of her pack — heavier now than when she had set out — and pressed on. Below her, the valley had disappeared into cloud, and the wind had taken on a new, colder edge. Kira did not let herself think about the alternative. She simply looked at the next step, then the one after that.

Question 1

GL-Style MCQ — Select the best answer

What does the phrase "checked her compass for the third time in an hour" suggest about Kira?

  • AShe is enjoying the challenge of navigating in the mountains.
  • BShe has forgotten how to use a compass.
  • CShe is feeling uncertain about her direction and anxious about the route.
  • DShe enjoys using technology while hiking.
  • EShe has checked her compass three times because it is broken.
Distractor Analysis (Study This)
A — Too extreme / unsupported: Nothing in the text suggests enjoyment. Checking three times signals anxiety, not pleasure.
B — Too literal and implausible: Someone who cannot use a compass would not be using one to navigate alone.
C — ✅ Correct: Checking repeatedly is a behaviour associated with uncertainty and anxiety. Combined with the earlier detail that "the map had been wrong before," this confirms her worry.
D — Off-topic: A compass is not technology in any relevant sense, and the text gives no hint she enjoys gadgets.
E — Too literal: The text does not say the compass is broken.

Question 2

GL-Style MCQ

What does "Kira did not let herself think about the alternative" most likely suggest?

  • AShe is unable to think of any other route to take.
  • BShe is deliberately avoiding frightening thoughts to stay focused and calm.
  • CShe is certain that the shelter will be where the map says it is.
  • DShe has decided to turn back and go home.
  • EShe is a professional mountaineer who never feels fear.
Distractor Analysis (Study This)
A — Too literal: "The alternative" is not a route; it refers to what might happen if she fails.
B — ✅ Correct: The phrase "did not let herself think" shows conscious self-control under pressure — she is managing fear by focusing only on the next step.
C — Contradicts the text (plausible distractor): The text says the map had been wrong before — so she is NOT certain.
D — Directly contradicts evidence: She "pressed on," meaning she continued forward.
E — Too extreme / unsupported: Her repeated compass-checking and conscious avoidance of fear prove she is not fearless.

Question 3

GL-Style MCQ

What atmosphere does the writer create in this passage?

  • APeaceful and beautiful
  • BJoyful and exciting
  • CCalm and reassuring
  • DTense and isolating
  • ENostalgic and reflective

Question 4

GL-Style MCQ

What does "the pack — heavier now than when she had set out" suggest?

  • AShe has bought new supplies along the way.
  • BShe packed too many things and regrets it.
  • CThe physical exertion and difficult conditions are making the journey feel harder as it progresses.
  • DThe weather has made her pack absorb water.
  • EShe is carrying provisions for the whole group.
Easy MCQ Practice — Elimination Skills
Q46. In a GL MCQ question, which type of wrong option sounds right but goes beyond what the text says?
Q47. Which option type ignores the context and just repeats a word from the passage?
Q48. What should you do FIRST when approaching a GL MCQ inference question?

Non-Fiction Passage — Inference Practice

Inference is just as important in non-fiction. With editorials, journalism, or reports, you are often asked to infer the writer's viewpoint, purpose, or assumptions.

📖 Non-Fiction Extract — Editorial
Every year, another village school closes its doors. The children are bussed further and further away to larger, more "efficient" centres of learning, while the buildings where their parents and grandparents first learned to read now stand empty, windows boarded, playgrounds silent. We are told this is progress. We are invited to celebrate the economies of scale, the improved facilities, the higher exam results — as if a child's education could be measured only in percentages. What is never counted, and what can never be counted, is what is lost when a community loses its school.
✦ Worked Example — Non-Fiction Inference Q1

Question: What is the writer's attitude towards the closure of village schools?

Answer: The writer is strongly opposed to the closures and saddened by them. The use of quotation marks around "efficient" signals irony — the writer disputes that the closures are truly efficient. The image of "windows boarded, playgrounds silent" carries a mournful, funereal quality, suggesting irreversible loss. The phrase "as if a child's education could be measured only in percentages" is openly critical of the authorities' reasoning.

✦ Worked Example — Non-Fiction Inference Q2

Question: What does the writer imply about the authorities' reasons for the closures?

Answer: The writer implies the authorities are prioritising financial and statistical efficiency over human and community values. The phrase "economies of scale" is presented as cold and bureaucratic, while the phrase "what can never be counted" suggests the writer believes the most important things — community, belonging, local identity — are things the authorities are not even attempting to measure.

Easy Practice — Non-Fiction
Q49. Why does the writer put the word "efficient" in quotation marks?
Intermediate Practice
Q50. What does the writer imply by describing the old school buildings as having "windows boarded, playgrounds silent"?
Q51. The writer mentions "their parents and grandparents." What does this detail add to the argument?

Poetry — Inference Through Imagery and Emotion

In poetry, inference often comes from imagery (the pictures a poet creates with words) and emotion (the feeling the poem builds). The same skills apply — clue + prior knowledge — but the clues are denser and more compressed.

📖 Original Poem — Read carefully
She cleared the house in half a day,
folding silence into cardboard boxes,
wrapping the clock that had stopped at four
in the paper from last year's birthday.

She did not cry. She made a list.
She counted teacups, stacked the chairs.
Outside the window, the garden waited,
neither helping nor understanding.

She left one photograph on the shelf.
Only one. She did not say why.
✦ Worked Example — Poetry Inference Q1

Question: What does the image "folding silence into cardboard boxes" suggest about how the speaker feels?

Answer: The poet personifies "silence" as something physical — something that can be packed away. This suggests the house is now associated with emptiness and absence. Packing it up implies the speaker is trying to contain or manage their grief by giving it a physical form. The act of folding and boxing suggests a need to control emotions through practical activity.

✦ Worked Example — Poetry Inference Q2

Question: What can you infer about why the speaker "left one photograph on the shelf"?

Answer: The deliberate choice to leave a single photograph — rather than pack it with everything else — suggests it holds a specific significance the speaker cannot yet let go of. The final line "She did not say why" creates a sense of private, unshared grief. The decision to leave one thing behind in an otherwise emptied house suggests the photograph represents a connection too painful to sever completely.

Easy Practice — Poetry
Q52. What is suggested by "the clock that had stopped at four"? What might this symbolise?
Q53. "She did not cry. She made a list." What does this contrast tell us about how she is coping?
Intermediate Practice
Q54. "Outside the window, the garden waited, / neither helping nor understanding." What does this personification of the garden add to the poem?
Advanced Practice
Q55. What is the overall emotion the poet creates in this poem? Use evidence from at least THREE images.

Common Wrong Answer Patterns — and Why They Fail

In GL MCQ exams, two or three options are designed to tempt you. Here are the four most common traps, with examples from real exam-style questions.

❌ Pattern 1 — Too Extreme

The trap: The option makes a much bigger claim than the text supports.

Example: Text says a character "hesitated before speaking." Wrong option: "She was absolutely terrified."

Why it fails: "Hesitation" suggests uncertainty or nervousness — not outright terror. The option leaps too far beyond the evidence.

Correct approach: Match the intensity of your inference to the intensity of the clue.

❌ Pattern 2 — Too Literal

The trap: The option describes only the surface fact — it finds the words but misses the meaning.

Example: Text says a character "stared at the closed door for a long time." Wrong option: "She was looking at the door."

Why it fails: This is merely retrieval, not inference. An inference question requires you to go deeper — why is she staring? What does it mean?

Correct approach: Always ask: what does this action tell us about how the character feels or thinks?

❌ Pattern 3 — Plausible but Unsupported

The trap: The option sounds reasonable but is not backed by any evidence in the text. It is something you invented, not inferred.

Example: Text says a character is late to a meeting. Wrong option: "She had been in an accident on the way."

Why it fails: The text gives no clue about the reason for her lateness — this is guessing, not inference. The correct answer must be grounded in textual clues.

Correct approach: Ask: where exactly in the text is this supported? If you can't point to a word or phrase, reject it.

❌ Pattern 4 — Contradicts the Text

The trap: The option directly contradicts something the passage states.

Example: Text says the character "pressed on despite everything." Wrong option: "She gave up and turned back."

Why it fails: This simply contradicts the text. Eliminate anything that cannot co-exist with stated facts.

Correct approach: Treat contradictions as the easiest options to eliminate — cross them out first.

Exam Strategy — Timing and Technique

Knowing how to answer is only half the skill. Knowing how to manage your time in the actual exam is just as important.

3 min

Read the passage first. Read the whole passage once without reading the questions. Build a mental map of the text.

~60s

Per MCQ question. Find the relevant part of the text → eliminate 2–3 clearly wrong options → choose between remaining 1–2.

Stuck?

Use the 2-minute rule. If you've been on one question more than 2 minutes: eliminate what you can, pick the best remaining option, and move on.

Always

Answer every question. There is no penalty for wrong answers in GL Assessment. A guess scores more than a blank.

MCQ Elimination Order

✦ The Four-Step Elimination Process

Step 1: Immediately eliminate anything that directly contradicts the text. (Usually 1–2 options.)

Step 2: Eliminate anything that is completely off-topic or irrelevant to the question asked.

Step 3: Be cautious of any option that sounds "too extreme" — check it against the actual words in the passage.

Step 4: Between the remaining plausible options, ask: which one is most fully supported by specific words or phrases in the text?

💡 If Two Options Both Look Right

Go back to the text. Re-read the exact sentence or phrase the question asks about. The correct answer will be the one where you can point to specific textual support. The tempting wrong answer will require you to invent or assume something the text does not provide.
