01
The Foundation
What Retrieval Questions Actually Test

Retrieval looks like the simplest question type — you just find the answer in the text, right? In reality, retrieval questions test three distinct skills, and students who treat them as "copy and paste" exercises routinely lose marks they should have earned.

The Two Types of Retrieval

Direct retrieval asks you to find a specific piece of information and either quote it or restate it. The answer is explicitly in the text — but the wording of the question will almost never match the wording of the passage.

"Own words" retrieval asks you to find the information and then express it differently — changing both the vocabulary and the structure. This is retrieval with a transformation step.

Side by Side
Passage: "The vessel was antiquated — its timbers warped and blackened, its sails threadbare, its rigging barely sufficient to hold."

Direct retrieval: "Give a quote that describes the condition of the vessel." → Answer: "its timbers warped and blackened"

Own words: "In your own words, explain the condition of the vessel." → Answer: The ship was old and falling apart, with rotting wood, worn sails, and fraying ropes.

Why Retrieval Is Harder Than It Looks

Three things make retrieval genuinely difficult:

  • Synonyms: the question uses different words from the passage. "Extremely cold" in the question might be "glacial" in the text.
  • Multi-part answers: the full answer is spread across two sentences or two paragraphs.
  • The "not in one place" problem: the information exists but isn't in a single phrase — it has to be assembled from multiple parts of the text.

Command Word Recognition

The command word tells you exactly what format your answer must take. Read it before you even look at the passage.

find → locate + quote the phrase
give → state the information (quote or own words)
identify → name or label precisely
list → multiple items, often numbered
quote → copy the exact words with quotation marks
in your own words, explain → find + paraphrase (no copying)
write down → copy the exact phrase from the text
Critical rule: If the command word says "quote" or "write down," you must use the exact words from the passage. If it says "in your own words," copying is penalised. Check the command word before you write a single word.

02
Finding the Answer
The Complete Search Strategy

Step 1 — Identify Your Anchor Word

Before touching the passage, extract the most unique word from the question — a word so specific that it will only appear in one or two places in the text. The best anchor words are: proper nouns (names, places, dates), unusual nouns, and numbers.

Anchor Word Selection

Question: "What did the expedition leader find at the base of the mountain?"

❌ Weak anchor: "find" (appears everywhere), "base" (common word)

✅ Strong anchor: "expedition leader" or "mountain" — these are specific enough to locate quickly

Step 2 — Keyword Sweep Scanning

Do not re-read the entire passage. Use a keyword sweep: move your eyes down each paragraph, looking only for your anchor word. Work column by column, paragraph by paragraph. You are not reading — you are searching.

How to sweep: Look at the beginning and end of each sentence in a paragraph. If neither contains your anchor word, skip the entire sentence and move to the next. Your eyes should be jumping, not flowing.

Step 3 — When Your Anchor Word Doesn't Appear

Sometimes the question uses a synonym of what's in the text. "Extremely cold" in the question might appear as "glacial" in the passage — and you cannot scan for "cold" because the word isn't there.

When this happens, work backwards from meaning:

1
Ask: what category of thing is the question looking for? (A colour? An emotion? A description of weather? A time?)
2
Think of two or three synonyms for your anchor word. Try scanning for each.
3
If still not found, use paragraph structure: if the question asks about the weather, go to the paragraph that describes setting or atmosphere.

Step 4 — Using Line References

If the question specifies "In lines 12–18" or "In the third paragraph," your search is already narrowed. Go directly to that section. Read it sentence by sentence — you are not searching a whole passage, you are reading a small excerpt carefully.

Do not go outside the specified lines unless you have finished reading the section and genuinely cannot find the answer. Examiners specify lines for a reason — the answer is in there.

A question specifies "In lines 14–20, find…" Where should you start reading?

"The expedition had been planned for three years. Dr Vasquez had mapped every route, cross-referenced every supply list, and consulted seventeen different weather reports before committing to a departure date of the fourteenth of March."

Unknown Words
Synonym-Without-Recognition Strategy

This is the hardest retrieval scenario: the question hints at a meaning, but the word in the passage is one you don't recognise. You cannot scan for a word you cannot identify. Here is the method.

The Three-Layer Approach

1
Identify the category. What kind of word is the question looking for? A colour? An emotion? An adjective describing temperature, size, speed, danger? Narrowing to a category tells you what to look for even before you find the word.
2
Use surrounding sentences as context. When you find a candidate word you don't recognise, read the two sentences around it. Does the surrounding language reinforce the idea of extreme cold? Of violence? Of fear? The context will confirm whether you've found the right word.
3
Scan for tone and intensity markers. Words like "quite," "rather," "somewhat" signal mild intensity. Words like "utterly," "fiercely," "absolutely," "impossibly" signal extreme intensity. If the question asks for "extreme," look near intensifiers.

Full Worked Example: "A Word Suggesting Extreme Cold"

Step-by-Step Process
"The expedition reached the plateau as evening fell. A thin mist gathered at their ankles. The wind off the glacier was gelid — the kind of cold that does not merely chill the skin but seems to penetrate bone itself. Forsythe pulled his coat tighter and said nothing."
Inner Monologue

Step 1: The question asks for "extreme cold." Category: an adjective describing temperature at a severe level. Not just "cold" — extreme.

Step 2: I scan the passage for the temperature/weather section. I see "wind off the glacier" — that's a cold setting. I look at nearby words.

Step 3: I see "gelid." I don't know this word. But the sentence around it says "does not merely chill the skin but penetrates bone itself" — this is clearly extreme, not mild cold.

Step 4: I confirm: "gelid" is surrounded by extreme language. This is my answer. I do not need to know the word in advance — context proved it.

Answer: gelid (it means intensely cold — but you found it through context, not recognition)

Easy
"She entered the chamber and was overwhelmed by the stench — a fetid miasma that made her gag and turn away."
The question asks: "Find a word suggesting the smell was very unpleasant." You don't know "fetid." What should you look at to confirm it answers the question?
"She entered the chamber and was overwhelmed by the stench — a fetid miasma that made her gag and turn away."
What is the correct one-word answer to: "Find a word suggesting the smell was very unpleasant"?
Intermediate
"The crowd was utterly quiescent — not a sound, not a movement, as though every person had been turned to stone by the announcement."
The question asks: "Find a word meaning completely still and silent." What word answers this?
When the question asks for a word of "extreme" intensity and you're scanning an unfamiliar passage, what type of words should you look near?
Challenging
"The general regarded his troops with an expression that could only be described as mordant contempt — a look that had withered careers and shattered confidence across thirty years of service."
The question asks: "Find a word suggesting the general's expression was harshly critical." What is the answer?

Multiple Pieces of Information
Multi-Part Retrieval Questions

Recognising a Multi-Part Question

Multi-part questions are signalled by: the mark allocation (a 3-mark question almost always requires 3 distinct points), plural nouns in the question ("give two reasons," "list three features," "identify the problems"), or the word "both."

Rule of thumb: The number of marks = the number of distinct pieces of information required. A [3] always needs three separate points.

Format: List, Sub-Labels, or Prose?

SituationBest FormatExample
Question says "list" or asks for 3+ itemsNumbered list or (a)(b)(c)1. The weather was stormy. 2. The crew was exhausted. 3. Supplies were low.
Question asks for "two reasons" — written answer expectedTwo clear sentencesFirstly… Secondly…
Question asks for details about one thing from multiple placesSingle synthesised sentenceThe captain was described as stern and experienced.

Locating Spread-Out Answers

When a question asks for three things about a character, those things may be spread across three different paragraphs. Your strategy:

1
Find the first mention of the subject (e.g., the character's name). Note the paragraph number.
2
Scan forward for each subsequent mention. Each mention may add another piece of information.
3
Keep a mental or pencilled tally: have you found 1 of 3? 2 of 3? Stop when you've found the required number.

Checking You Have Genuinely Distinct Points

"The explorer was brave and courageous" is NOT two distinct points for a 2-mark question — brave and courageous are synonyms. You need two genuinely different pieces of information. Read each point you've written and ask: does this say something the other point doesn't?

Easy
A question is worth 3 marks and asks for reasons. How many distinct reasons must you give?
A student writes: "The house was decrepit and dilapidated" as their two-part answer. Is this two distinct points? (yes or no)
Intermediate
"Captain Reyes had spent forty years at sea. Her navigational instincts were uncanny — she could read the stars without instruments. But it was her voice, calm and absolute in a crisis, that her crew trusted most."
The question asks [2 marks]: "Give two things that made Captain Reyes impressive." Write your first answer point only.
Which format is most appropriate when a question says "List three items the explorer packed"?
Challenging
"The village had changed enormously. Where once there had been a mill, there was now a car park. The old forge had become a gift shop. The schoolhouse, where three generations of the family had been educated, had been converted into luxury flats."
The question asks [3 marks]: "Give three changes to the village." What is the THIRD change mentioned?

Using the Text's Own Words
Valid Quotation Guide

What Counts as a Good Short Quotation

A good quotation is specific and targeted. It contains the key word or phrase that directly answers the question — not the entire sentence it came from. Copying a full sentence when a three-word phrase would do is not wrong, but it wastes time and can suggest you are uncertain about what the answer actually is.

Key Phrase vs Full Sentence
"The ancient oak tree, its bark gnarled and furrowed with centuries of growth, stood at the edge of the clearing like a silent sentinel."

Question: "Quote a phrase that describes the tree's bark."

❌ Too long: "its bark gnarled and furrowed with centuries of growth, stood at the edge of the clearing"

✅ Correct: "gnarled and furrowed" or "gnarled and furrowed with centuries of growth"

Using Ellipsis (…) to Shorten a Quote

When the key information is at the start and end of a long sentence, and the middle is not relevant, you can use an ellipsis to show something has been left out.

Ellipsis Example
"The expedition leader, who had been on fourteen previous voyages across Arctic waters, declared the mission complete."

Full quote: "The expedition leader, who had been on fourteen previous voyages across Arctic waters, declared the mission complete."

With ellipsis: "The expedition leader… declared the mission complete."

This is valid when the omitted section is genuinely irrelevant to the question being answered.

Embedding a Quote Grammatically

A quote must fit naturally into your sentence. The most common error is producing a quote that doesn't connect to the sentence around it.

Embedded vs. Dropped

❌ Dropped: The tree was old. "gnarled and furrowed with centuries of growth."

✅ Embedded: The tree's bark is described as "gnarled and furrowed with centuries of growth," suggesting great age.

Own Words Answers
Paraphrasing Mechanics

Why One Swapped Word Is Not Paraphrasing

The most common error in "own words" answers is substituting one or two words while keeping the rest of the sentence identical. Examiners recognise this immediately, and it is penalised. True paraphrasing requires changing both the vocabulary and the sentence structure.

The Two-Change Rule

Before accepting your paraphrase as valid, check that you have made both changes:

  • Change 1 — Vocabulary: Replace the key words with genuine synonyms or explanatory phrases
  • Change 2 — Structure: Reorganise the sentence (e.g., passive → active, adjective first → verb first, subordinate clause moved)
Before and After — Full Worked Example
Original: "The fortress was impregnable — its walls of black basalt rose forty feet without a foothold, and its single gate was reinforced with iron bars a foot thick."

Question: "In your own words, explain why the fortress was difficult to attack."

❌ Copied: "The fortress was impregnable — its walls rose forty feet without a foothold."
(Almost identical — vocabulary barely changed, structure identical)

❌ One-word swap: "The fortress was unassailable — its walls of black rock rose forty feet without a foothold."
(Only "impregnable" → "unassailable" and "basalt" → "rock" — structure unchanged)

✅ True paraphrase: "Attackers had almost no chance of breaking in because the sheer walls offered nothing to grip, rising to an enormous height, and the only entrance was blocked by thick iron barriers."
(New vocabulary + new structure — active rather than descriptive, reorganised information)

The Boundary Between Paraphrase and Inference

Retrieval paraphrase stays within what the text explicitly says — it just says it differently. The moment you add an interpretation or explanation not directly supported by the text, you have crossed into inference. For a retrieval question, that is out of scope and may cost marks.

Test: Can you point to every claim in your answer in a specific sentence of the passage? If yes, you're paraphrasing. If you're adding something the text implies but doesn't state, you've crossed into inference.

Synthesis Skill
Answers That Span Multiple Paragraphs

Recognising When the Answer Is Not All in One Place

Some questions have answers distributed across the text. Signals include: a character who appears multiple times, a theme that recurs, or a question asking you to summarise the overall impression. If you've found one piece and the answer doesn't feel complete, keep scanning.

How to Synthesise Across Paragraphs

1
Find the first relevant piece of information and note the paragraph and key phrase.
2
Continue scanning for further mentions of the subject. Mark each one.
3
Combine the information into one coherent answer — making sure the combined answer doesn't contradict itself. (If paragraph 1 says the weather was calm and paragraph 3 says it became stormy, your answer must reflect both.)
Synthesis Worked Example
Paragraph 1: "Mira was the youngest of the guides, barely twenty-three."
Paragraph 3: "She had already led six successful ascents and spoke four languages fluently."
Paragraph 5: "The climbers quickly learned to trust her instincts above all else."

Question [2 marks]: "Give two details about Mira from across the passage."

Answer: She was young (twenty-three) but highly experienced, having led six successful ascents. The climbers came to trust her instincts completely.

Both points drawn from different paragraphs, combined into a coherent answer.

Easy
If you have found one piece of information but the question is worth 2 marks, what should you do?
Intermediate
Paragraph 2: "The harbour had been constructed in 1842."
Paragraph 4: "By the turn of the century, it handled over a thousand vessels annually."
The question asks: "Give two facts about the harbour." What is the fact from paragraph 2?

Multiple Items
List Questions

Format Conventions

When a question asks you to "list" items, use numbered points or comma separation depending on the scale. Three or more items: number them. Two items: a comma-separated answer in a sentence is fine.

Always match the format to the question: If asked for "three items," give exactly three. If the passage contains five examples, you still only need three — choose the three most clearly supported by the text.

Quote or Paraphrase in List Answers?

Follow the command word. If the question says "list," you may quote directly or use own words unless specified. Quoting is usually faster and equally valid. Paraphrasing is necessary only when the question explicitly requires it.

When the Passage Has More Examples Than Needed

If asked for three items and the passage contains six, choose the three that are most distinct from each other. Avoid items that overlap in meaning. Prioritise items that are clearly supported by single, unambiguous phrases rather than implied ideas.

Easy
A question asks: "List two items the character put in her bag." The passage mentions: a compass, a notebook, a torch, and a water bottle. Which two items should you choose?
Does "order" matter in a list question — do you need to give items in the order they appear in the passage? (yes or no)
Intermediate
"The old mansion had been left untouched for decades. The wallpaper was peeling. The floorboards creaked under the slightest weight. The windows were so grimy they admitted barely any light, and damp had crept up the walls of every room."
The question asks: "List three signs that the mansion was in poor condition." Which sign is NOT listed in the passage? (Choose: peeling wallpaper / no electricity / creaking floorboards / damp walls)
Challenging
A student is asked for two distinct pieces of evidence. They write: "The captain was exhausted and worn out." How many marks will this earn?

Exact Match
Precision Matching

Match Your Answer to What the Question Asks For

Every retrieval question specifies exactly what it wants: one word, a phrase, two reasons, a description. The most avoidable mark-loss in retrieval comes from ignoring this specification.

Question asks forWhat you must writeCommon error
"one word"Exactly one wordWriting a phrase: "very cold" instead of "glacial"
"a phrase"2–6 words, no full sentence neededCopying the entire paragraph
"two reasons"Two separate pointsOne detailed reason expressed in two sentences
"give a quote"Exact words in quotation marksParaphrasing without quotation marks

The Re-Read Rule

After writing your answer, re-read the question. Ask: "Have I answered exactly what was asked?" This ten-second check recovers marks that would otherwise be lost to over-answering, under-answering, or answering in the wrong format.

"The child ran swiftly across the yard."
The question asks: "Find ONE word that tells us how the child moved." What is the correct answer?

Intermediate
"The fog rolled in from the estuary, dense and impenetrable, swallowing the lighthouse whole."
The question asks: "Find a phrase (not a full sentence) that describes the fog." What is the best short phrase?

Understanding Marks
Mark Scheme Awareness

How Marks Are Awarded

Mark ValueWhat It Usually Requires
[1 mark]One specific, correct piece of information — a single word, phrase, or fact
[2 marks]Two distinct pieces of information — OR one piece + a valid quotation or explanation
[3 marks]Three separate facts, or two facts + a quotation, or one well-developed explanation with evidence
Before scanning: Look at the mark allocation. A [1] question needs one thing. A [3] question needs three things. This tells you how much text to scan and how complete your answer needs to be.

When to Stop Writing

Adding information beyond what is needed is a risk, not a bonus. Examiners mark the first correct answer and any additional correct answers up to the mark total — but an incorrect statement after a correct one can sometimes undermine confidence in the answer. Write exactly what is needed, then stop.

Easy
A question is worth [2 marks]. How many distinct pieces of information do you need in your answer?
True or false: adding extra information beyond the mark total is always safe and can only help your score. (true or false)
Intermediate
A question worth [1 mark] asks: "What time did the train depart?" A student writes: "The train departed at 6 AM, and it arrived at the capital by noon, having stopped three times." How many marks will they earn?

When Scanning Fails
The "Not Found" Protocol

Every student reaches a question where the answer isn't where they expected it to be. The following four-step protocol stops panic and gives you a systematic path to recovery.

1
Try a synonym of your anchor word. If you scanned for "cold" and found nothing, try "icy," "frigid," "arctic," "bitter," "frozen." The text uses its own vocabulary — your scan must match it.
2
Identify the most likely paragraph using logic. What type of information is the question asking for? Setting/description? → Introduction or scene-setting paragraphs. A character's action? → Middle paragraphs. The outcome or result? → Final paragraphs.
3
Read that paragraph sentence by sentence. Abandon scanning and read carefully. Look for meaning rather than exact words.
4
If still not found, mark the question and move on. Write your best attempt with what you have — never leave a blank. Return to this question after completing the rest. Unanswered questions score zero; a reasonable guess may score.
Never leave a blank: In a multiple-choice paper, guess if necessary. In a written answer paper, write your best approximation even if uncertain — partial credit is possible and a blank is always zero.

After all four steps, you still cannot find the answer. What must you NOT do?

The Centrepiece
Full Worked Think-Aloud Example

This is the most important section of the guide. Read every step of this worked example carefully — it shows the complete internal process of an expert student solving a retrieval question from start to finish.

The Passage
"The market at Karisimbi had operated without interruption since the fourteenth century, and its ancient rhythms were immediately apparent to any newcomer. The stalls were arranged in concentric rings: the innermost circle reserved for spices — vivid crimson mounds of chilli, golden drifts of turmeric — the middle ring given over to cloth merchants whose bolts of indigo and saffron fabric rippled in the breeze, and the outer ring reserved for livestock. The noise was formidable: a compound of bleating, haggling, and the sharp percussion of a blacksmith's hammer from somewhere beyond the gates. Old Adama, who had occupied the same stall for thirty-seven years, presided over his mounds of dried herbs with the serene authority of a man who has never been in a hurry in his life."
The Question [2 marks]

"In your own words, describe what was sold in the innermost circle of the market stalls."

Expert Student Inner Monologue — Step by Step

Step 1 — Read the question and identify the command word.
"In your own words" — this means I cannot copy. I must paraphrase. Two marks means two pieces of information, or a well-expressed single fact.

Step 2 — Identify my anchor word.
"Innermost circle" — that's specific and unusual. I'll scan for "inner" or "innermost."

Step 3 — Keyword sweep.
I run my eye down the passage. I see "innermost circle" in the second sentence. Found it. I read the phrase around it: "the innermost circle reserved for spices — vivid crimson mounds of chilli, golden drifts of turmeric."

Step 4 — Check I have the full answer.
The question asks what was sold there. The text says "spices" and then gives two examples: chilli and turmeric. That's one general answer plus two specific details — good for 2 marks.

Step 5 — Plan my own-words answer.
I cannot write: "the innermost circle reserved for spices — vivid crimson mounds of chilli, golden drifts of turmeric." That's copying.
I need to: (1) change the vocabulary, (2) change the structure.
Vocabulary changes: reserved → kept for / sold only; vivid crimson → bright red; mounds → piles; golden drifts → yellow heaps.
Structure: I'll lead with the general point, then the examples.

Step 6 — Write the answer.
"The central ring of stalls was set aside for selling spices, including brightly coloured varieties such as red chilli and yellow turmeric."

Step 7 — Re-read the question and verify.
"In your own words" ✓ — I have not copied. "Describe what was sold in the innermost circle" ✓ — I have said spices with two examples. 2 marks ✓ — two pieces of information given.

What made this answer correct: (1) command word identified first; (2) anchor word selected immediately; (3) scan was efficient — straight to the relevant sentence; (4) full context was read before writing; (5) paraphrase changed both vocabulary and structure; (6) the answer was verified against the question before finishing.

What was sold in the middle ring of stalls? (One word from the passage)
Intermediate
The passage says the market's noise was "formidable." Using context clues, what does "formidable" mean here? (One or two words)

Exam Management
Timing & Question Triage

Time Budget by Mark Value

1-mark retrieval question~45 sec
2-mark retrieval question~75 sec
3-mark retrieval question~2 min
Move on threshold (if not found)at 2× budget
The rule: If a 1-mark question has taken you 90 seconds and you still haven't found the answer, mark it with a circle and move on. Return at the end. Do not let one question eat the time of three.

Why Retrieval Before Inference

Retrieval questions are faster to answer than inference or language questions because the answer is in the text — you just need to find it. Completing retrieval questions first gives you a bank of correct answers quickly, reducing anxiety and preserving time for the harder questions that need more thought.

How to Flag and Return

Circle the question number clearly — not so heavily that you mark through your answer. Write a brief note in the margin: "need to find" or "not yet." When you return at the end, go to the circled questions in order and use the Not Found Protocol.

Difficulty-Calibrated Practice Passage

Read this passage carefully before attempting the questions below. It is written at genuine GL difficulty — note the varied vocabulary, multiple characters, and dense description.

Practice Passage — "The Cartographer's House"
The house at the end of Sextant Lane had belonged, for as long as anyone in the village could remember, to the Delacroix family — though by the winter of 1891, only one Delacroix remained. Céleste, the last of her line, was eighty-two and had not left the house in eleven years. Her sole companion was a grey parrot named Ptolemy, who had the disconcerting habit of reciting latitude and longitude coordinates without warning.

The interior of the house was extraordinary. Every surface — the walls, the ceiling, the backs of doors — was covered in maps. There were nautical charts of uncharted Pacific archipelagos, hand-drawn surveys of African river systems never published, and, most remarkable of all, a vast parchment map that covered the entirety of the north-facing wall. This last had been the life's work of Céleste's grandfather, Édouard Delacroix, who had spent forty years sailing between continents in the service of the French Admiralty.

The young archivist sent by the municipal library, a taciturn man named Hargreaves, arrived on a Tuesday in November and spent his first morning simply standing in the entrance hall, overwhelmed. Mastery Passage — "The Lighthouse Inspector"

The lighthouse at Morven Point had stood for two hundred and fourteen years, and in all that time, it had never once failed to illuminate the treacherous rocks below. Inspector Alicia Drummond arrived on the island in March, tasked by the Northern Lights Authority with conducting a thorough examination of the structure and its records. She was, by reputation, an exacting woman — colleagues described her as methodical to a fault, though none disputed the accuracy of her reports.

The lighthouse keeper, a bearded, soft-spoken man named Calum Beag, greeted her at the dock with conspicuous courtesy, though his expression suggested he found the visit unwelcome. He had kept the light for nineteen years without incident, and official scrutiny, even the politely conducted variety, made him uneasy.

The inspection took four days. Drummond examined the mechanism, the fuel stores, the logbooks stretching back seven decades, and the structural integrity of the tower itself. She found three minor deficiencies — a cracked lens mounting, a portion of the external staircase that had begun to corrode, and a gap in the logbook entries for the winter of 1987 — none of which, she noted in her report, posed an immediate danger.

On the final morning, Drummond stood at the lantern room window and looked out at the rocks that the lighthouse had kept sailors from for two centuries. Beag stood beside her and said, quietly, "It has not let anyone down." She considered this for a moment and replied: "Not yet."
