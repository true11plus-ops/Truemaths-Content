① The 3-Step Sieve

A good summary is not a rewrite. It is a distillation — keeping only the essential information and expressing it in your own words. Use this three-step process every time.

1
Identify
Find the key information — the main facts, actions, or arguments. Often in the topic sentence (first or last sentence of a paragraph).
2
Filter
Cross out adjectives, metaphors, examples, and repetition. These are the "fluff." You need the core facts and actions only.
3
Synthesise
Combine what remains into one clear sentence or short paragraph using your own words — never copy phrases from the original.

The Golden Rule

Never lift phrases verbatim. If you copy the writer's exact words, you are proving you can read — not that you understand. Paraphrase every time, even for simple facts. Change the vocabulary AND the sentence structure.

Worked Example

Original Extract

"The towering, snow-capped mountains loomed over the tiny, shivering village like ancient giants, blocking out the winter sun for six long months of the year and making any kind of travel to the outside world nearly impossible for the terrified inhabitants."


Step 1 — Identify the key information

Mountains → village → blocked sun → prevented travel. CORE FACTS

Step 2 — Filter the fluff

towering, snow-capped, loomed, tiny, shivering, like ancient giants, long, terrified — all decoration. REMOVED

Step 3 — Synthesise in own words

Summary: The village was cut off by large mountains that blocked sunlight and prevented travel for half the year. FINAL ANSWER

Note: "Towering" → "large"; "blocked out the winter sun" → "blocked sunlight"; "making travel nearly impossible" → "prevented travel". Every phrase is paraphrased.

② Writing Within a Word Limit

GL Assessment papers often specify a word limit: "Summarise in no more than 10 words" or "in one sentence." This is a precision skill — not a trick. Here is how to master it.

Three Rules for Word-Limited Summaries

Rule 1: Count every word, including articles — "a," "the," "and," "in," "of" all count. Numbers written as figures (3) count as one word.

Rule 2: Keep only the "load-bearing" information — the facts that, if removed, would make the summary inaccurate. Everything else goes.

Rule 3: Never sacrifice accuracy for brevity — if your 10-word summary misses a key point, it will lose marks even if it is exactly 10 words.

Three Worked Examples at Different Constraints

Example A — 10-Word Limit

Original (47 words): "Scientists at the university have discovered that regular daily exercise of at least thirty minutes significantly reduces the risk of developing heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain types of cancer later in life, even if that exercise only begins in middle age."


Student's process:

Key facts: scientists, exercise, reduces risk of disease IDENTIFY

Remove: university detail, specific diseases, specific duration, age detail FILTER

Draft 1: "Scientists found that regular exercise reduces risk of developing disease." — 10 words ✓ SYNTHESISE

Decision: "Developing" kept because it specifies future risk. "Several diseases" would be more precise than "disease" — but accuracy beats exact word count. This draft is acceptable.

Example B — 15-Word Limit

Original (52 words): "The council voted last Tuesday to permanently close the much-loved Riverside Library, despite a petition signed by over six thousand local residents who argued the library was a vital community resource, particularly for elderly residents and children from lower-income families who rely on it most."


Student's process:

Key facts: council, closed library, despite petition, 6000 residents, community resource IDENTIFY

Remove: "much-loved," "last Tuesday," specific groups listed FILTER

Final (15 words): "The council closed the library despite 6000 residents signing a petition to save it." SYNTHESISE

Decision: "6000" counts as 1 word. "Community resource" omitted — too vague. Core conflict (council vs community) is preserved.

Example C — One-Sentence Limit

Original (3 paragraphs, ~120 words): A text describing how plastic waste enters the ocean through rivers, damages marine ecosystems, enters the food chain, and ultimately affects human health.


Student's process:

Main thread: plastic → rivers → ocean → food chain → humans IDENTIFY

Remove: all individual examples, statistics, specific animal species FILTER

One sentence: "Plastic waste travels through rivers into the ocean, where it damages marine life and eventually enters the human food chain." SYNTHESISE

Note: A one-sentence limit can be a long sentence — use a comma or subordinate clause to combine two key points.

③ "Whose View?" Summary

This question type asks you to summarise what the writer thinks, not just what the text says. These are different things.

Summarising an argumentIdentifying a viewpoint
What does the text say?What does the writer think?
Focus on content and facts presentedFocus on the writer's attitude, bias, or opinion
"The text argues that plastic is harmful.""The writer believes plastic use is irresponsible and urgently needs regulation."
Can be neutralMust identify a position (for/against/concerned/supportive)

Strategy: Focus on the verbs and adjectives

Ask: Is the writer criticising, praising, questioning, warning, or celebrating? The verbs and adjective choices will tell you. Then write: "The writer believes / argues / warns that…"

Worked Example

Extract: "Despite repeated promises, the government has done nothing meaningful about the crisis in social care. Thousands of elderly people are being left without help, their dignity stripped away by a system more concerned with cost-cutting than with compassion."


Question: Summarise the writer's viewpoint in one sentence.


Weak answer: "The writer says the government hasn't helped elderly people." [Too neutral — no viewpoint]

Strong answer: "The writer argues that the government has failed the elderly through deliberate neglect, prioritising money over people's welfare." IDENTIFIES POSITION USES EVIDENCE FROM TEXT WRITER'S VERB ("argues") USED

Why this works: "Argues" signals opinion; "deliberate neglect" paraphrases "done nothing meaningful"; "prioritising money over welfare" paraphrases the final clause. Every word earns its place.

④ Non-Fiction Text Structure

Non-fiction texts are built using recognisable patterns. Identifying which pattern a text uses helps you understand the writer's purpose and predict where to find information.

StructurePurposeSignal WordsExample Sentence
Chronological To tell a story or trace a history in time order Initially, subsequently, decades later, then, eventually, by the 1990s "Initially the factory employed fifty workers; by 1950, it had expanded to five hundred."
Cause & Effect To explain why something happened and what resulted Consequently, as a result, therefore, this led to, which caused "The prolonged drought caused crop failure, which consequently led to widespread food shortages."
Problem & Solution To argue for a specific fix to an identified issue The dilemma, the challenge, however, one solution, resolved by, a remedy "The crisis in affordable housing is severe; however, increasing social housing stock could resolve it."
Compare & Contrast To show similarities and differences between two or more things Conversely, in contrast, similarly, whereas, on the other hand, unlike "Whereas urban schools have extensive facilities, rural schools often lack even basic equipment."

Non-Fiction Structural Devices

Introduction Paragraph

Purpose: To introduce the topic, hook the reader, and signal what the text will argue or explore. Often uses a question, a striking statistic, or an anecdote.

Example opening: "Every year, 8 million tonnes of plastic enter our oceans. This single fact should alarm every one of us." — The statistic hooks; the follow-up sentence states the writer's position.

Conclusion Paragraph

Purpose: To summarise the argument, deliver a final verdict, or issue a call to action. Often refers back to the introduction (circular structure).

Example: "If 8 million tonnes seems an impossible problem, remember: every piece of plastic we refuse to buy is one less piece threatening the sea." — Returns to opening statistic; ends with a call to action.

Topic Sentence

Purpose: The first sentence of a paragraph — states the paragraph's main idea. The rest of the paragraph develops, explains, or evidences this idea.

Example: "The impact of plastic on marine wildlife has been catastrophic." — Everything in this paragraph will be evidence for "catastrophic impact on wildlife."

Subheadings

Purpose: To divide a text into sections; to signal what each section will cover; to help readers navigate. Subheadings are structural signposts.

Example subheading: "The Human Cost" — signals the next section will focus on how plastic affects people, not wildlife. This is new territory after a section on marine life.

⑤ Paragraph Function Taxonomy

Every paragraph in a well-written text has a job to do. Being able to name that job — and explain why it is there — is a key GL Assessment skill.

Function 1
Introducing
Presents the topic, establishes context, or raises the central question. Often contains the writer's thesis or central claim.
"Every city in the world is facing the same crisis: not enough homes, and too many people priced out of them."
Function 2
Developing
Adds evidence, detail, examples, or statistics that support the point made in the previous paragraph.
"In London alone, average rents have risen by 40% in five years, forcing young professionals to commute from towns over an hour away."
Function 3
Complicating
Introduces a counter-argument, qualification, or a twist — something that makes the issue more complex. Often begins with "However," "Yet," or "On the other hand."
"However, building more homes alone will not solve the crisis if those homes are bought by investors and left empty."
Function 4
Concluding
Draws together the argument, delivers the writer's verdict, or issues a call to action. Does not introduce new information.
"Until governments treat housing as a human right rather than an investment opportunity, the crisis will only deepen."

How to Identify a Paragraph's Function

Ask three questions: What does this paragraph do? (introduce/add/complicate/conclude) Where does it appear? (beginning/middle/end) What does it add to the text around it? (context/evidence/doubt/resolution)

⑥ Paragraph Connections

Questions that ask "how does paragraph 3 link to paragraph 2?" require you to explain the relationship between paragraphs — not just describe each one separately.

The "What, Why, How" Prompt

What: What does this paragraph do that is different from the one before?
Why: Why has the writer placed it here at this point in the text?
How: How do the specific words or signals connect it to the previous paragraph?

Worked Example: Two Connected Paragraphs

Paragraph 2: "Cycling has transformed urban commuting. In cities across Europe, cycling rates have doubled in the past decade, reducing congestion and cutting carbon emissions significantly."

Paragraph 3: "However, this progress has not been without controversy. In many neighbourhoods, the construction of cycle lanes has removed parking spaces and narrowed roads, leading to anger among residents who feel their needs have been ignored."


Question: How does paragraph 3 link to paragraph 2?


Weak answer: "Paragraph 3 is about controversy and parking." [No connection made]

Strong answer: "Paragraph 3 complicates the positive picture established in paragraph 2. Paragraph 2 presents cycling as a clear success; the word 'However' at the start of paragraph 3 signals a direct reversal, introducing the costs and conflicts that paragraph 2 did not acknowledge. The writer places this complication here to show the issue is more complex than it first appeared, giving balance to the argument."

What: Para 3 introduces conflict. Why: To add balance and complexity. How: "However" signals the reversal; "this progress" links directly back to para 2's "transformed."

⑦ Fiction / Narrative Structure

Fiction texts have their own structural vocabulary. You must be able to identify which stage of a narrative you are reading — and explain what the writer achieves by placing content at that particular moment.

Stage 1
Exposition
Introduces characters, setting, and background. Establishes the "normal world" before the story's conflict begins.
Stage 2
Rising Action
A series of events that build tension and complicate the situation. The problem grows.
Stage 3
Climax
The moment of highest tension or the turning point. The conflict reaches its peak.
Stage 4
Falling Action
Events after the climax. Tension begins to ease. Consequences of the climax play out.
Stage 5
Resolution
The final state: conflicts are resolved (or deliberately unresolved). A new "normal" is established.

One-Sentence Examples for Each Stage

Exposition: "Mara had lived on the island all her life and had never once felt afraid of the sea — until the morning she woke to find the boats were gone."

Rising Action: "Each day brought a new discovery: first the torn nets, then the missing oars, and finally the letter she was never meant to find."

Climax: "She stood at the cliff edge, letter in hand, as the full weight of what her father had done finally broke open inside her."

Falling Action: "She walked back to the village slowly, the letter now folded into her pocket, unsure of anything except that she could not pretend she didn't know."

Resolution: "By spring, the boats had returned and life had resumed its rhythm — but Mara had not returned to what she was, and she knew she never would."

⑧ Flashback & the Sequencing Trap

A flashback is when the narrative jumps backward in time to show an earlier event. It is one of the most common sources of student error in sequencing questions.

How to Recognise a Flashback

Tense shift: The main narrative is in past tense; the flashback may shift to past perfect ("had been," "had seen," "had told") — a tense even further back in time.

Time markers: Phrases like "she remembered," "years earlier," "as a child," "looking back," "it had all started when," "he recalled the day."

Sensory triggers: A smell, sound, or sight in the present prompts the character to remember something from the past.

The Sequencing Trap — Read This Carefully

Narrative order = the order events appear in the text (the order the writer tells them).

Chronological order = the order events actually happened in real time (the order they occurred in the story's world).

In a flashback text, these are DIFFERENT. A sequencing question always asks for chronological order — not narrative order. Students who number events in the order they read them will get the wrong answer whenever there is a flashback.

Worked Sequencing Example

[A] The train pulled away from the platform and Elsa pressed her face against the cold glass, watching the city dissolve into suburbs, then fields. She had not been on this line in eleven years.

[B] It had all begun with a phone call from her sister. "Come home," Maya had said. "Dad's not well." Those two sentences had ended Elsa's Tuesday as she knew it.

[C] She remembered the last time she had made this journey — the summer she was sixteen, heading to their grandmother's cottage for the final time before it was sold. Her father had driven, and the three of them had sung badly to the radio all the way.

[D] The train slowed for a signal and Elsa sat back, watching a crow settle on a fence post. She would arrive in two hours. She was not ready.

[E] Her grandmother had died the following winter. It was the first funeral Elsa had attended, and she had not known how to feel — only that the cottage was gone, and that something else had changed that she couldn't yet name.

Question: Number the events below 1–5 in the order they happened chronologically (the order they occurred in real life, not the order they appear in the text).

  • Elsa receives a phone call from her sister (Event B)
  • Elsa travels to her grandmother's cottage aged 16 (Event C)
  • Elsa's grandmother dies (Event E)
  • Elsa boards the train to go home (Events A & D)
  • Elsa's sister says "Come home — Dad's not well" (part of Event B)

THE TRAP: Students who number in narrative order (A=1, B=2, C=3, D=4, E=5) are wrong.

CORRECT chronological order:

  1. Elsa travels to grandmother's cottage aged 16 (Event C — happened 11 years ago)
  2. Grandmother dies the following winter (Event E — "the following winter" after Event C)
  3. Elsa receives Maya's phone call (Event B — the trigger for the present journey)
  4. Elsa boards the train and presses her face to the glass (Event A)
  5. The train slows; Elsa sits back (Event D)

Key reasoning: Events C and E are in the past — they are flashbacks triggered by Elsa's memories. The phrase "eleven years" and "the following winter" (after age 16) place them long before the present train journey. Events A and D are the present narrative — A occurs before D because the train is moving in A and slowing in D.

⑨ Sentence-Level Structure: Hook, Shift, Echo

Structure operates at the level of individual sentences and paragraphs, not just whole texts. Three structural moments are particularly important for exam questions.

The Hook (Opening)

The first sentence of a text sets the tone, establishes the register, and creates the first impression. Types of hook:

  • Question hook: "Have you ever wondered what happens to the food you throw away?"
  • Shock statistic: "One in three children in this country goes to school without breakfast."
  • Dramatic statement: "The building was on fire before anyone thought to call for help."
  • In medias res: Beginning in the middle of action — "She ran."

The Shift (Volta)

A moment in the text where the direction changes — a new argument is introduced, a counter-point appears, the mood changes, or a character's perspective shifts. Signal words: "However," "Yet," "Suddenly," "But," "On the other hand," "Nevertheless."

Effect: The Shift prevents the text from feeling one-sided. It creates complexity, tension, or surprise. The reader's expectations are disrupted.

The Echo (Circular Ending)

When the conclusion returns to an image, phrase, or idea from the opening. This creates a sense of completion — the reader ends where they began, but with new understanding.

Example: Opens with: "The lighthouse had stood for two hundred years." Closes with: "The lighthouse still stood — but its keeper was gone, and the light had not shone in a decade." Same image, transformed meaning.

⑩ Heading Match Questions

The heading match question gives you four possible headings and asks which one best describes a paragraph. "Reading the first sentence" is a starting point — but it is not enough. Here is the full process.

Full Reasoning Process — Four Steps

  1. Read the whole paragraph — not just the first sentence. Identify its overall function and main point.
  2. Rule out headings that are too specific — a heading that only captures one example or detail from the paragraph is wrong.
  3. Rule out headings that are too broad — a heading that could apply to any paragraph in the text is wrong.
  4. Check tone and scope match — the heading should match the paragraph's register (formal/informal) and cover all its main content, not just part of it.

Fully Worked Heading Match Example

Paragraph: "The Roman road network was not merely a convenience for travel — it was the infrastructure that made empire possible. Roads allowed armies to march quickly across thousands of miles of territory, tax collectors to reach distant provinces, and news to travel from the frontier to Rome in days rather than months. Without this network, the empire's vast size would have been its undoing."


Options:

A) Roman Armies on the March
B) The Roman Empire
C) How Roads Held the Empire Together
D) The Speed of Ancient Communication


Elimination process:

A) Roman Armies on the March — Too specific. Armies are mentioned, but the paragraph also covers tax collectors and news travel. This heading only captures one of three examples. ELIMINATED — too specific

B) The Roman Empire — Too broad. This heading could describe any paragraph about Rome in any text about the Roman Empire. ELIMINATED — too broad

D) The Speed of Ancient Communication — Partially correct (news travel is mentioned) but misses the main point — roads as infrastructure enabling empire. Too narrow. ELIMINATED — wrong scope

C) How Roads Held the Empire Together ✓ — Covers the paragraph's main claim (roads = empire's infrastructure), matches the tone (factual, historical), and the scope (armies + tax + communication = "held together"). CORRECT

⑪ Practice Passage 1 — Non-Fiction

The Hidden Cost of Food Waste

Every year, approximately one third of all food produced globally is lost or wasted. This staggering figure represents not only an economic catastrophe but an environmental one: the greenhouse gases emitted by rotting food make the global food system one of the largest contributors to climate change.

Why Waste Happens

The causes are varied and interconnected. At the consumer level, overpurchasing and poor meal planning account for roughly 60% of household waste. Supermarkets bear significant responsibility too: "cosmetic standards" that reject misshapen fruit and vegetables — perfectly edible, simply imperfect-looking — result in tonnes of produce being discarded before they ever reach a shelf. Consequently, the burden of waste is shared across the entire supply chain.

A Problem That Can Be Solved

Solutions exist at every level. Governments in France and South Korea have introduced legislation requiring supermarkets to donate unsold food rather than discard it, with measurable success. At home, simple changes — writing shopping lists, planning meals in advance, understanding date labels — can reduce household waste by up to 30%. The technology also exists to redistribute surplus food via apps that connect restaurants and shops with local food banks within hours.

Yet for all these tools, progress remains frustratingly slow. Consumer habits are deeply ingrained, corporate interests resist regulation, and many people remain unaware of the scale of the problem. The solutions are clear; the will to implement them, it seems, is not.

⑫ Practice Passage 2 — Fiction

[Paragraph 1] The letter had arrived that morning. Jonah had read it three times and was now sitting at the kitchen table, his tea growing cold beside him, watching the neighbour's cat cross the garden wall as though the world had not just shifted on its axis.

[Paragraph 2] He thought of the summer he was eight — the summer his father had taken him fishing at Hazel Beck for the last time. He hadn't known it would be the last time. That was the cruelty of lasts: you never recognised them until they were behind you. The trout had been rising, silver flashes in the brown water, and his father had laughed at something Jonah said, a real laugh, the kind that came from deep in the chest and filled the whole river valley.

[Paragraph 3] His father had died the following spring. Forty-two years old. Jonah had been nine and had spent weeks afterwards trying to remember if there had been any signs, as though grief could be made into a kind of detective work, something manageable. There had been no signs.

[Paragraph 4] He picked up the letter again. His own son, now twenty-six, was getting married. The date was in July — the same month as that last fishing trip. Jonah did not think this was a coincidence. He was not sure whether to laugh or cry, so he did neither: he simply folded the letter carefully along its original crease and put it in the drawer where he kept important things.

Modelled Top-Mark Response — Q5

Paragraph Connection — 3-Mark Model Answer

Paragraph 4 returns to the present WHAT after the sustained flashback of paragraphs 2 and 3. The phrase "He picked up the letter again" HOW — direct echo of para 1 directly echoes the opening of paragraph 1, creating a circular movement: Jonah has been on an internal journey into the past and now returns to the same moment he left.

The writer places this return here WHY to show that the past and present are not separate — the detail that July is "the same month as that last fishing trip" fuses the memory of loss with the announcement of new life. The connection is not accidental: the writer uses it to suggest that grief and joy can occupy the same emotional space simultaneously.

The final image — Jonah folding the letter "carefully along its original crease" and placing it "where he kept important things" — HOW — connects para 4 back to all previous paras suggests he is treating this letter with the same reverence he gives to his memories of his father. Paragraph 4 does not resolve the emotion: it holds it, just as Jonah holds both the letter and the past.
