Section 1
Active Reading — The GIST Framework
▼

Before you answer a single question, you need to understand the passage. The GIST framework gives you a four-point checklist for your very first read-through. Do this quickly — no more than 3–4 minutes.

G
General Idea
What is this story basically about?
I
Interaction
Who are the characters and how do they relate?
S
Setting
Where and when does this take place?
T
Tone
What is the overall feeling or atmosphere?

Step 1 — Read the blurb first

The introduction or blurb before the passage is free information. It often tells you the time period (Victorian, modern), the genre (Gothic, adventure, realistic fiction), and sometimes who the narrator is. Read it carefully before the passage itself.

Step 2 — The first pass (3 minutes)

Read quickly to get the GIST. Do not stop to figure out difficult words yet. Your job is to understand what is happening and who is involved.

Step 3 — Annotation (1 minute)

After the first read, go back and do two things:

  • Underline "power words" — strong verbs (e.g. "lurched," "snatched") and evocative adjectives (e.g. "ashen," "trembling").
  • Circle shift points — places where the mood, pace, or feeling changes. These are almost always the subject of a question.
💡 Pro tip: If you spot a word you don't know, circle it lightly. You may need it for a vocabulary question — and reading on often helps you guess its meaning from context.

Contextual clues to spot

  • Sentence length: Very short sentences = tension. Long, flowing sentences = calm description.
  • Verb choices: "Crept" vs "walked" vs "strode" — each tells you something completely different.
  • Paragraph breaks: A sudden short paragraph in the middle of a long passage signals a key moment.

Section 2
The Three Question Types
▼

Almost every question on a GL Assessment fiction paper falls into one of three types. Recognising the type as soon as you read a question tells you exactly what to do.

🔍 Retrieval
🔎 Inference
✍️ Literary Analysis
Retrieval — "The WHAT"

These questions ask for facts that are literally in the text. There is one right answer.

Command words to spot: Identify, Give two reasons, According to the text, Find a word that means…, List three things…

Strategy: Scan for the exact keywords from the question. Copy the text accurately — do not paraphrase or add your own ideas. If the text says the sky was "charcoal grey," don't write "black."

Common mistake: Students add extra information from their own imagination. If it's not in the text, don't write it.

Inference — "The WHY and HOW"

These questions ask you to work something out that the writer hasn't directly stated. Think of yourself as a detective — you're following clues.

Command words to spot: Explain why…, What does this suggest…, What impression do you get…, Why do you think…, How does the character feel…?

Strategy: Always root your answer in a specific word or phrase from the text. "Because the writer uses the word '___'" is always a stronger opening than a general comment about the plot.

Common mistake: Giving a "plot summary" answer instead of exploring the feelings or motives behind events. Inference is about the why, not the what.

Literary Analysis — "The CRAFT"

These questions ask about how the writer has crafted the passage — their language choices, techniques, and the effect these create on the reader.

Command words to spot: How does the writer use language to…?, Analyse the effect of…, What effect does [technique] create?, How does the author build tension/atmosphere?

Strategy: Always use the formula: Technique → Quotation → Effect on the reader. Never identify a technique without explaining the effect it has. See Section 9 for the full template.

Common mistake: Spotting the technique but not explaining its effect. "The writer uses alliteration" earns no marks on its own. "The alliteration of 's' sounds in '___' creates a hissing, sinister effect that makes the reader feel uneasy" earns marks.

⏱ Time per question type: Retrieval questions should take the least time — roughly 1 minute per mark. Inference: 1.5 minutes per mark. Literary analysis: 2 minutes per mark. Attempt retrieval questions first to secure easy marks before tackling the harder ones.

Section 3
Literary Techniques — Glossary of 20 Devices
▼

Click any technique to expand its definition, example, and the key effect it creates. The green box always tells you what to write about the effect — this is what earns marks.

🔵 Sound 🟢 Image 🟡 Structure 🟣 Rhetoric 🔴 Mood
Simile 🟢 Image ▼
DefinitionA direct comparison using like or as.
"Her face was as pale as candle wax."
✅ Effect: Creates a vivid mental image that makes the reader visualise the subject precisely and feel the intended emotion.
Metaphor 🟢 Image ▼
DefinitionA comparison that states something is something else — no "like" or "as."
"The city was a beast that never slept."
✅ Effect: More powerful than a simile — it presents the comparison as a truth, forcing the reader to fully inhabit the image.
Personification 🟢 Image ▼
DefinitionGiving human qualities, feelings, or actions to non-human things.
"The wind howled in frustration as the storm approached."
✅ Effect: Makes the natural world feel alive and threatening (or comforting), drawing the reader into the emotional atmosphere of the scene.
Onomatopoeia 🔵 Sound ▼
DefinitionWords that mimic the sounds they describe.
"The fire crackled and hissed as the logs shifted."
✅ Effect: Immerses the reader in the scene by recreating its sounds; makes the writing feel vivid and immediate.
Alliteration 🔵 Sound ▼
DefinitionRepetition of the same consonant sound at the start of nearby words.
"The dark, damp dungeon dripped with despair."
✅ Effect: Creates a rhythm that can feel menacing (hard sounds: b, d, k) or soft and soothing (s, l, w). It links words together to emphasise mood.
Sibilance 🔵 Sound ▼
DefinitionA specific type of alliteration using 's,' 'sh,' or 'z' sounds repeatedly.
"She slipped silently through the shadows."
✅ Effect: Creates a hissing, whispering sound that can feel secretive, serpentine, or menacing depending on the context.
Pathetic Fallacy 🔴 Mood ▼
DefinitionWhen the weather or natural environment reflects the mood or emotions of the characters.
"A cold rain began to fall as she received the news."
✅ Effect: Deepens the emotional atmosphere without stating feelings directly; the reader feels the emotion through the landscape rather than being told it.
Hyperbole 🟣 Rhetoric ▼
DefinitionDeliberate and dramatic exaggeration for effect — not meant to be taken literally.
"She had told him a million times not to be late."
✅ Effect: Emphasises the intensity of a feeling or situation; can create humour, frustration, or a sense of dramatic scale depending on tone.
Juxtaposition 🟡 Structure ▼
DefinitionPlacing two contrasting ideas, characters, or images side by side.
"The magnificent feast was laid out beneath a ceiling of crumbling plaster."
✅ Effect: Forces the reader to compare two contrasting ideas, highlighting differences and creating a sense of irony, tension, or social comment.
Repetition 🟣 Rhetoric ▼
DefinitionDeliberately repeating a word, phrase, or sentence structure for emphasis.
"She ran and ran and ran, but she could not escape."
✅ Effect: Hammers a feeling or idea into the reader's mind, creating urgency, obsession, or emotional weight depending on what is repeated.
Rule of Three 🟣 Rhetoric ▼
DefinitionGrouping three words, phrases, or ideas together, as three things feel natural and complete to the human brain.
"The room was cold, damp, and utterly silent."
✅ Effect: Creates a pleasing rhythm and makes the point feel conclusive and memorable; the third item often lands hardest and carries the most weight.
Rhetorical Question 🟣 Rhetoric ▼
DefinitionA question asked for effect — not to receive an answer.
"Was there any point in continuing?"
✅ Effect: Draws the reader in, forcing them to engage with the narrator's dilemma or emotion; creates a moment of intimacy or uncertainty.
Polysyndeton 🟡 Structure ▼
DefinitionUsing many conjunctions (and, but, or) in quick succession, often where commas would normally be used.
"He grabbed the rope and swung and landed and rolled and ran."
✅ Effect: Creates a breathless, relentless pace that conveys action or overwhelm; the reader feels rushed forward through events.
Asyndeton 🟡 Structure ▼
DefinitionOmitting conjunctions entirely, listing items separated only by commas.
"She was tired, cold, hungry, alone."
✅ Effect: Creates a clipped, stark rhythm — each item lands separately, giving equal weight to all of them and making the list feel exhaustive and heavy.
Imagery (Sensory) 🟢 Image ▼
DefinitionLanguage that appeals to the five senses: sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch.
"The sweet, smoky scent of pine needles filled the damp air."
✅ Effect: Places the reader physically inside the scene; the more senses engaged, the more immersive and believable the writing feels.
Symbolism 🟢 Image ▼
DefinitionUsing an object, colour, or event to represent a larger idea or theme.
"The single candle guttered and died, plunging the room into darkness."
✅ Effect: Layers meaning beneath the surface of the story; the reader finds deeper themes without being told them directly, which is more powerful and memorable.
Short Sentences / Fragmentation 🟡 Structure ▼
DefinitionVery short or incomplete sentences used deliberately to control pace and emphasis.
"He turned. The door was open. She was gone."
✅ Effect: Slows the reader down and creates impact; each short sentence lands like a punch, building tension and forcing the reader to pause at every revelation.
Irony 🔴 Mood ▼
DefinitionWhen words mean the opposite of their literal meaning, or when events turn out contrary to expectations.
"Oh, what a delightful surprise," she said flatly, not looking up from her book.
✅ Effect: Creates layers of meaning; the reader feels clever for noticing the gap between what is said and what is meant, and it often reveals character.
Foreshadowing 🟡 Structure ▼
DefinitionA hint or warning of events to come, planted earlier in the story.
"He had no way of knowing, as he laughed and reached for the door handle, that this would be the last time he would ever laugh at all."
✅ Effect: Creates a sense of dread or anticipation; the reader is made uneasy, knowing something bad is coming even before it arrives.
Free Indirect Discourse 🔴 Mood ▼
DefinitionWhen the narrator blends with a character's thoughts and feelings — not quite narration, not quite dialogue — written in third person but feeling like first person.
"She looked at the envelope. What could it possibly say? Nothing good — it never was."
✅ Effect: Creates deep intimacy with the character's inner world; the reader feels they are inside the character's mind, making emotional responses more immediate and powerful.

Section 4
Vocabulary-in-Context Questions
▼

Vocabulary questions are among the most common on GL Assessment papers. They come in two forms: "What does the word X mean in this context?" and "Find a word in paragraph 3 that means…". Both require the same skills.

Key principle: A word's meaning in a story can be different from its everyday meaning. The context — the words and sentences around it — is your most reliable guide. Never guess from the word alone.

Connotation vs Denotation

This is a crucial distinction that GL papers often test directly:

  • Denotation = the dictionary definition, the literal meaning. The denotation of "serpent" is "a snake."
  • Connotation = the emotional associations a word carries. The connotations of "serpent" are treachery, evil, and danger — far more powerful than simply "snake."

When a question asks "What does the writer's choice of the word X suggest?" they are asking about connotation, not denotation. Always think about the emotional baggage a word carries.

Four-Step Strategy

Step 1

Read the surrounding sentences

Read at least two full sentences before and after the word. The context almost always contains clues. Look for contrasting words (but, however, although) or supporting words (and, also, furthermore) that point to the word's meaning.

Step 2

Check the register

Is the passage formal or informal? Threatening or comforting? The register tells you whether the unknown word is likely to have positive or negative connotations. A threatening atmosphere won't contain a word meaning "cheerful."

Step 3

Look for roots, prefixes, and suffixes

Many English words come from Latin or Greek. Mal- means bad (malevolent, malicious). Ben- means good (benevolent, benign). -ous means full of (ominous, joyous). Breaking a word into parts often reveals its meaning.

Step 4

The synonym substitution test

Once you have a candidate meaning, substitute your synonym back into the sentence and read it aloud. Does the sentence still make sense? Does it fit the tone? If yes, you're right. If the sentence sounds odd, try a different synonym.

Example in action: "She regarded him with a glacial stare." You might not know "glacial" precisely. The context: a cold atmosphere, a formal register. The root: "glaci-" = ice (glacier). Substitution test: "icy stare" — does that fit? Yes, perfectly. So "glacial" here means cold, unfriendly, and emotionless.

Common Trap: Choosing the wrong register

If the passage is set in a tense, dark moment and the question asks what "smouldering" means, the answer is not "gently warming" even though that is technically a definition. In context, "smouldering" suggests suppressed fury or hidden danger. Always match your answer to the tone.

Section 5
Narrative Voice — Who is Telling the Story?
▼

The narrative voice is one of the most powerful tools a writer has. Knowing who is telling the story, and how much they know, changes everything about how you read and analyse a passage.

First Person vs Third Person

First Person

The narrator is a character in the story

Uses "I," "me," "my," "we." You experience events through their eyes and with their bias.

"I crept down the stairs, my heart hammering."

GL question stem: "How does the first-person narrator's perspective affect the reader's understanding of events?"

Third Person

The narrator is outside the story

Uses "he," "she," "they," character names. Can feel more objective — but watch for bias.

"She crept down the stairs, her heart hammering."

GL question stem: "How does the writer create sympathy for the character despite using a third-person narrator?"

Limited vs Omniscient Narrator

Limited

Knows one character's thoughts

The narrator only shows us the inner world of one character. Events outside their experience remain mysterious — just as they do for that character. Creates suspense and intimacy.

Effect: The reader is as confused or frightened as the character. Dramatic irony is impossible from this position.

Omniscient

Knows everything

The narrator can access any character's thoughts and knows the outcome before it happens. This creates the possibility of dramatic irony — where the reader knows more than the character.

Effect: Can create dread when the reader sees danger the character doesn't.

Unreliable Narrator

Sometimes the narrator cannot be fully trusted — either because they are a child with limited understanding, emotionally unstable, or deliberately hiding something. Watch for:

  • Gaps or inconsistencies in their account
  • Other characters reacting in ways that don't match the narrator's description of events
  • The narrator claiming things the evidence doesn't support
GL question stem: "How does the writer make the narrator seem unreliable?" — Look for inconsistencies, contradictions, and moments where the narrator's account seems incomplete or biased.

Free Indirect Discourse

This is a technique where the narrator blends their voice with a character's thoughts — written in third person but sounding like first person. It is extremely common in literary fiction at 11+ level.

Why had nobody told her? She had a right to know. The whole thing was perfectly ridiculous — she would have words with them in the morning. Every last one of them.

Notice: there are no speech marks and no "she thought." The narrator has dissolved into the character's mind. This technique creates deep intimacy and allows us to hear a character's exact emotional voice without it being presented as direct thought.

GL question stem: "How does the writer reveal the character's feelings in this paragraph?" — Free indirect discourse is often the answer. Name it and explain the effect.

Section 6
Tone vs Mood — A Critical Distinction
▼

These two terms are often confused, but GL Assessment distinguishes them carefully. Getting this right can be the difference between a band 2 and a band 3 answer.

TONE

The author's attitude

Tone is the feeling the writer brings to the story — it's their stance or perspective. Ask yourself: "How does the writer feel about what they are describing?"

Example: A writer can describe poverty with a pitying tone, an angry tone, or even a darkly humorous tone.
MOOD

The atmosphere the reader feels

Mood is the emotional atmosphere the reader experiences. Ask yourself: "How does reading this make me feel?"

Example: A passage can create a mood of unease, claustrophobia, or warmth — regardless of what the writer's own tone is.

Word Bank: Tone adjectives

Use these when asked about the narrator's or writer's attitude:

sardonic melancholic wistful detached reverent contemptuous sympathetic ironic bitter resigned urgent nostalgic admiring reflective critical celebratory

Word Bank: Mood adjectives

Use these when asked about the atmosphere or how the passage makes the reader feel:

ominous foreboding oppressive claustrophobic eerie tense elegiac serene unsettling idyllic turbulent desolate charged tranquil menacing wistful
⚠ Avoid these weak words: "scary," "nice," "sad," "bad," "good." Replace them with precise adjectives from the banks above. "The mood is scary" earns 0 marks. "The mood is foreboding and oppressive" earns marks.

How to identify tone and mood in a passage

For tone: Look at verb and adjective choices. Is the writer selecting words that mock, celebrate, mourn, or expose? Notice the register — formal and elevated language signals reverence or seriousness; colloquial language signals intimacy or satire.

For mood: Look at pathetic fallacy, sentence length, the sounds of the words (harsh consonants = harsh mood), and the pace of events. A slow, detailed description of darkness creates a different mood from a rapid sequence of short sentences about the same thing.

Section 7
PEEL — Method + Fully Worked Model Answer
▼

PEEL is the structure you use for inference and literary analysis answers. Every multi-mark question should follow this shape. Here is what each letter means, followed by a complete worked example.

P
Point
Your direct answer / claim
E
Evidence
Short quote (3–5 words)
E
Explain
What does the quote prove?
L
Link
Tie back to the question

The model passage

The kitchen had once been warm, Elsa thought. She remembered the smell of it — bread and cinnamon, the clatter of her mother's wooden spoon. Now the room waited in silence, grey with dust, the old table scarred with rings she did not recognise. She ran her finger across the surface and pulled it away quickly, as if the touch itself might hurt her.

The question

"How does the writer convey Elsa's feelings when she returns to the kitchen? Use evidence from the text to support your answer." [4 marks]

The model answer

P — Point
The writer conveys Elsa's deep sense of loss and grief at returning to a place that no longer feels like home.
E — Evidence
This is shown through the contrast between the past and present: she recalls "the smell of it — bread and cinnamon," but the kitchen is now "grey with dust."
E — Explain
The sensory warmth of bread and cinnamon represents the comfort and security Elsa once felt in this space. The juxtaposition with the cold, colourless image of grey dust forces the reader to feel the contrast as sharply as Elsa does. The dust is not just physical — it suggests abandonment, the passage of time, and things that will not return. The detail that the table bears "rings she did not recognise" deepens this: the space has changed without her, and she is now a stranger in her own past.
L — Link
Overall, the writer creates a powerful sense of displacement: Elsa's feelings of grief are not stated directly, but instead conveyed through the physical details of the room, which allows the reader to experience her loss as their own.
Why this scores full marks: It makes a clear claim, uses two well-chosen short quotations, explains both the specific word choices and the technique (juxtaposition), notes the effect on the reader, and links back to the question. Notice that the word "grief" never appears in the passage — this is genuine inference.

Section 8
STEAL — Characterisation
▼

When a question asks you to describe or analyse a character, use STEAL to structure your thinking. A character's personality is revealed not through labels ("she is kind") but through these five channels.

S
Speech
What and how they speak
T
Thoughts
Their inner monologue
E
Effect
How others react to them
A
Actions
What they do under pressure
L
Looks
Physical appearance as symbol

How to use STEAL in an answer

You do not need to address all five elements in every answer. Choose the two or three that are most richly evidenced in the passage. Quantity of elements does not matter — depth of analysis does.

S — Speech

Analyse what a character says, but also how they say it. Do they use short, clipped sentences (suggesting anger or authority)? Do they hedge and qualify (suggesting anxiety or lack of confidence)? Do they ask questions rather than make statements (suggesting vulnerability)?

T — Thoughts

In first-person narratives, you have direct access to a character's inner world. In third-person narratives, look for free indirect discourse (Section 5). What worries the character? What do they value? What do they notice — and what do they ignore?

E — Effect on Others

How do other characters respond? Do they defer, challenge, avoid, or admire? A character who makes others nervous without doing anything explicitly threatening is a very different type of person from one who earns obvious affection. The reactions of others are often the most reliable evidence of a character's personality because they are not filtered through the character's own biases.

A — Actions

This is especially important under pressure. Character is most clearly revealed by what people do when things are difficult. A character who pauses to help someone else while running from danger is telling you everything. Look also at small, habitual actions — they reveal character more subtly than dramatic events.

L — Looks

Physical descriptions in literary fiction are almost never neutral. The writer chooses what to describe and how to describe it. A character described with "sharp eyes" is presented differently from one with "warm eyes," even though both can see perfectly well. Ask: what is the writer suggesting about this person through their appearance?

Advanced point: Sometimes STEAL elements contradict each other — a character's actions are generous, but their thoughts are calculating. This tension is itself worth analysing: it can suggest a character who is more complex than they first appear, or who is hiding something.

Section 9
The "Effect on the Reader" Formula
▼

This is the single most important upgrade you can make to your literary analysis answers. Every answer that identifies a technique must end by explaining the effect on the reader. Without it, you will never score the top mark for a literary analysis question.

The non-negotiable template

The writer uses [technique]
through the phrase "short quotation",
which [explain what this does — describe the imagery, sound, structure].
This makes the reader feel [specific emotion/response]
because [explain why this effect occurs].

Worked example — before and after

❌ Answer WITHOUT effect on reader (scores 1/3):
The writer uses personification when they write "the shadows crept forward." This is personification because shadows are not alive and cannot creep.
✅ Answer WITH effect on reader (scores 3/3):
The writer uses personification through the phrase "the shadows crept forward," giving the darkness a deliberate, predatory intent — as though it is hunting the character. This makes the reader feel a deep sense of unease and vulnerability, because the natural world seems to have turned against the protagonist, removing their sense of safety.

Useful phrases for effect on the reader

  • "This makes the reader feel…"
  • "The reader is made to sense…"
  • "This creates a feeling of… in the reader, because…"
  • "The reader is unsettled/comforted/alarmed by…"
  • "This causes the reader to share the character's…"
  • "The reader is drawn into…"
Remember: You are writing about a fictional reader responding to the text, not about your own personal feelings. Never write "I felt scared when I read this." Write "this creates fear in the reader."

Section 10
Comparative Questions
▼

Some GL papers include questions that ask you to compare two sections of the same passage: "How does the writer's presentation of the setting in paragraph 1 differ from paragraph 4?" These require a specific approach.

The key rule: A comparative answer must contain a comparison — not just two separate descriptions. You need connecting words that signal the comparison: whereas, while, in contrast, however, on the other hand, unlike, by comparison.

Five-Step Method

1
Read both sections carefully. Note the key feeling/image/tone of each before writing anything.
2
Identify what they have in common. Even contrasting sections often share a theme or character — note this as a starting point.
3
Identify the key difference. Is it tone? Pace? Imagery? The character's emotional state? Focus on the most significant change, not every small difference.
4
Write your answer using the PEEL structure for each section, but link them with a comparative connector. Do not simply write about Section A and then Section B independently — weave the comparison throughout.
5
Conclude with a point about why the writer has made this shift. What effect does the change create? This is where top marks are earned.

Model comparison sentence

In paragraph [X], the writer presents the [setting/character/mood] as [quality A], shown through the phrase "quotation".
However, by paragraph [Y], this has shifted to [quality B], as the writer now uses "quotation".
This contrast suggests [interpretation], creating [effect on reader].

Useful connecting words

whereas while in contrast however on the other hand unlike by comparison similarly equally

Section 11
Timing & Question Sequencing — 45 Minutes
▼

A GL Assessment fiction comprehension is typically 45 minutes with a passage of 400–700 words and 8–12 questions. Knowing how to allocate your time is as important as knowing the content.

Phase Task Time Notes
Reading First read using GIST + annotation 5 min Read the blurb first. Circle shift points. Don't linger.
Retrieval Qs Answer all 1-mark fact questions 8 min ~1 min per mark. Do these first — they are the easiest marks to secure. Scan and find exact words.
Vocabulary Qs Word meaning / find-a-word questions 5 min Use the 4-step vocab strategy. Don't spend more than 2 min on any single word.
Inference Qs Why/how questions requiring PEEL 14 min ~1.5 min per mark. Use PEEL. Keep quotations short — 3 to 5 words only.
Literary Analysis Qs Language / technique questions 10 min ~2 min per mark. Always include technique + quotation + effect on reader. This is where top marks are earned.
Review Check for unanswered questions; re-read any answer you're unsure about 3 min Prioritise questions where you left blanks. Don't re-write answers that are already good.
The golden rule of sequencing: Always attempt questions in order of ease — retrieval first, vocabulary second, inference third, literary analysis last. This ensures you collect the easy marks before spending time on the harder ones.

Marks-to-minutes rule of thumb

  • 1-mark question → spend a maximum of 1 minute
  • 2-mark question → spend a maximum of 2–3 minutes
  • 3-mark question → spend a maximum of 4–5 minutes
  • 4-mark question → spend a maximum of 6–8 minutes

If a question is taking significantly longer than this, note your progress and move on. Return to it at the end if time allows. A half-answered 4-mark question is worth more than a perfect 1-mark question you spent five minutes on.

Marks as instructions

The number of marks beside a question is the most important clue on the paper. If a question is worth 3 marks, you need either three distinct points or one fully developed PEEL paragraph. Never give a one-line answer to a 3-mark question. Never write a paragraph for a 1-mark question.

Section 12
Common Pitfalls — And How to Fix Them
▼

These are the most frequent mistakes that cost students marks on GL papers. Read each one carefully — recognising the mistake is the first step to fixing it.

❌
Over-quoting
Students copy out long sections of the passage as their "evidence." A marker does not need to read the passage back to themselves — they want analysis.
✅ Fix: Keep all quotations to 3–5 words. Choose the most powerful words, not the longest extract. Embed quotes naturally into your sentence rather than starting a new line.
❌
Vague adjectives
Using words like "nice," "bad," "scary," "sad," or "interesting" to describe tone, mood, or character. These tell the marker nothing and will never earn more than 1 mark.
✅ Fix: Replace every vague adjective with a precise one. Use the Tone and Mood word banks in Section 6. "The atmosphere is ominous and foreboding" is infinitely more impressive than "the atmosphere is scary."
❌
Missing the mark allocation
Writing one line for a 3-mark question, or a long paragraph for a 1-mark question. The marks tell you exactly how much to write.
✅ Fix: Before writing any answer, glance at the marks. If it's 3 marks, plan 3 points or a full PEEL paragraph. If it's 1 mark, write one precise sentence and stop.
❌
Ignoring the tense
Writing "the author showed" or "the character felt." Analysis is always written in the present tense because the text always exists in the present.
✅ Fix: Always write: "the author shows," "the character feels," "the writer creates." The text does not change — it always does what it does.
❌
Adding your own ideas to retrieval answers
For retrieval questions, students often add logical-but-not-stated information from their own imagination. If it isn't in the text, it will not get the mark.
✅ Fix: For retrieval questions, treat the text as the only source. Locate the relevant section, use the exact language of the text, and do not embellish.
❌
Spotting techniques without explaining effects
"The writer uses a simile" is worth 0 marks on its own. Technique identification is only the starting point of an analysis answer, not the answer itself.
✅ Fix: Use the Effect on the Reader template from Section 9 every single time. Never name a technique without immediately explaining what it makes the reader feel or understand.
❌
Confusing tone and mood
Writing "the tone is ominous" when the question asks about the author's attitude — or vice versa. These are different things and mixing them up signals you don't understand the terms.
✅ Fix: Tone = writer's attitude (use the amber word bank). Mood = reader's experience of the atmosphere (use the teal word bank). See Section 6 for the full distinction.
❌
Personal response instead of analytical response
Writing "I felt scared when I read this passage" or "I think this story is very sad." GL Assessment requires analytical writing about the text, not a personal reaction to it.
✅ Fix: Remove "I" from all your analysis answers. Replace with "the reader feels," "this creates," "the writer makes the reader." You are analysing a text, not writing a diary.
❌
Spending too long on hard questions early
Getting stuck on a 2-mark inference question and spending 8 minutes on it, then running out of time for the 4-mark literary analysis at the end.
✅ Fix: Follow the timing guide in Section 11. If a question is taking too long, write your best partial answer, put a star beside it, and return to it at the end. Never sacrifice later questions for an earlier one.
