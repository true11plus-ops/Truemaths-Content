Part 1 — Foundation

What is a percentage, really?

Imagine a cricket match. The commentator says: "Virat hit the ball 8 out of 10 times he faced."

Your friend says: "My favourite player Rohit hit 16 out of 20 times."

Who was better? It's hard to compare — the totals are different.

Stop and think
What if both players had faced exactly 100 balls? Then comparing would be instant — you'd just look at the number and know.

That's all a percentage is. It's a way of saying "let me pretend the total is 100, and tell you how many parts I have."

The word comes from Latin: per centum = per hundred. A cricket century is 100 runs — same root word, same idea.

Why "scaling to 100" works — the step most books skip

With 16 out of 20, ask: what do I multiply 20 by to get 100?

The total was multiplied by 5. The number of parts must also be multiplied by 5 — otherwise the ratio would change and you'd be misrepresenting the fraction.

80

Key insight: Same multiplier top and bottom — the fraction's value doesn't change, only how it's written. 16/20 and 80/100 are identical fractions. We prefer the /100 version because it's a percentage.

Now both cricketers are directly comparable:

Virat — 8 of 10

Equal performance — and now you can see it at a glance.

Part 2 — Fractions to percentages

Converting any fraction to a percentage

The general method: to express a/b as a percentage, multiply by 100 ÷ b on both top and bottom. The shortcut — multiply the fraction by 100 — does exactly this in one step.

The rule and why it works: Multiplying a/b by 100 is the same as asking "if the total were 100, how many parts would I have?" It rescales the fraction to the /100 form — which is what percentage means.
A
Emma scored 14 out of 25. Multiply by 4 to get to 100. So 14 × 4 = 56%
B
A team won 3 out of 5 games. Multiply by 20. So 3 × 20 = 60%
C
A student got 17 out of 40. Multiply by 2.5 → 17 × 2.5 = 42.5% — multipliers don't have to be whole numbers.
Worked example — fraction to percentage
Emma's bracelet has 8 red beads and 12 blue beads. What percentage are red?
1
Find total: 8 + 12 = 20 beads
2
Write fraction: Red = 8 out of 20 = 8/20
3
Scale: 20 × 5 = 100, so 8 × 5 = 40. Answer: 40% red, 60% blue
Common mistake
Writing 8/12 instead of 8/20. Percentage is always out of the whole, not the other part.
40% red · 60% blue
Part 2b — Decimals and percentages

Decimals to percentages — same idea, one step

A decimal like 0.75 means "75 hundredths" — it's already a fraction of 1 whole. To express it as a percentage (out of 100), multiply by 100.

0.75
decimal
× 100
75%
percentage
Why "move the decimal two places right"? Multiplying by 100 shifts every digit two columns left in place value — which looks like the decimal point moving two places right. It's not a separate rule — it's the same multiplication, done mentally.
Worked example — decimal to percentage
Mark and Henry shared a donut. Henry ate 0.4 of it. What percent did each eat?
1
Henry: 0.4 × 100 = 40%
2
Mark: 100% − 40% = 60%
The whole is always 100%. If you know one person's share, subtract to get the other's.
Part 3 — Going backwards

Percentage → fraction → decimal

Converting to percentage means multiplying by 100. So converting from percentage means dividing by 100. Same road, opposite direction.

75%
percentage
÷ 100
75/100
= 3/4 fraction
Worked example — % to fraction
Convert 54% to a fraction in simplest form.
1
Write as fraction: 54/100
2
Simplify — HCF of 54 and 100 is 2: 27/50
27/50
Watch out — "fraction percent" is a trap
1/2% does NOT mean 50%. It means half of one percent — a tiny amount. First convert 1/2 to 0.5, then divide by 100: → 0.005. Always check whether the % sign comes after a fraction.
Part 4 — % of a quantity

What does "of" mean in maths?

When you see "40% of 500", the word of means multiply.

Why? 40% means 40 for every 100. In 500, there are five "hundreds." 40 × 5 = 200. The formula 40/100 × 500 does this exact counting — it's not arbitrary.
Think before calculating
Your friend wants 40% of ₹500. Before using any formula — is 40% more or less than half? Half of 500 is 250, so the answer should be less than 250. Now calculate and check: 40/100 × 500 = 200. Checks out.

Worked example — % of a quantity
In a hall of 600 seats, 75% were filled. How many seats were vacant?
1
Think first: 75% filled means 25% vacant. (75 + 25 = 100)
2
Find 25% of 600: 25/100 × 600 = 150 vacant seats
Working with the complement (100% − given%) often saves a step.
150 seats vacant

Worked examples

5 model problems — read the thinking, not just the steps

Example 1 — Fraction to percentage (beginner)
Riya's cricket team won 9 out of 15 matches this season. What percentage did they win?
1
Sense check first: 9/15 = 3/5. Is this more than half? Yes. Answer should be above 50%.
2
Convert: 9/15 × 100 = 60%
Simplifying 9/15 to 3/5 first makes the multiplication easier. Always look for that shortcut.
60% — above 50% as expected
Example 2 — Finding % of a quantity (beginner)
A shopkeeper has 240 mangoes. During a sale, 35% were sold by noon. How many mangoes were sold?
1
Estimate first: 35% ≈ one-third. One-third of 240 ≈ 80. Answer should be near 80.
2
Calculate: 35/100 × 240 = 84
A rough estimate before calculating is not optional — it's how you catch mistakes.
84 mangoes — close to estimate of 80
Example 3 — Reverse percentage (intermediate)
30% of students who tried out were selected. If 48 were selected, how many tried out in total?
1
Identify the structure: 30% of [Total] = 48. We know the part, not the whole.
2
Rearrange: Total = 48 × 100/30 = 160
3
Check: 30% of 160 = 48. ✓
When you know the % and the result but not the whole — divide, don't multiply. You're working backwards.
160 students tried out
Example 4 — Percentage change (intermediate)
A cycle costs ₹4,000 and is sold for ₹3,400 after a festival discount. What is the percentage discount?
1
Find the change: 4000 − 3400 = ₹600
2
Express over original: 600/4000 × 100 = 15%
% change = (Change ÷ Original) × 100. Always divide by the starting value — not the new one.
Common trap
Writing 600/3400. That divides by the new price, giving a wrong answer.
15% discount
Example 5 — Two-step reverse (advanced)
Meera's salary was increased by 20%. Her new salary is ₹18,000. What was her original salary? By what percentage should the new salary be reduced to return to the original?
1
Part A: New = 120% of Original. So 120/100 × Original = 18000 → Original = ₹15,000
2
Part B: Reduction needed = 18000 − 15000 = 3000. % of new salary = 3000/18000 × 100 = 16.67%
Critical insight: A 20% increase is NOT reversed by a 20% decrease. After a rise, the base is larger — so a smaller percentage is needed to bring it back. This surprises most students.
Original: ₹15,000 · Reduction needed: 16.67%
