Core Definitions

1

🔷 Face

A flat or curved surface on a 3D shape. Faces can be any 2D shape — squares, rectangles, triangles, circles, hexagons, and more.

Cube → 6 flat square faces

📏 Edge

The line where two faces meet. Edges can be straight (on polyhedra) or curved (on shapes like cylinders).

Cube → 12 straight edges

📍 Vertex (Vertices)

A corner point where three or more edges meet. Some curved shapes (like cones) have an apex, which counts as their only vertex.

Cube → 8 vertices
⚡ Flat vs Curved: A flat face is a perfect 2D polygon (e.g. a square or triangle). A curved face wraps around like the side of a cylinder or a ball. Shapes with any curved part are not polyhedra — Euler's Formula does not apply to them!

Euler's Formula

3
F + V − E = 2

For any polyhedron (flat faces only, no holes): Faces + Vertices − Edges always equals 2. Use this to check your answers!

🧊 Cube

Faces (F) = 6
Vertices (V) = 8
Edges (E) = 12
6 + 8 − 12 = 2 ✓
✅ Formula checks out!

🔺 Triangular Prism

✅ Formula checks out!

Volume of a Cuboid

4
V = l × w × h

l = length   w = width   h = height

Volume is measured in cm³ (cubic centimetres) or m³ (cubic metres)

📝 Worked Example

A box is 8 cm long, 5 cm wide, and 3 cm tall. What is its volume?

1
Write down the formula: V = l × w × h
2
Substitute the values: V = 8 × 5 × 3
3
Calculate: 8 × 5 = 40, then 40 × 3 = 120
Answer: 120 cm³

Practice Problem 1

A fish tank is 30 cm long, 15 cm wide, and 20 cm tall. What is its volume in cm³?

✅ 30 × 15 × 20 = 9,000 cm³

Practice Problem 2

A cuboid has a volume of 60 cm³. Its length is 5 cm and width is 4 cm. What is its height?

✅ 5 × 4 = 20. Then 60 ÷ 20 = 3 cm

Nets of 3D Shapes

5

A net is a flat (2D) shape that folds up to make a 3D shape. Each face of the 3D shape appears exactly once in the net.

Cube

6 squares in a cross shape

Cuboid

6 rectangles — 3 pairs of equal faces

Triangular Prism

3 rectangles + 2 triangles = 5 faces

Square-based Pyramid

1 square + 4 triangles = 5 faces

🔎 "Which Corner Meets Which?" — Worked Example (Cube Net)

Look at the cube net above (the cross-shaped one). In the exam, you're shown a net with corners labelled A, B, C... and asked which corners touch when it folds.

  1. Identify the faces — label each square in the net (Top, Front, Left, Right, Back, Bottom).
  2. Mentally fold — start from the front face, fold the top up, the bottom down, left and right in.
  3. Track corners — a corner shared by two faces in the net will meet the corner of the face that folds adjacent to it. The corners of the "top" face that are free (not shared with the front) will touch the rear-top corners when folded.
  4. Check your answer — count that the final shape has 8 corners total from 6 faces folding correctly.

💡 Tip: In the exam, trace the edges with your finger as you "fold" each face — it prevents mistakes!

Exam Tips

9
👁️

Hidden Lines & Edges

When shapes are drawn in 2D on your paper, dashed lines show the edges you cannot see. Always count those too! Many pupils miss 1–3 edges by forgetting the hidden ones.

📐

Prism Vertex Rule

A prism always has a constant cross-section. Vertices = 2 × corners of the base shape. E.g. an octagonal prism: 8 × 2 = 16 vertices. This works for every prism!

🗺️

Net Visualisation

Fold in your mind: start with the front face fixed, then fold neighbours toward you one at a time. Trace edges with a finger on the paper. Which free edges will join when the shape closes?

✅

Euler's Formula Check

After counting F, E, V — always verify: F + V − E = 2. If you get a different answer, you've miscounted. Recount edges first (most common error).

🎯

Curved Shapes Exception

Euler's Formula only works for polyhedra. Never apply it to spheres, cylinders, or cones — they have curved faces and the formula breaks down.

🔎

Read Questions Carefully

The exam often asks for vertices, edges, and faces in that order — not alphabetical. Re-read each question and circle the keyword (faces? edges? vertices?) before answering.

🔗

Connection to Non-Verbal Reasoning (NVR)

10

🧩 Your 3D shape knowledge helps you score in NVR too!

The GL Assessment has a separate Non-Verbal Reasoning paper. It tests your ability to think visually and spatially — and 3D shapes appear directly in several question types.

🗺️ Cube Nets You'll be shown a flat net and asked which 3D shape it folds into — or which letter/pattern will end up on which face.
🔄 Spatial Rotation You're shown a 3D shape and must identify what it looks like rotated. Knowing faces, edges, and vertices helps you track which part is which.
🪞 Mirror Images 3D shapes reflected in a mirror — you need to identify the correct mirrored version from four options.
🔢 Sequences & Patterns Shapes may appear in sequences where the number of faces or edges follows a pattern. Knowing your shape properties lets you spot the rule quickly.

💡 Study tip: After learning this Maths guide, practise GL NVR past papers that include cube nets and 3D rotation questions. The same shapes (cube, cuboid, prism, pyramid) appear in both papers — double value from your revision!
