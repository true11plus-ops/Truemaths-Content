Angle Types

An angle is the amount of turn between two straight lines that meet at a point. We measure angles in degrees (°). A full turn is 360°.

Acute
less than 90°
Right
exactly 90°
Obtuse
90° – 180°
Straight
exactly 180°
Reflex
180° – 360°

Acute — less than 90°

Right — exactly 90°

Obtuse — 90° to 180°

Straight — exactly 180°

Reflex — 180° to 360°

⭐
Exam Tip GL often shows an angle and asks you to name the type, or gives a value like 145° and asks "is this acute, obtuse, or reflex?" Always check: is it less than 90°, between 90° and 180°, or greater than 180°?
2

Core Angle Rules

These three rules are used in almost every GL angle question. Learn them by heart.

Rule 1 — Angles on a straight line add up to 180°

Angles on a straight line = 180°
Worked Example

One angle on a straight line is 65°. What is the other angle?

Other angle = 180° − 65° = 115°

Rule 2 — Angles around a point add up to 360°

Angles around a point = 360°
Worked Example

Three angles meet at a point: 90°, 135°, and angle x. Find x.

90° + 135° + x = 360°

225° + x = 360°

x = 360° − 225° = 135°

Rule 3 — Vertically opposite angles are equal

When two straight lines cross, the angles opposite each other are always equal.
Worked Example

Two lines cross. One angle is 42°. What are the other three angles?

Opposite angle = 42°  |  Other pair = 180° − 42° = 138° each
⭐
Exam Tip GL loves "find the missing angle" questions. Always write down which rule you're using: "angles on a straight line," "angles around a point," or "vertically opposite." You'll almost always need Rule 1 or Rule 2.

Triangle Types

All triangles: interior angles add up to 180°

Equilateral

  • All 3 sides equal
  • All 3 angles = 60°
  • 3 lines of symmetry

Isosceles

  • 2 sides equal
  • 2 base angles equal
  • 1 line of symmetry

Scalene

  • No sides equal
  • No angles equal
  • No lines of symmetry

Right-Angled

  • One angle = 90°
  • Can be isosceles or scalene
  • Other two angles sum to 90°
Worked Example — Find the Missing Angle

An isosceles triangle has two equal angles of 55° each. Find the third angle.

55° + 55° + third angle = 180°

110° + third angle = 180°

Third angle = 180° − 110° = 70°
⭐
Exam Tip For isosceles triangles, GL often gives you just one base angle and asks for the apex angle (or vice versa). Always use: the two base angles are equal, and all three angles sum to 180°.

Quick visual guide

Square / Rectangle

  • Square: all sides equal
  • Rectangle: only opposite sides equal

Rhombus

  • "Pushed-over" square
  • All sides equal, no right angles

Parallelogram

  • Opposite sides parallel & equal
  • Looks like a slanted rectangle

Trapezium

  • Exactly 1 pair of parallel sides
  • The two parallel sides are called bases

Kite

  • 2 pairs of adjacent equal sides
  • One diagonal is an axis of symmetry
Worked Example

A parallelogram has one angle of 70°. Find the other three angles.

Opposite angle = 70° (opposite angles in a parallelogram are equal)

Adjacent angles are co-interior → they sum to 180°

Other two angles = 180° − 70° = 110° each. So angles are 70°, 110°, 70°, 110°.
⭐
Exam Tip GL often asks you to identify a shape from its properties — e.g. "I have 4 equal sides but no right angles. What am I?" (rhombus). Memorise what makes each quadrilateral unique.

Regular Polygons & the Interior Angle Formula

Sum of interior angles = (n − 2) × 180°    where n = number of sides
Each interior angle (regular polygon) = (n − 2) × 180° ÷ n
PolygonSides (n)Angle sumEach angle (regular)
Triangle3(3−2)×180 = 180°60°
Quadrilateral4(4−2)×180 = 360°90°
Pentagon5(5−2)×180 = 540°108°
Hexagon6(6−2)×180 = 720°120°
Heptagon7(7−2)×180 = 900°≈128.6°
Octagon8(8−2)×180 = 1080°135°
Worked Example 1 — Find a missing angle

A regular hexagon has one angle labelled x. Find x.

Sum of interior angles = (6 − 2) × 180° = 4 × 180° = 720°

All angles equal, so each = 720° ÷ 6

x = 120°
Worked Example 2 — Find a missing angle in an irregular pentagon

A pentagon has four angles: 100°, 115°, 90°, 105°. Find the fifth angle.

Sum of all interior angles in a pentagon = (5 − 2) × 180° = 540°

100 + 115 + 90 + 105 = 410°

Fifth angle = 540° − 410° = 130°
⭐
Exam Tip This formula works for ANY polygon, regular or irregular. GL nearly always includes one question where you're given (n − 1) angles and must find the last. Just use the formula to get the total, then subtract the ones you know.
6

Regular vs Irregular Polygons

Regular polygon
✔ All sides are equal length
✔ All interior angles are equal
Irregular polygon
✖ Sides are NOT all equal
✖ Angles are NOT all equal
(but angle sum formula still works!)

The angle sum formula always works for both regular and irregular polygons — but only regular polygons let you divide the total equally to find each angle.

Key check: Is this polygon regular?

Look at a hexagon. If all 6 sides are the same length AND all 6 angles are 120° → it's regular. If one side is longer or one angle is 100° → it's irregular.

⭐
Exam Tip GL sometimes shows a shape and asks "is this regular or irregular?" Check both sides AND angles. A shape with equal sides but unequal angles is still irregular.

Symmetry

Lines of Symmetry

A line of symmetry divides a shape into two mirror-image halves. If you fold the shape along the line, both halves match exactly.

ShapeLines of symmetry
Equilateral triangle3
Isosceles triangle1
Scalene triangle0
Square4
Rectangle (non-square)2
Rhombus2
Parallelogram0
Trapezium (isosceles)1
Trapezium (general)0
Kite1
Regular pentagon5
Regular hexagon6
Regular octagon8
CircleInfinite

Shortcut: For any regular polygon, the number of lines of symmetry = number of sides.

Rotational Symmetry

A shape has rotational symmetry if it looks exactly the same after being rotated less than a full turn (360°). The order is how many times it looks the same during one full rotation.

ShapeOrder of rotational symmetry
Equilateral triangle3
Square4
Rectangle (non-square)2
Rhombus2
Parallelogram2
Regular pentagon5
Regular hexagon6
Kite1 (no rotational symmetry)
Scalene triangle1 (no rotational symmetry)

Note: Order 1 means no rotational symmetry (it only looks the same after a full 360° turn).

Shortcut: For any regular polygon, the order of rotational symmetry = number of sides.

⭐
Exam Tip The most-tested fact: a parallelogram has NO lines of symmetry but HAS order-2 rotational symmetry. Students often get this wrong. Also remember: a rectangle has 2 lines of symmetry (through the midpoints of opposite sides), NOT through the corners.

Transformations

A transformation moves or changes a shape. The original shape is called the object; the result is called the image.

1. Reflection

A shape is flipped over a mirror line. Every point of the image is the same distance from the mirror line as the matching point on the object, but on the opposite side. The shape stays the same size.

Reflection in a vertical mirror line

2. Rotation

A shape is turned around a fixed point called the centre of rotation. You need to know the centre, the angle (e.g. 90°), and the direction (clockwise or anticlockwise).

Rotation 90° clockwise

3. Translation

A shape is slid to a new position without rotating or flipping it. We describe a translation using a column vector: the top number is movement left/right (+ = right), the bottom number is up/down (+ = up).

Column vector: (3/−2) means 3 right, 2 down

Translation by vector (4, −2)

4. Enlargement

A shape is made bigger or smaller by a scale factor, from a fixed point called the centre of enlargement. All lengths are multiplied by the scale factor; angles stay the same.

New length = original length × scale factor
Worked Example

A triangle has sides 3cm, 4cm, 5cm. It is enlarged with scale factor 2. What are the new side lengths?

3×2 = 6cm,   4×2 = 8cm,   5×2 = 10cm
⭐
Exam Tip GL frequently tests reflection across horizontal or vertical mirror lines on a grid. Count the squares from the shape to the mirror line, then count the same number the other side to find the image. Always check that the image is the same size as the original.

Coordinates

Coordinates tell us the position of a point on a grid. We always write them as (x, y) — the x value first (across), then the y value (up/down).

Remember: "along the corridor, then up (or down) the stairs" → x first, y second

The grid has four quadrants. In Quadrant 1, both x and y are positive. As you move left, x becomes negative. As you move down, y becomes negative.

Describing a Translation with Coordinates

If a point moves from A(2, 3) to A'(5, 1), the translation is:

Worked Example

Point A is at (2, 3). After a translation it is at (5, 1). Describe the translation.

x change: 5 − 2 = +3 (3 right)

y change: 1 − 3 = −2 (2 down)

Translation: 3 right and 2 down, or column vector (3, −2)
⭐
Exam Tip GL questions often ask you to reflect a shape across the x-axis or y-axis. Reflecting across the x-axis: y coordinate changes sign. Reflecting across the y-axis: x coordinate changes sign. E.g. (3, 4) reflected in the y-axis becomes (−3, 4).

Perimeter & Area

Perimeter = the total distance around the outside of a shape (add all the sides).
Area = the amount of space inside a shape (measured in cm², m², etc.).

ShapeArea formulaPerimeter
Rectanglelength × width2 × (l + w)
Squareside²4 × side
Triangle½ × base × heightsum of all 3 sides
Parallelogrambase × perpendicular height2 × (a + b)
Worked Example 1 — Rectangle

A rectangle is 8cm long and 5cm wide. Find its area and perimeter.

Area = 8 × 5 = 40 cm²  |  Perimeter = 2 × (8 + 5) = 2 × 13 = 26 cm
Worked Example 2 — Triangle

A triangle has a base of 10cm and a perpendicular height of 6cm.

Area = ½ × 10 × 6 = 30 cm²

Compound Shapes — The Split Strategy

A compound shape is made from two or more simpler shapes joined together. To find the area, split it into rectangles and/or triangles, find each area separately, then add them up.

Worked Example 3 — L-shaped compound shape

Split into Rectangle A (top) and Rectangle B (bottom-left):

Area A = 120 × 40 = 4800 cm²

Area B = 60 × 60 = 3600 cm²

Total area = 4800 + 3600 = 8400 cm²
⭐
Exam Tip For compound shapes, GL sometimes gives you the total area and a missing dimension — work backwards by subtraction. Also remember: area uses square units (cm²), perimeter just uses cm.

Circles

PartDescription
Radius (r)Distance from centre to edge
Diameter (d)Distance across the circle through the centre. d = 2r
CircumferenceDistance all the way around the outside
TangentA straight line that touches the circle at exactly one point
Circumference = π × d    (or π × 2r)
Area = π × r²
Use π ≈ 3.14 (or the π button on a calculator)
Worked Example 1 — Circumference

A circle has a diameter of 10cm. Find its circumference. (Use π = 3.14)

C = π × d = 3.14 × 10

Circumference = 31.4 cm
Worked Example 2 — Circumference from radius

A circle has a radius of 7cm. Find its circumference. (Use π = 3.14)

Diameter = 2 × 7 = 14cm

C = π × d = 3.14 × 14

Circumference = 43.96 cm
Worked Example 3 — Area

A circle has a radius of 5cm. Find its area. (Use π = 3.14)

A = π × r² = 3.14 × 5² = 3.14 × 25

Area = 78.5 cm²
Worked Example 4 — Find radius from diameter

A circle has a diameter of 18cm. Find its area.

Radius = 18 ÷ 2 = 9cm

A = π × r² = 3.14 × 81

Area = 254.34 cm²
⭐
Exam Tip The most common mistake: using the diameter instead of the radius in the area formula. Always halve the diameter first! Also, GL may give you the circumference and ask for the radius — rearrange: r = C ÷ (2π).

3D Shapes, Nets & Euler's Formula

3D shapes have faces (flat surfaces), edges (lines where faces meet), and vertices (corners where edges meet).

Euler's Formula: Faces + Vertices − Edges = 2   (works for all standard polyhedra)
ShapeFaces (F)Vertices (V)Edges (E)F+V−E
Cube68126+8−12 = 2 ✓
Cuboid68126+8−12 = 2 ✓
Square-based pyramid5585+5−8 = 2 ✓
Triangular prism5695+6−9 = 2 ✓
Cylinder3 (2 flat + 1 curved)02 (curved)— (not a polyhedron)
Cone2 (1 flat + 1 curved)1 (apex)1 (curved)— (not a polyhedron)
Sphere1 (curved)00— (not a polyhedron)

3D Shape Descriptions

Cube / Cuboid

  • 6 rectangular faces
  • Cube: all faces are squares
  • 8 vertices, 12 edges

Triangular Prism

  • 2 triangular faces (ends)
  • 3 rectangular faces (sides)
  • 6 vertices, 9 edges

Square-based Pyramid

  • 1 square base
  • 4 triangular faces
  • 5 vertices, 8 edges

Cylinder

  • 2 circular faces
  • 1 curved surface
  • No edges or vertices

Cone

  • 1 circular base
  • 1 curved surface
  • 1 vertex (apex)

Sphere

  • 1 curved surface
  • No flat faces, edges, or vertices

Nets of 3D Shapes

A net is what a 3D shape looks like when unfolded flat. Knowing nets is key for GL exam questions.

  • A cube net has 6 squares — many different arrangements are possible (there are 11 valid nets of a cube).
  • A triangular prism net has 2 triangles and 3 rectangles.
  • A square-based pyramid net has 1 square and 4 triangles.
  • A cylinder net has 2 circles and 1 rectangle (wrapped around).
Worked Example — Euler's Formula check

A triangular prism has 5 faces and 6 vertices. How many edges does it have?

F + V − E = 2  →  5 + 6 − E = 2  →  11 − E = 2

E = 11 − 2 = 9 edges
⭐
Exam Tip GL tests nets in two ways: (1) "Which of these diagrams is a valid net of a cube?" — mentally fold each option to check. (2) "A shape has 5 faces and 8 edges — how many vertices?" — use Euler's formula. Also know: a sphere, cone, and cylinder do NOT follow Euler's formula because they have curved surfaces.
