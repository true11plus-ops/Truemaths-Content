Section 1

Angle Types

Angles are measured in degrees (°). There are six types you need to know for GL.

Acute
Greater than 0° and less than 90°
Right Angle
Exactly 90°
Obtuse
Greater than 90°, less than 180°
Straight Line
Exactly 180°
Reflex
Greater than 180°, less than 360°
Full Turn
Exactly 360°
Memory trick: Acute < 90° · Right = 90° · Obtuse: 90°–180° · Straight = 180° · Reflex: 180°–360° · Full turn = 360°
Section 2

Angles on Straight Lines & Around a Point

Angles on a straight line

All angles on one side of a straight line add up to 180°.
Worked Example

Two angles on a straight line are 65° and x. Find x.

x = 180° − 65° = 115°

Angles around a point

All angles meeting at a single point add up to 360°.

Vertically opposite angles

When two straight lines cross, the angles opposite each other are equal.
Section 3

Parallel Lines & Angle Pairs

When a transversal (a line) crosses two parallel lines, three special angle pairs are formed. Parallel lines are shown with small arrows (→) on each line.

Z-angles (Alternate)
Alternate angles are between the parallel lines, on opposite sides of the transversal. They form a Z shape.
Rule: Alternate angles are equal.
F-angles (Corresponding)
Corresponding angles are in the same position at each intersection. They form an F shape.
Rule: Corresponding angles are equal.
C-angles (Co-interior)
Co-interior angles are both between the parallel lines on the same side. They form a C shape.
Rule: Co-interior angles add up to 180°.
Worked Example — Z-angles

Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. One alternate angle is 48°. Find the other alternate angle.

Alternate angles are equal → the other angle = 48°

Worked Example — C-angles

Two co-interior angles are 112° and x. Find x.

x = 180° − 112° = 68°

Section 4

Angles in Triangles

The interior angles of any triangle always add up to 180°.
Equilateral
All angles 60°. All sides equal.
Isosceles
Two equal sides → two equal base angles.
Right-angled
One angle = 90°. Other two sum to 90°.
Scalene
All sides and angles different.

Exterior Angle Rule (advanced shortcut)

The exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles.
Worked Example

A triangle has interior angles 40° and 65°. Find the exterior angle at the third vertex.

Exterior angle = 40° + 65° = 105°

Section 5

Quadrilaterals & Polygons

General formula: Interior angle sum of any polygon with n sides = (n − 2) × 180°
Shape Sides (n) Angle sum Each angle (regular) Special properties
Triangle 3 180° 60° —
Square / Rectangle 4 360° 90° each All right angles
Parallelogram 4 360° Varies Opposite angles equal; co-interior pairs = 180°
Rhombus 4 360° Varies Opposite angles equal; diagonals bisect at 90°
Trapezium 4 360° Varies One pair of parallel sides; co-interior pairs = 180°
Kite 4 360° Varies One pair of opposite angles equal; diagonals meet at 90°
Pentagon 5 540° 108° —
Hexagon 6 720° 120° —
Octagon 8 1080° 135° —
Exterior angles: The exterior angles of any polygon always add up to 360°, regardless of the number of sides.
Worked Example — Missing angle in a quadrilateral

A quadrilateral has angles 95°, 110°, 75°, and x. Find x.

Total = 360° (all quadrilaterals)

x = 360° − 95° − 110° − 75° = 80°

Worked Example — Regular polygon each angle

Find each interior angle of a regular hexagon.

Angle sum = (6 − 2) × 180° = 4 × 180° = 720°

Each angle = 720° ÷ 6 = 120°

Section 6

Angle Notation

GL exam diagrams always label angles using letters and specific notation. You must be able to read and write these.

∠ABC
Three-letter notation. The middle letter (B) is always the vertex — the point where the angle actually is. A and C are points on the two arms. Read as "angle ABC" or "the angle at B".
⌒
Arc symbol. A small arc drawn between two lines in a diagram marks the angle being referred to. Two arcs of the same style mean those two angles are equal.
⊓
Right-angle square. A small square in the corner of an angle always means exactly 90°. You do not need to measure it.
→→
Parallel line arrows. Single arrows (→) on two lines mean they are parallel. Double arrows (→→) mean a different pair of parallel lines in the same diagram.
| ||
Tick marks on sides. One tick mark on each of two sides means those sides are equal in length. Two ticks means another pair of equal sides.
Section 7

Protractor Skills

How to measure an angle

  1. Place the centre hole of the protractor exactly on the vertex (the point of the angle).
  2. Line up the baseline (0°) of the protractor with one arm of the angle.
  3. Read off the scale where the second arm crosses — use the scale starting from 0° on your baseline side.
  4. Check: does your answer match the type of angle you can see? (Acute should be < 90°, obtuse > 90°.)

How to draw an angle of a given size

  1. Draw a straight line and mark the vertex point.
  2. Place the protractor centre on the vertex, baseline on the line.
  3. Make a dot at the required degree mark on the correct scale.
  4. Remove the protractor and draw a line from the vertex through the dot.
  5. Label the angle.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Wrong scale: Protractors have two scales (inner and outer). Always start from 0° on the arm of your angle — don't just read the nearest number.
  • Centre not on vertex: Even 1 mm off gives a wrong reading. Take care to position it precisely.
  • Not checking the angle type: If you get 140° for what looks like an acute angle, you've read the wrong scale — subtract from 180°.
  • Reflex angles: Protractors only go to 180°. For a reflex angle, measure the smaller angle and subtract from 360°.
Quick check rule: Acute angle → answer must be < 90°. Obtuse → between 90° and 180°. If your reading doesn't fit the visible angle type, you've read the wrong scale.
Section 8

Compass Directions & Turns

Key angle facts

  • N to E (clockwise) = 90°
  • N to S (clockwise) = 180°
  • N to W (clockwise) = 270°
  • Full turn back to N = 360°
  • N to NE (clockwise) = 45°
Turns are measured clockwise from North. A right turn = 90°. A half turn = 180°.
Sample Problem

Tom faces North and turns clockwise to face South-East. How many degrees has he turned?

North → East = 90°. East → South-East = 45°. Total = 90° + 45° = 135°

Sample Problem 2

A ship faces West. It turns 90° anti-clockwise. What direction does it now face?

Turning anti-clockwise from West by 90° → South

Section 9

Exam Problem-Solving Strategy

GL angle questions always involve a labelled diagram. Follow this sequence every time:

  1. Read the whole question first. Identify what angle you're asked to find and label it clearly on the diagram (write a letter if there isn't one already).
  2. Identify the shapes. Is there a triangle? Parallel lines? A polygon? Each shape type has its own angle rules — know which applies.
  3. Spot the markings. Look for: right-angle squares (90°), tick marks (equal sides → isosceles triangle), arrows (parallel lines).
  4. Write down every rule you use. GL awards method marks. Always write, for example, "angles in a triangle = 180°" before you calculate.
  5. Work step-by-step. Find intermediate angles before the final answer. Label each one on the diagram as you go.
  6. Check your answer. Does the angle look right for its type? Do all the angles in the shape still add up correctly?
The golden rule: Never guess. Every angle in a GL question can be found using a rule. If you're stuck, write down all the facts you know about the diagram — the next step will usually become clear.
Section 10

Labelled Diagram Practice

Find the missing angle in each diagram. Show your reasoning.

Diagram Question 1 — Angles on a straight line

Three angles meet on a straight line. Two are 72° and 55°. Find x.

Show Answer
Angles on a straight line sum to 180°.
x = 180° − 72° − 55°
x = 53°
Diagram Question 2 — Isosceles triangle

An isosceles triangle has an apex angle of 40°. Find each base angle x.

Show Answer
Angles in a triangle sum to 180°.
The two base angles are equal (isosceles triangle).
2x = 180° − 40° = 140°
x = 70°
Diagram Question 3 — Parallel lines (Z-angle)

Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. The upper alternate angle is 63°. Find y.

Show Answer
Alternate angles (Z-angles) are equal when lines are parallel.
y = 63°
Section 11

Multi-Step Integrated Practice

These questions mix several rules together — just like the real GL exam.

Question 1

Two angles on a straight line are in the ratio 2 : 3. Find both angles.

Show Worked Solution
Angles on a straight line = 180°.
Ratio 2 : 3 → 5 parts total. One part = 180° ÷ 5 = 36°.
Angles are 2 × 36° = 72° and 3 × 36° = 108°.
Question 2

In a triangle, one angle is 90° and another is twice the third angle. Find all three angles.

Show Worked Solution
Let the smaller unknown angle = x. Then the other = 2x.
x + 2x + 90° = 180° (angle sum of triangle)
3x = 90° → x = 30°
The three angles are 90°, 60°, and 30°.
Question 3

A quadrilateral has angles 3x, 2x, x, and 90°. Find x and state the four angles.

Show Worked Solution
Interior angles of a quadrilateral sum to 360°.
3x + 2x + x + 90° = 360°
6x = 270° → x = 45°
The four angles are: 135°, 90°, 45°, and 90°.
Question 4

Two parallel lines are crossed by a transversal. One co-interior angle is (3x + 10)° and the other is (2x + 20)°. Find x and both angles.

Show Worked Solution
Co-interior (C) angles sum to 180°.
(3x + 10) + (2x + 20) = 180°
5x + 30 = 180° → 5x = 150° → x = 30°
Angles: 3(30) + 10 = 100° and 2(30) + 20 = 80°. Check: 100 + 80 = 180° ✓
Question 5

A straight line crosses two sides of a triangle, creating an exterior angle of 115°. One of the non-adjacent interior angles of the triangle is 48°. Find the other non-adjacent interior angle, and then find all three interior angles of the triangle.

Show Worked Solution
Exterior angle = sum of two non-adjacent interior angles.
48° + second angle = 115°
Second non-adjacent angle = 115° − 48° = 67°
Third (interior) angle = 180° − 115° = 65° (angles on a straight line)
Three interior angles: 48°, 67°, and 65°. Check: 48 + 67 + 65 = 180° ✓
