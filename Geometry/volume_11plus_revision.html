1The Concept of Volume

Volume answers the question: how much space does this object take up? We measure it in cubic units — cm³, m³, or mm³.

💡 The Unit Cube Model

Imagine a tiny cube that is exactly 1 cm × 1 cm × 1 cm. This cube has a volume of 1 cm³ (one cubic centimetre). If you said "the volume is 60 cm³", that means exactly 60 of those tiny cubes would fit perfectly inside the shape — no gaps, no overlaps.

This idea of counting unit cubes is what leads us to formulas. If a box is 5 cm long and you can fit 5 cubes in a row, and 3 cm wide meaning 3 rows, and 4 cm tall meaning 4 layers — the total count is 5 × 3 × 4 = 60. That's all a volume formula really is.

⚠️ Critical: Always label your answer with the correct unit

Volume is always in cubic units. Write cm³, not cm or cm². Examiners deduct marks for missing or wrong units.

2Volume of a Cuboid

A cuboid (also called a rectangular prism) has three different measurements: length (l), width (w), and height (h).

📐 Formula
Volume = length × width × height    →    V = l × w × h

Picture the cuboid with length going left–right, width going front–back, and height going up–down. The formula simply counts all unit cubes across all three directions.

✏️ Worked Example 1 — Whole Numbers
Given: A box with length = 6 cm, width = 4 cm, height = 5 cm
Step 1: Write the formula: V = l × w × h
Step 2: Substitute values: V = 6 × 4 × 5
Step 3: Work left to right: 6 × 4 = 24, then 24 × 5 = 120
✅ V = 120 cm³
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Decimal Dimensions
Given: A cuboid with length = 2.5 cm, width = 4 cm, height = 3 cm
Step 1: V = l × w × h
Step 2: V = 2.5 × 4 × 3
Step 3: 2.5 × 4 = 10.0, then 10 × 3 = 30
✅ V = 30 cm³

3Volume of a Cube

A cube is a special cuboid where all three dimensions are equal: length = width = height = side length s.

📐 Formula
V = s × s × s = s³

The notation s³ is read as "s cubed". This is where the term "cubic" in cubic centimetres comes from!

✏️ Worked Example
Given: A cube with side length = 4 cm
Step 1: V = s³ = 4³
Step 2: 4 × 4 = 16, then 16 × 4 = 64
✅ V = 64 cm³

4Volume of a Triangular Prism

A triangular prism has a triangular cross-section (its face) and a length (also called depth or height of the prism). Think of it like a Toblerone chocolate box.

🔑 Identifying the Parts

Triangle face: has a base (b) and a perpendicular height (h) — these are the two measurements of the triangle itself.

Length (l): how long the prism stretches — this is the "depth" of the box, going away from you.

Don't confuse the triangle's height with the prism's length. They are different measurements!

📐 Formula
V = ½ × base × height × length
V = ½ × b × h × l    (where b and h belong to the triangle, l is the prism's length)

Why ½? Because the area of a triangle is ½ × base × height. Volume = cross-sectional area × length.

✏️ Worked Example 1
Given: Triangle base = 6 cm, triangle height = 4 cm, prism length = 10 cm
Step 1: Area of triangle = ½ × 6 × 4 = 12 cm²
Step 2: Volume = triangle area × prism length = 12 × 10
✅ V = 120 cm³
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Decimal Dimensions
Given: Triangle base = 5 cm, triangle height = 3 cm, prism length = 8.5 cm
Step 1: Area of triangle = ½ × 5 × 3 = 7.5 cm²
Step 2: Volume = 7.5 × 8.5 = 63.75
✅ V = 63.75 cm³
⚠️ Most Common Mistake — Forgetting the ½

Students often compute b × h × l without the ½ and get double the correct answer. Always write ½ × base × height first to get the triangle area, then multiply by the prism's length.

5Unit Conversion — Fully Worked

All dimensions must be in the same unit before you calculate. GL papers regularly give you mixed units to catch students out.

📐 Conversion Rules
mm → cm: divide by 10    (e.g. 50 mm = 5 cm)
cm → m: divide by 100    (e.g. 250 cm = 2.5 m)
m → cm: multiply by 100    (e.g. 1.2 m = 120 cm)
✏️ Worked Example — Mixed Units
Given: length = 1.2 m, width = 40 cm, height = 50 mm
Step 1: Convert everything to cm (cm is most convenient here)
· 1.2 m × 100 = 120 cm
· 40 cm = 40 cm (already in cm)
· 50 mm ÷ 10 = 5 cm
Step 2: V = 120 × 40 × 5
Step 3: 120 × 40 = 4800, then 4800 × 5 = 24 000
✅ V = 24 000 cm³
⚠️ The Classic Trap

A student reads "length 1.2 m, width 40 cm, height 50 mm" and just multiplies: 1.2 × 40 × 50 = 2400. This is completely wrong because the units are different. Always convert first.

6Capacity and Liquid Volume

When a 3D shape holds liquid (a tank, a jug, a pool), we often need to express volume as a capacity in millilitres or litres.

📐 Key Relationships
1 cm³ = 1 ml
1 litre = 1000 ml = 1000 cm³
To convert cm³ → litres: divide by 1000
✏️ Worked Example — Litres in a Tank
Given: A tank is 30 cm × 20 cm × 15 cm. How many litres does it hold?
Step 1: Volume = 30 × 20 × 15 = 9000 cm³
Step 2: Since 1000 cm³ = 1 litre: 9000 ÷ 1000 = 9 litres
✅ The tank holds 9 litres.

7Filling and Container Problems

These questions give a container that is partially filled and ask how much more liquid is needed, or how high water will rise.

✏️ Worked Example — How Much More Water?
Given: A tank is 50 cm × 30 cm × 20 cm. It already contains 18 litres of water. How many more litres are needed to fill it completely?
Step 1: Total volume = 50 × 30 × 20 = 30 000 cm³
Step 2: Convert to litres: 30 000 ÷ 1000 = 30 litres
Step 3: Extra water needed = 30 − 18 = 12 litres
✅ 12 more litres are needed.

8Packing Small Cubes into a Larger Box

These questions ask how many small cubes can be packed into a larger cuboid. The critical technique is to divide each dimension separately, then multiply those results together.

📐 Method
Cubes along length = box length ÷ cube side
Cubes along width = box width ÷ cube side
Cubes along height = box height ÷ cube side
Total = (along length) × (along width) × (along height)
✏️ Worked Example
Given: How many 2 cm cubes fit into a box that is 10 cm × 8 cm × 6 cm?
Step 1: Along the length: 10 ÷ 2 = 5
Step 2: Along the width: 8 ÷ 2 = 4
Step 3: Along the height: 6 ÷ 2 = 3
Step 4: Total = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60
✅ 60 small cubes fit into the box.
⚠️ Common Wrong Method — Do NOT do this!

A student divides volumes: box volume = 480 cm³, small cube volume = 8 cm³, so 480 ÷ 8 = 60. This happens to work only when all dimensions divide exactly. If the cube size doesn't divide evenly into all three dimensions, volume-division gives the wrong answer. Always divide dimensions separately.

9Finding a Missing Dimension

When the total volume is known and two of the three dimensions are given, you can find the third by working backwards.

📐 Method
Step 1: Multiply the two known sides together
Step 2: Missing side = Volume ÷ (product of the two known sides)
✏️ Worked Example 1 — Whole Numbers
Given: Volume = 240 cm³, length = 8 cm, width = 5 cm. Find the height.
Step 1: Multiply known sides: 8 × 5 = 40
Step 2: Height = 240 ÷ 40 = 6
✅ Height = 6 cm
✏️ Worked Example 2 — Decimal Answer
Given: Volume = 90 cm³, length = 6 cm, width = 4 cm. Find the height.
Step 1: 6 × 4 = 24
Step 2: Height = 90 ÷ 24 = 3.75
✅ Height = 3.75 cm

10Compound / L-Shaped Solids

Some 3D shapes look like two cuboids joined together — often in an L-shape or a step shape. Split them into simpler cuboids, find each volume, then add.

📐 Method
Step 1: Split the shape into two simple cuboids
Step 2: Calculate the volume of each cuboid
Step 3: Add the two volumes together
✏️ Worked Example — L-Shape
Given: An L-shaped solid. Imagine a large cuboid 10 cm × 4 cm × 6 cm with a rectangular notch cut from one corner. The notch is 4 cm × 4 cm × 6 cm. Alternatively: think of Piece A (8 cm × 4 cm × 6 cm) joined to Piece B (4 cm × 2 cm × 6 cm).
Step 1: Volume of Piece A = 8 × 4 × 6 = 192 cm³
Step 2: Volume of Piece B = 4 × 2 × 6 = 48 cm³
Step 3: Total = 192 + 48 = 240 cm³
✅ Total Volume = 240 cm³

11Scaling and Proportion

What happens to volume when you change the size of a shape? This is a classic GL question type that tests deep understanding rather than formula recall.

📐 Scaling Rules
All dimensions × 2 → Volume × 2³ = Volume × 8
All dimensions × 3 → Volume × 3³ = Volume × 27
One dimension × 2 → Volume × 2 (only)
✏️ Worked Example — Doubling All Dimensions
Original: Cuboid 3 cm × 4 cm × 5 cm → V = 60 cm³
New dimensions: 6 cm × 8 cm × 10 cm
New volume: 6 × 8 × 10 = 480 cm³
Ratio: 480 ÷ 60 = 8
✅ Volume multiplied by 8 (= 2³) when all dimensions were doubled.
💡 Why does volume multiply by 8?

Doubling each of 3 dimensions gives 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 times the original volume. If only one dimension is doubled, the volume doubles. If two dimensions double, volume × 4. This is called the cube law of scaling.

12Surface Area vs Volume — Side by Side

These are two completely different measurements of the same shape. Students regularly confuse them under exam pressure.

FeatureSurface AreaVolume
What it measuresThe "skin" or outsideThe "filling" inside
Unitcm² (square)cm³ (cubic)
Visual analogyWrapping paper neededSand that fills it
✏️ Side-by-Side Calculation — Same Cuboid (5 cm × 3 cm × 4 cm)
SURFACE AREA
6 faces: top/bottom, front/back, left/right
Top + Bottom: 2 × (5 × 3) = 30 cm²
Front + Back: 2 × (5 × 4) = 40 cm²
Left + Right: 2 × (3 × 4) = 24 cm²
SA = 94 cm²
VOLUME
V = l × w × h
V = 5 × 3 × 4
V = 60
V = 60 cm³
⚠️ Exam Warning

If a question asks for volume, your answer should end in cm³. If it asks for surface area, it ends in cm². A wrong unit label tells the examiner you've confused these two concepts — even if your number is right.

13Decimal and Fractional Dimensions

GL papers regularly use non-integer dimensions. Don't panic — the method is exactly the same. Just convert fractions to decimals first.

📐 Useful Fraction–Decimal Conversions
¼ = 0.25    ½ = 0.5    ¾ = 0.75
⅓ ≈ 0.333    ⅔ ≈ 0.667    ⅕ = 0.2
✏️ Worked Example — Fraction to Decimal
Given: A cuboid with length = ¾ m, width = 60 cm, height = 40 cm. Find volume in cm³.
Step 1: Convert ¾ m to cm: ¾ × 100 = 75 cm
Step 2: V = 75 × 60 × 40
Step 3: 75 × 60 = 4500, then 4500 × 40 = 180 000
✅ V = 180 000 cm³

14Common Pitfalls — Consolidated Warning List
  • ⚠️
    Unit Mismatch: Never multiply dimensions that are in different units. Always convert everything to the same unit before calculating. 1.2 m × 40 cm × 50 mm must become 120 cm × 40 cm × 5 cm first.
  • ⚠️
    Surface Area vs Volume: Surface area uses cm² and measures the outside. Volume uses cm³ and measures the inside. They need completely different calculations — don't mix up the formulas or the units.
  • ⚠️
    Packing — Wrong Method: Don't divide volumes to find how many cubes fit. Divide each dimension separately, then multiply the three results. Dividing volumes only works by coincidence when dimensions divide exactly.
  • ⚠️
    Forgetting ½ in the Triangular Prism: The formula is ½ × base × height × length. Missing the ½ doubles your answer. Always find the triangle area first (½ × b × h), then multiply by the prism's length.
  • ⚠️
    ml and cm³ Confusion: 1 cm³ = 1 ml. Both are the same size. But 1 litre = 1000 ml = 1000 cm³. Convert to litres by dividing by 1000, not 100 or 10.
